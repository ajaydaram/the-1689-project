## Anchored in Eternity: Why the State After Death and the Resurrection Are the Believer's Sure Hope

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 31 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

---

### Introduction: The Last Enemy and the Final Victory

Chapter 31 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the State of Man after Death, and of the Resurrection of the Dead," is the penultimate chapter of the confession.¹ If earlier chapters have described the life of the believer in this world—liberty, worship, law, civil government, marriage, the church, and the ordinances—then Chapter 31 turns our gaze to the world to come.²

The chapter comprises three paragraphs. Paragraph 1 describes the **intermediate state**—what happens to the soul between death and the resurrection. Paragraph 2 affirms the **resurrection of the body**—that the same body that dies will be raised. Paragraph 3 declares the **twofold outcome** of the resurrection—the wicked to dishonor, the righteous to glory.³

The chapter stands against several errors: **soul sleep** (the denial that the soul is conscious after death), **purgatory** (the claim that a middle place exists for purification), **annihilationism** (the denial of eternal conscious punishment), and any denial of the bodily resurrection.⁴ As one expositor notes, "The intermediate state is not the final state, and the resurrection body is not a different body—it is the same body transformed."⁵

---

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the State After Death and the Resurrection

Chapter 31 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the intermediate state and the final resurrection.⁶

---

#### Paragraph 1 – The Intermediate State: Body and Soul at Death

The confession opens: **"The bodies of men after death return to dust, and see corruption; but their souls, which neither die nor sleep, having an immortal subsistence, immediately return to God who gave them. The souls of the righteous are then made perfect in holiness, and received into the highest heavens, where they behold the face of God in light and glory, waiting for the full redemption of their bodies. And the souls of the wicked are cast into hell, where they remain in torment and utter darkness, reserved for the judgment of the great day. Besides these two places, for souls separated from their bodies, the Scripture acknowledgeth none."**⁷

**1. "The bodies of men after death return to dust, and see corruption."** The confession affirms the reality of physical death and decay. Genesis 3:19 declares, "For you are dust, and to dust you shall return."⁸

**2. "Their souls... neither die nor sleep, having an immortal subsistence."** The confession denies the doctrine of soul sleep. The soul does not cease to exist or lapse into unconsciousness at death.⁹ Ecclesiastes 12:7 states, "The dust returns to the earth as it was, and the spirit returns to God who gave it."¹⁰

**3. "Immediately return to God who gave them."** There is no intermediate place of waiting or purgatory. The soul goes directly into the presence of God (for the righteous) or into judgment (for the wicked).¹¹

**4. "The souls of the righteous are then made perfect in holiness."** At death, believers are not merely released from the body; they are perfected in holiness. Hebrews 12:23 speaks of "the spirits of the righteous made perfect."¹²

**5. "Received into the highest heavens, where they behold the face of God in light and glory."** The confession cites 2 Corinthians 5:1, 6, 8: "We would rather be away from the body and at home with the Lord."¹³ Philippians 1:23 declares, "My desire is to depart and be with Christ, for that is far better."¹⁴

**6. "Waiting for the full redemption of their bodies."** The intermediate state is not the final state. Believers await the resurrection of their bodies.¹⁵

**7. "The souls of the wicked are cast into hell, where they remain in torment and utter darkness."** The confession affirms the conscious, eternal punishment of the wicked. Luke 16:23-24 describes the rich man "in Hades, being in torment."¹⁶

**8. "Besides these two places... the Scripture acknowledgeth none."** The confession explicitly rejects purgatory. There is no third place for purification after death.¹⁷

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Genesis 3:19; Acts 13:36; Ecclesiastes 12:7; Luke 23:43; 2 Corinthians 5:1,6,8; Philippians 1:23; Hebrews 12:23; Jude 6-7; Luke 16:23-24.¹⁸

#### Paragraph 2 – The Resurrection of the Body: The Same Body, Transformed

Paragraph 2 states: **"At the last day, such of the saints as are found alive shall not sleep, but be changed; and all the dead shall be raised up with the self-same bodies, and none other, although with different qualities, which shall be united again to their souls forever."**¹⁹

**1. "Such of the saints as are found alive shall not sleep, but be changed."** The confession affirms the rapture of living believers at the last day. 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 declares, "We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be changed, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye."²⁰ 1 Thessalonians 4:17 adds, "Then we who are alive, who are left, will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air."²¹

**2. "All the dead shall be raised up with the self-same bodies, and none other."** The confession affirms the bodily resurrection. Job 19:26-27 declares, "After my skin has been thus destroyed, yet in my flesh I shall see God, whom I shall see for myself, and my eyes shall behold, and not another."²²

**3. "With different qualities, which shall be united again to their souls forever."** The resurrection body is the same body in identity but transformed in quality. 1 Corinthians 15:42-43 explains, "What is sown is perishable; what is raised is imperishable. It is sown in dishonor; it is raised in glory. It is sown in weakness; it is raised in power."²³

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** 1 Corinthians 15:51-52; 1 Thessalonians 4:17; Job 19:26-27; 1 Corinthians 15:42-43.²⁴

#### Paragraph 3 – The Twofold Outcome: Resurrection to Life or to Judgment

The confession concludes: **"The bodies of the unjust shall, by the power of Christ, be raised to dishonor; the bodies of the just, by his Spirit, shall be raised to honor, and be made conformable to his glorious body."**²⁵

**1. "The bodies of the unjust shall, by the power of Christ, be raised to dishonor."** The confession affirms the resurrection of the wicked. Acts 24:15 declares, "There will be a resurrection of both the just and the unjust."²⁶ John 5:28-29 records Jesus' words: "All who are in the tombs will hear his voice and come out, those who have done good to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil to the resurrection of judgment."²⁷

**2. "The bodies of the just, by his Spirit, shall be raised to honor, and be made conformable to his glorious body."** The confession affirms that the righteous will be conformed to Christ's glorified body. Philippians 3:21 declares, "Who will transform our lowly body to be like his glorious body."²⁸

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Acts 24:15; John 5:28-29; Philippians 3:21.²⁹

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of the Doctrine of the Resurrection

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of the state after death and the resurrection.³⁰

**Pillar 1: The Soul Is Immortal and Conscious After Death.** Against soul sleep, the confession affirms that the soul continues in conscious existence after death.³¹

**Pillar 2: Immediate Transition to Heaven or Hell.** There is no purgatory and no second chance. The soul goes immediately to its eternal destination.³²

**Pillar 3: Perfection at Death for Believers.** Believers are made perfect in holiness at death, not through purgatorial suffering but through the grace of Christ.³³

**Pillar 4: The Same Body Is Raised.** The resurrection body is not a different body; it is the same body, transformed.³⁴

**Pillar 5: Transformed Qualities.** The resurrection body is imperishable, glorious, powerful, and spiritual (1 Corinthians 15:42-44).³⁵

**Pillar 6: Twofold Resurrection.** Both the just and the unjust are raised—the just to life, the unjust to judgment.³⁶

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Diminished Eschatology

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 31.

**Crisis 1: Soul Sleep.** Some traditions teach that the soul sleeps after death, unconscious until the resurrection. The confession affirms immediate consciousness.³⁷

**Crisis 2: Purgatory.** The Roman Catholic doctrine of purgatory claims that most believers must undergo purification after death. The confession declares that there is no third place.³⁸

**Crisis 3: Annihilationism.** Some deny eternal conscious punishment, teaching that the wicked will cease to exist. The confession affirms eternal torment for the wicked.³⁹

**Crisis 4: The Denial of the Bodily Resurrection.** Some (like the Sadducees of old) deny the resurrection of the body. The confession affirms it as an essential article of faith.⁴⁰

**Crisis 5: The Loss of Hope in the Face of Death.** Many believers grieve as those who have no hope. The confession grounds hope in the resurrection of the body.⁴¹

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Death and Resurrection

**Step 1: Take Comfort in the Intermediate State.** When a believing loved one dies, do not despair. Their soul is immediately with Christ, perfected in holiness.⁴²

**Step 2: Reject False Comforts.** Do not pray for the dead or seek their intercession. They are either with Christ or in torment; no third option exists.⁴³

**Step 3: Hope in the Resurrection of the Body.** Your body is not a prison to be escaped but a gift to be redeemed. The same body that dies will be raised imperishable.⁴⁴

**Step 4: Live in Light of the Twofold Resurrection.** The same power that raises the wicked to judgment raises the righteous to glory. Let this truth motivate evangelism and holiness.⁴⁵

**Step 5: Grieve with Hope.** When you mourn, do not mourn as those who have no hope. The resurrection is certain. You will see your loved ones again.⁴⁶

**Step 6: Purify Yourself.** John writes, "Everyone who thus hopes in him purifies himself as he is pure" (1 John 3:3). The hope of the resurrection is a motivation for holiness.⁴⁷

**Step 7: Long for the Last Day.** Do not be content with the intermediate state. Long for the resurrection—for the full redemption of your body, for the reunion of soul and body, for the new heavens and the new earth.⁴⁸

### Conclusion: Anchored in Eternity

Chapter 31 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's anchor in the face of death. The body returns to dust, but the soul returns to God. The righteous are made perfect and behold the face of Christ. The wicked are cast into torment. At the last day, the dead will be raised with the self-same bodies, transformed in quality. The just will rise to honor, conformed to Christ's glorious body. The unjust will rise to dishonor, to the resurrection of judgment.⁴⁹

This is the sure hope of every believer. Death is not the end. The grave is not the final destination. The same power that raised Christ from the dead will raise every believer to eternal life. To Him be the glory, now and forever.⁵⁰

**A Prayer from Chapter 31** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O God, the God of the living, not of the dead: We thank Thee that the souls of believers are immediately with Christ, perfected in holiness. We thank Thee that the same bodies that return to dust shall be raised imperishable, glorious, and powerful. We thank Thee that we shall see Thee face to face. Grant us to live in the light of the resurrection—to grieve with hope, to purify ourselves as He is pure, and to long for the last day when we shall be conformed to the glorious body of our Savior. Until that day, keep us anchored in this sure hope. For Christ's sake. Amen.*⁵¹
