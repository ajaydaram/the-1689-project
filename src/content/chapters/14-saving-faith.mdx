# The Hand That Receives All: Why Saving Faith Is the Instrument of Every Blessing in Christ

Chapter 14 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Saving Faith," occupies a strategic position within the confession's unfolding of the order of salvation.[^1] Having explained the covenant blessings that God works *in* His people—effectual calling (Chapter 10), justification (Chapter 11), adoption (Chapter 12), and sanctification (Chapter 13)—the confession now turns to the covenant graces, the human responses to those divine acts: saving faith and repentance (Chapters 15–18 follow).[^2] As Sam Waldron observes, "The Christian life is not a passive receiving of grace. It is an active, obedient pursuit of holiness. But even that active obedience is itself the fruit of grace."[^3]

The chapter is divided into three paragraphs, each building on the previous: Paragraph 1 describes the *source and means* of saving faith; Paragraph 2 describes the *acts of saving faith* in relation to Scripture and to Christ; and Paragraph 3 describes the *distinctiveness and growth* of saving faith.[^4]

The title is significant: not merely "faith" but *saving* faith. As one expositor notes, "This is a chapter on saving faith. The key word there is faith, but the key adjective is saving."[^5] The confession distinguishes saving faith from other kinds of faith—temporary faith (Matthew 13:20-21), demonic faith (James 2:19), and dead faith (James 2:17). The chapter is not about faith in general but about the faith that unites the soul to Christ and receives all the benefits of His redemption.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Saving Faith

Chapter 14 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, source, and operation of saving faith.[^6]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Source and Means of Saving Faith

The confession opens with a declaration that traces faith to its divine origin: **"The grace of faith, whereby the elect are enabled to believe to the saving of their souls, is the work of the Spirit of Christ in their hearts, and is ordinarily wrought by the ministry of the Word; by which also, and by the administration of baptism and the Lord's Supper, prayer, and other means appointed of God, it is increased and strengthened."**[^7]

**1. Saving Faith Is a Grace.** "The grace of faith."[^8] The first word of the chapter is crucial: faith is not a human achievement or a natural capacity; it is a *grace*—a supernatural gift from God. As the confession states in Chapter 11, faith "they have not of themselves, it is the gift of God" (Ephesians 2:8).[^9]

**2. The Recipients Are the Elect.** "Whereby the elect are enabled to believe to the saving of their souls."[^10] Not all who hear the gospel are given saving faith; it is given only to the elect (Acts 13:48). As Waldron explains, "The grace of faith is given only to the elect. This is not to say that the offer of the gospel is not sincere to all who hear it. But the saving reception of that offer is a gift reserved for the elect."[^11]

**3. The Author: The Spirit of Christ.** "Is the work of the Spirit of Christ in their hearts."[^12] Faith is not manufactured by human effort or reasoning; it is "the work of the Spirit." The confession cites 2 Corinthians 4:13: "Since we have the same spirit of faith according to what has been written, 'I believed, and so I spoke,' we also believe, and so we also speak."[^13]

**4. The Ordinary Means: The Ministry of the Word.** "Is ordinarily wrought by the ministry of the Word."[^14] While the Spirit is the ultimate author, He ordinarily works through the instrument of the preached and read Word. Romans 10:17 declares, "Faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ."[^15]

**5. The Growth of Faith.** "By which also, and by the administration of baptism and the Lord's Supper, prayer, and other means appointed of God, it is increased and strengthened."[^16] Faith is not static; it grows. The same Word that produces faith also nourishes it. The sacraments (baptism and the Lord's Supper) and prayer are means of grace that strengthen faith.[^17]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Ephesians 2:8; 2 Corinthians 4:13; Romans 10:14, 17; Luke 17:5; 1 Peter 2:2; Acts 20:32; 2 Peter 1:1; 1 Corinthians 1:21; Romans 1:16; Matthew 28:19-20; Acts 2:42; 1 Timothy 4:13.[^18]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Acts of Saving Faith: Scripture and Christ

Having described the source and means of faith, the confession now describes the *acts* of saving faith. Paragraph 2 is the longest and most detailed in the chapter: **"By this faith, a Christian believeth to be true whatsoever is revealed in the Word, for the authority of God himself; and also apprehendeth an excellency therein above all other writings and all things in the world, as it bears forth the glory of God in his attributes, the excellency of Christ in his nature and offices, and the power and fullness of the Holy Spirit in his workings and operations; and so is enabled to cast his soul upon the truth thus believed; and also acteth differently upon that which each particular passage thereof containeth; yielding obedience to the commands, trembling at the threatenings, and embracing the promises of God for this life and that which is to come. But the principal acts of saving faith have immediate relation to Christ, accepting, receiving, and resting upon him alone for justification, sanctification, and eternal life, by virtue of the covenant of grace."**[^19]

The reformers recognized that biblical faith has three essential aspects: *notitia* (knowledge), *assensus* (assent), and *fiducia* (trust). This paragraph beautifully captures all three.[^20]

**1. *Notitia* – Knowledge of the Truth.** "Believeth to be true whatsoever is revealed in the Word, for the authority of God himself."[^21] The first act of faith is to receive the entire Word of God as true because it is the Word of God. Notitia is the content of faith. As the Ligonier article explains, "There is a definite body of content to be believed, which is part and parcel of our religious activity."[^22]

**2. *Assensus* – Affirmation of the Truth.** "And also apprehendeth an excellency therein above all other writings and all things in the world."[^23] Assensus is the conviction that this content is true. It goes beyond mere intellectual acknowledgment to a recognition that Scripture has a unique and surpassing excellence—a moral and spiritual beauty that only the Spirit can enable one to see. The confession cites 1 Thessalonians 2:13: "When you received the word of God, which you heard from us, you accepted it not as the word of men but as what it really is, the word of God."[^24]

**3. The Ground of Scripture's Excellency.** "As it bears forth the glory of God in his attributes, the excellency of Christ in his nature and offices, and the power and fullness of the Holy Spirit in his workings and operations."[^25] The confession grounds Scripture's excellency in its Trinitarian content: it displays the Father's glory, the Son's excellency, and the Spirit's power.[^26]

**4. *Fiducia* – Personal Trust.** "And so is enabled to cast his soul upon the truth thus believed."[^27] Fiducia is the entrusting of oneself to the truth believed. As the Ligonier article states, "Fiducia refers to personal trust and reliance. Knowing and believing the content of the Christian faith is not enough, for even demons can do that. Faith is effectual only if one personally trusts in Christ alone for salvation."[^28]

**5. Differential Responses to Scripture.** "And also acteth differently upon that which each particular passage thereof containeth; yielding obedience to the commands, trembling at the threatenings, and embracing the promises of God for this life and that which is to come."[^29] Saving faith responds appropriately to different parts of Scripture—not by picking and choosing which to believe, but by obeying commands, heeding warnings, and embracing promises.[^30]

**6. The Principal Act: Faith in Christ.** "But the principal acts of saving faith have immediate relation to Christ, accepting, receiving, and resting upon him alone for justification, sanctification, and eternal life, by virtue of the covenant of grace."[^31] The highest act of faith is not believing *about* Christ but receiving *Christ Himself*—for justification (the forgiveness of sins and imputation of righteousness), for sanctification (the power to grow in holiness), and for eternal life (the hope of glory).[^32]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Distinctiveness and Growth of Saving Faith

The confession concludes with a paragraph that addresses two crucial pastoral questions: how is saving faith different from other kinds of faith, and can it grow? **"This faith, although it be different in degrees, and may be weak or strong, yet it is in the least degree of it, different in kind, or nature, (as is all other saving grace,) from the faith and common grace of temporary believers; and therefore, though it may be many times assailed and weakened, yet it gets the victory, growing up in many to the attainment of a full assurance through Christ, who is both the author and finisher of our faith."**[^34]

**1. Degrees of Faith.** "Although it be different in degrees, and may be weak or strong."[^35] The confession acknowledges that faith is not uniform in intensity. Some believers have stronger faith (Abraham, Romans 4), some have weaker faith (the father in Mark 9:24: "I believe; help my unbelief!").[^36]

**2. Difference in Kind, Not Just Degree.** "Yet it is in the least degree of it, different in kind, or nature, (as is all other saving grace,) from the faith and common grace of temporary believers."[^37] The weakest saving faith is of a different nature than the strongest temporary faith. As Sam Waldron explains, "This means that there is a faith and a common grace which do not save and which the unconverted may possess. God's kindness is shown even to the non-elect and sometimes bursts in them a kind of temporary faith. But saving faith is not different from false faith merely because it lasts longer. It is different in kind or nature from such faith."[^38]

**3. The Assaults on Faith.** "Though it may be many times assailed and weakened."[^39] Faith is under constant attack from the world, the flesh, and the devil. But the confession is realistic: genuine faith can be weakened—not destroyed.[^39]

**4. The Victory of Faith.** "Yet it gets the victory."[^40] 1 John 5:4 declares, "For everyone who has been born of God overcomes the world. And this is the victory that has overcome the world—our faith."[^29]

**5. The Growth to Full Assurance.** "Growing up in many to the attainment of a full assurance through Christ."[^41] Full assurance is not given to all believers immediately, but it is attainable through growth in grace. As Waldron states, "Our present degree of faith and our present degree of assurance of salvation is not all there is. More can and will unfold from true saving faith."[^42]

**6. Christ as Author and Finisher.** "Who is both the author and finisher of our faith."[^43] Hebrews 12:2 declares that Christ is the "founder and perfecter of our faith." He begins the work of faith in the soul, and He will complete it.

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Saving Faith

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of saving faith.[^45]

**Pillar 1: Faith Is a Gift, Not a Human Achievement.** The confession begins with "the grace of faith," not "the act of believing." Faith is not a work that earns salvation but a gift that receives it.[^46]

**Pillar 2: Saving Faith Has a Definite Object.** Faith must have content. The confession grounds faith in "whatsoever is revealed in the Word" and especially in Christ Himself. As one expositor explains, "Faith, by definition, has to have an object. You put your faith in something."[^47]

**Pillar 3: The Three Elements of Faith: *Notitia, Assensus, Fiducia*.** The confession captures the classic Reformed understanding: knowledge of the truth, affirmation of its truth, and personal trust in Christ.[^48]

**Pillar 4: Faith Is Distinct from Temporary Faith.** The weakest saving faith is qualitatively different from the strongest temporary faith. This guards against false assurance while encouraging weak believers.[^49]

**Pillar 5: Faith Grows through the Means of Grace.** The confession emphasizes that faith is not static but is "increased and strengthened" through the Word, sacraments, and prayer.[^50]

**Pillar 6: Christ Is Both the Author and Finisher of Faith.** The work of faith begins with Christ and ends with Christ. It is not a human project but a divine gift from start to finish.[^51]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted View of Faith

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 14.[^52]

**Crisis 1: Reducing Faith to Mental Assent.** Many professing Christians believe that saving faith is merely agreeing that certain doctrines are true. James 2:19 warns that "even the demons believe—and shudder!" The confession's emphasis on *fiducia* (personal trust) addresses this error.[^53]

**Crisis 2: "Decisional Regeneration" and the Denial of Faith as a Gift.** Many evangelicals treat faith as a human decision that precedes and enables regeneration. The confession's teaching that faith is "the work of the Spirit of Christ" grounds conversion in divine grace, not human will.[^54]

**Crisis 3: The Neglect of the Means of Grace.** Many believers neglect the Word, the sacraments, and prayer, yet wonder why their faith is weak. The confession's teaching that faith is "increased and strengthened" by these means is a direct exhortation.[^55]

**Crisis 4: Despair over Weak Faith.** Many weak believers wonder if they are truly saved because their faith seems small. The confession's teaching that the weakest saving faith is of the same nature as the strongest is a profound comfort.[^56]

**Crisis 5: False Assurance.** Many professing believers have a "temporary faith" that withers in times of trial. The confession's distinction between saving faith and the "common grace of temporary believers" calls for self-examination.[^57]

**Crisis 6: The Divorce between Faith and the Full Scope of Redemption.** Many believers think of faith only in relation to justification, neglecting its role in sanctification and eternal life. The confession's teaching that the principal acts of saving faith are "accepting, receiving, and resting upon him alone for justification, sanctification, and eternal life" corrects this narrow view.[^58]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Saving Faith

**Step 1: Recognize Faith as a Gift.** Do not boast in your faith as if it were your own achievement. Every time you believe, thank God for giving you the faith to believe.[^59]

**Step 2: Use the Means of Grace.** If you desire stronger faith, immerse yourself in the Word, receive the Lord's Supper regularly, and pray without ceasing. These are the channels through which the Spirit strengthens faith.[^60]

**Step 3: Direct Your Faith to Christ.** Do not rest in your faith—rest in Christ. The principal act of saving faith is not believing *about* Christ but receiving Christ Himself. Ask yourself daily: "Am I trusting in my own faith, or am I trusting in Christ?"[^61]

**Step 4: Comfort Weak Believers.** If your faith is weak, take heart. The weakest saving faith is of the same nature as the strongest. The father's cry, "I believe; help my unbelief!" (Mark 9:24) is a prayer that Christ honors.[^62]

**Step 5: Pursue Full Assurance.** Do not be content with a weak, wavering faith. Use the means of grace to grow in assurance. As the confession states, faith "grows up in many to the attainment of a full assurance through Christ."[^63]

**Step 6: Distinguish Saving Faith from Temporary Faith.** Examine yourself: does your faith produce obedience? Does it endure trials? Does it lead to love for Christ and His people? These are the marks of saving faith.[^64]

**Step 7: Live by Faith in All of Life.** Faith is not only for justification; it is for sanctification and eternal life. Trust Christ daily for power to obey, for strength to endure, and for the hope of glory.[^65]

### Conclusion: The Hand That Receives All

Chapter 14 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the grace that unites us to Christ. Saving faith is not a human work that earns salvation but a divine gift that receives it. It is not a blind leap in the dark but an intelligent, affectionate, and volitional embrace of the truth. It is not static but grows through the means of grace. And it is not fragile but victorious, for Christ is both the author and the finisher of our faith.[^66]

The confession calls believers to recognize that faith is a gift, to use the means of grace, to direct their faith to Christ alone, to comfort weak believers, to pursue full assurance, to examine themselves for the marks of saving faith, and to live by faith in all of life. The hand that receives all blessings from Christ is the empty hand of faith.[^67]

**A Prayer from Chapter 14** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father, the giver of every good gift: We thank Thee for the grace of saving faith. We confess that we did not manufacture it by our own efforts; it is the work of the Spirit of Christ in our hearts. We thank Thee for the Word, by which faith is ordinarily wrought and strengthened. We thank Thee for the sacraments and for prayer, by which faith is nourished. Grant us to believe whatsoever is revealed in Thy Word, for the authority of Thyself alone. Enable us to apprehend the excellency of Scripture above all other writings, as it bears forth Thy glory, the excellency of Thy Son, and the power of Thy Spirit. Teach us to yield obedience to Thy commands, to tremble at Thy threatenings, and to embrace Thy promises. But above all, draw our hearts to Christ, that we may accept, receive, and rest upon Him alone for justification, sanctification, and eternal life. Though our faith be weak, preserve it. Though it be assailed, let it get the victory. And grow it up in us to the attainment of a full assurance through Christ, who is the author and finisher of our faith. Amen.*[^68]
