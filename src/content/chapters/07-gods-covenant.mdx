# The Architecture of Redemption: Why the Covenant of Grace Is the Believer's Only Hope

**The Architecture of Redemption: Why the Covenant of Grace Is the Believer's Only Hope**

Chapter 7 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of God's Covenant," stands at the very heart of the confession's soteriology. If Chapter 3 declares God's eternal decree, Chapter 4 His creative act, Chapter 5 His providential governance, and Chapter 6 the tragedy of the fall, then Chapter 7 announces the *architecture of redemption*—the covenantal framework through which God saves sinners.[^1] As Charles Spurgeon once declared, "The doctrine of the divine covenant lies at the root of all theology."[^2]

The term *covenant* (Hebrew *berith*, Greek *diatheke*) speaks of a binding agreement, a sovereign arrangement of God by which man may be blessed.[^3] A covenant typically contains three elements: promises (the blessings to be received), conditions (the stipulations to be met), and penalties (the curses for breach).[^4] When God enters into covenant with His creatures, He condescends—stoops down—to define and secure a relationship that, by nature, He was not obligated to establish.[^5] As Sam Waldron explains, "The distance between God and the creature is so great, that although reasonable creatures do owe obedience to him as their Creator, yet they could never have attained the reward of life but by some voluntary condescension on God's part, which he hath been pleased to express by way of covenant."[^6]

The chapter's three paragraphs unfold like a theological triptych, moving from the historical necessity of covenant (paragraph 1) to the gracious provision of a new covenant (paragraph 2) and finally to the progressive revelation and eternal grounding of that covenant (paragraph 3). The arrangement is deliberate and pastoral: it first establishes the problem (the broken covenant of works), then announces the solution (the covenant of grace), and finally reveals the eternal source and historical unfolding of that solution.[^7]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Divine Covenants

Chapter 7 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on God's covenantal dealings with humanity. The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—anchored each assertion in the Word of God, refusing to speculate beyond what is written.[^8]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Necessity of Covenant: Why God Condescends to Relate by Covenant

The confession opens with a declaration of both the problem and its solution: <ConfessionBox>
**"The distance between God and the creature is so great, that although reasonable creatures do owe obedience unto him as their Creator, yet they could never have attained the reward of life but by some voluntary condescension on God's part, which he hath been pleased to express by way of covenant."**[^9]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Creator-Creature Distinction** The confession begins by affirming the absolute distinction between God and His creatures. This "distance" is not merely spatial but ontological and moral.[^10] God is infinite, eternal, immutable, holy; creatures are finite, temporal, mutable, dependent. Yet the confession immediately qualifies that this distance does not nullify obligation: reasonable creatures "do owe obedience unto him as their Creator."[^11] The proof text is Luke 17:10, where Jesus declares that when a servant has done all that was commanded, he must say, "We are unworthy servants; we have only done what was our duty."[^12]

**2. The Reward of Life** The confession identifies what Adam did not yet possess: "the reward of life."[^13] This is eternal life—the forever life, the state of confirmed righteousness and immortality that Adam could not have by nature. As Waldron explains, "Adam had a kind of life by creation, but he did not have the life of which the confession is speaking here. The creation account shows that Adam did not have eternal life. He could sin, and he could die."[^14] The proof text is Genesis 3:22, where God declares, "Behold, the man has become like one of Us, knowing good and evil; and now, he might stretch out his hand, and take also from the tree of life, and eat, and live forever."[^15]

**3. The Necessity of Divine Condescension** Even perfect, perpetual obedience could not have *earned* eternal life. There is no legal correlation between the obedience required of Adam and the blessing he would have gained; the blessing far surpassed the worth of the obedience required.[^16] Therefore, the attainment of eternal life required "some voluntary condescension on God's part."[^17] God was not obligated to promise life for obedience; He chose to. And He chose to express that arrangement "by way of covenant."[^18]

**4. The Covenant of Works Implied** Though the phrase "covenant of works" does not appear in this paragraph of the 1689 (it was moved to chapters 19 and 20), the concept is clearly affirmed.[^19] The confession assumes that Adam stood in a covenantal relationship with God: obedience would bring life; disobedience would bring death (Genesis 2:16-17).[^20] As the Founders Ministries article explains, "Paragraph 1 confesses a prelapsarian covenant of works. Though the phrase 'covenant of works' is located in 7:1 of the Westminster Confession, but is missing in 7:1 of the 1689, it is not because the 1689 denies that God's pre-fall arrangement with Adam was a covenant of works. This is made clear in 20:1, where the 1689 calls it 'the covenant of works.'"[^21]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Covenant of Grace: The Free Provision of Salvation

Having established the necessity of a covenant for the attainment of eternal life, and having implied the breaking of the covenant of works through Adam's fall, the confession now announces God's gracious response: <ConfessionBox>
**"Moreover, man having brought himself under the curse of the law by his fall, it pleased the Lord to make a covenant of grace, wherein he freely offereth unto sinners life and salvation by Jesus Christ, requiring of them faith in him, that they may be saved; and promising to give unto all those that are ordained unto eternal life, his Holy Spirit, to make them willing and able to believe."**[^23]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Curse of the Law** The paragraph begins with "Moreover"—a connective that assumes the previous discussion. Man has "brought himself under the curse of the law by his fall."[^24] The covenant of works was broken, and its penalty—death (spiritual, physical, eternal)—now hangs over Adam and all his posterity.[^25] Galatians 3:10 declares, "Cursed is everyone who does not abide by all things written in the Book of the Law, and do them."[^26]

**2. The Divine Initiative** "It pleased the Lord to make a covenant of grace."[^27] The verb "make" (Hebrew *karath*, "to cut") assumes God's absolute sovereignty. The covenant of grace is not negotiated between God and sinners; it is established unilaterally by God. As Sam Waldron explains, "In agreement with the Reformed, the Baptists set beside the covenant of works a covenant of grace by which God determined to restore fallen mankind to a place of favor in His sight and put them into possession of the eternal life they had forfeited by breaking the covenant of works."[^28]

**3. The Free Offer of Salvation** "Wherein he freely offereth unto sinners life and salvation by Jesus Christ."[^29] The confession affirms the *free offer of the gospel*—God offers, invites, and commands all who hear the gospel to receive salvation. This free offer is not limited to the elect.[^30] As Waldron notes, "How can God desire and command what He has not chosen to include in His eternal decree? That perhaps is a mystery beyond our comprehension. We only know that it is true. It is not, however, different from many other things. God by His will of precept commands many things that He does not will by His will of decree."[^31]

**4. The Condition of Faith** "Requiring of them faith in him, that they may be saved."[^32] The covenant of grace has a condition: faith in Jesus Christ. But this condition is radically different from the condition of the covenant of works. In the covenant of works, Adam had to *provide* his own perfect obedience; in the covenant of grace, sinners are only asked to *receive* the obedience already provided by another—Jesus Christ.[^33]

**5. The Sovereign Gift of the Spirit** "And promising to give unto all those that are ordained unto eternal life, his Holy Spirit, to make them willing and able to believe."[^34] The condition of faith is not left to human ability. Those whom God has "ordained unto eternal life" (Acts 13:48) receive the Holy Spirit, who sovereignly works in them the faith that they are required to exercise.[^35] This is the same teaching as LBCF 9.4: "When God converts a sinner, he brings him out of the state of sin and death into a state of grace by the Holy Spirit... removing his heart of stone and giving him a heart of flesh."[^36] The confession thus holds together free offer and sovereign grace, human responsibility and divine enabling.[^37]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Progressive Revelation and Eternal Grounding of the Covenant of Grace

The confession now addresses how the covenant of grace has been revealed across redemptive history and upon what foundation it rests. This paragraph is the distinctive contribution of the Particular Baptists, differing significantly from the Westminster and Savoy formulations.[^39] <ConfessionBox>
**"This covenant is revealed in the gospel; first of all to Adam in the promise of salvation by the seed of the woman, and afterwards by farther steps, until the full discovery thereof was completed in the New Testament; and it is founded in that eternal covenant transaction that was between the Father and the Son about the redemption of the elect; and it is alone by the grace of this covenant that all the posterity of fallen Adam that ever were saved did obtain life and blessed immortality, man being now utterly incapable of acceptance with God upon those terms on which Adam stood in his state of innocency."**[^40]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. Progressive Revelation: "By Farther Steps"** The confession affirms that the covenant of grace is **revealed** progressively in Scripture. It was revealed "first of all to Adam in the promise of salvation by the seed of the woman" (Genesis 3:15—the *protoevangelium*), "and afterwards by farther steps, until the full discovery thereof was completed in the New Testament."[^41] Instead of speaking of different "administrations" of one covenant of grace (the Westminster formulation), the Baptists speak of a gradually emerging revelation of the one covenant of grace beginning with Adam after the fall and completed in the New Testament.[^42] As Sam Waldron explains, "There is a much more biblical idea of an organic and progressive revelation of the gospel throughout redemptive history. Thus, the fact that there is one gospel through which men have always been saved is maintained. But at the same time, a place is found for the progression of redemptive history and the distinctiveness of the new covenant."[^43]

**2. The Eternal Foundation: The Covenant of Redemption** The covenant of grace in history is grounded in "that eternal covenant transaction that was between the Father and the Son about the redemption of the elect."[^44] This is the **covenant of redemption** (Latin: *pactum salutis*), made between the Father and the Son from all eternity.[^45] The Father promised the Son the salvation of His people on the condition that He become a man and die in their place. The Son, in beautiful filial love for His Father, willingly agreed to the role given to Him.[^46] The key expressions of this eternal covenant are found in two passages: Titus 1:2 speaks of "hope of eternal life, which God, who never lies, promised before the ages began"; and 2 Timothy 1:9 speaks of salvation given "in Christ Jesus from all eternity."[^47]

**3. The Sole Ground of Salvation** "It is alone by the grace of this covenant that all the posterity of fallen Adam that ever were saved did obtain life and blessed immortality."[^48] Every saved person—from Adam to Abraham to David to the thief on the cross to the believer today—is saved by the grace of this one covenant of grace. There are not two ways of salvation (law for Old Testament saints, grace for New).[^49] As Paul declares in Ephesians 2:12, the covenants (plural) of promise (singular) all point to the same gospel.[^50]

**4. The Inability of Man** The paragraph concludes with a sobering reminder: "man being now utterly incapable of acceptance with God upon those terms on which Adam stood in his state of innocency."[^51] After the fall, no human being can be accepted by God on the basis of personal, perfect, perpetual obedience (the terms of the covenant of works). Therefore, the covenant of grace is not merely *helpful*; it is *absolutely necessary*. If there were no covenant of grace, there would be no salvation.[^52]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Robust Covenant Theology

The confession's doctrine of the covenants rests on several interconnected theological pillars, each essential to maintaining biblical orthodoxy.[^54]

**Pillar 1: The Creator-Creature Distinction as the Ground of Covenant Necessity**

The confession begins with the distance between God and the creature. This is not a flaw or a deficiency; it is the necessary ontological order of reality. God is God; creatures are creatures. Yet because God is good, He condescends. As the Founders Ministries article explains, "God is not required to make any covenant at any point with anyone anywhere. Obedience to God's law is just honestly what is required as being a part of God's creation."[^55] The covenant, therefore, is not an obligation but a gift.[^56]

**Pillar 2: The Covenant of Works (Implied)**

Though not named in this chapter, the covenant of works is the presupposition of the entire discussion. Adam was given a righteous law, promised life upon obedience, and threatened with death upon disobedience (Genesis 2:16-17). When Adam fell, he broke this covenant and brought the curse upon himself and all his posterity. Yet the covenant of works was not abrogated; it continues to bind all of Adam's posterity, leaving every human being under its curse unless delivered by a second Adam.[^57]

**Pillar 3: The Covenant of Grace as the Sole Means of Postlapsarian Salvation**

The covenant of grace is not a second chance to keep the covenant of works. It is an entirely different arrangement: the terms are not "do and live" but "believe and live." And even the believing is a gift of God through the Holy Spirit. This is the distinctively Christian doctrine: salvation is by grace through faith, not by works.[^58]

**Pillar 4: The Covenant of Redemption as the Eternal Foundation**

The Particular Baptists explicitly grounded the covenant of grace in the intra-Trinitarian covenant of redemption. This was not a new invention; it was the logical outworking of biblical passages such as Titus 1:2, 2 Timothy 1:9, and John 17. The covenant of redemption ensures that the covenant of grace is not an afterthought or a contingency plan but the eternal purpose of the Triune God.[^59] As Sam Waldron writes, "The covenant grace in history is grounded in the covenant of redemption in eternity."[^60]

**Pillar 5: Progressive Revelation, Not Different Administrations**

The 1689's approach to the relationship between the Old and New Testaments differs from the Westminster formulation. Westminster speaks of one covenant of grace under two administrations (Old and New). The 1689 speaks of one covenant of grace progressively revealed "by farther steps" until its full discovery in the New Testament.[^61] This formulation better accounts for the distinctiveness of the new covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34) and avoids the implication that the Old Testament saints were saved by the New Testament covenant.[^62]

**Pillar 6: The Free Offer of the Gospel**

The confession explicitly teaches that God "freely offers unto sinners life and salvation by Jesus Christ, requiring of them faith in him, that they may be saved." This free offer is not limited to the elect; it is a genuine offer to all who hear the gospel.[^63] The confession then teaches that the Holy Spirit is given only to those ordained to eternal life, making them willing and able to believe.[^64] These two truths—free offer and sovereign grace—are not contradictory; they are complementary mysteries revealed in Scripture. As Matthew 22:14 declares, "Many are called, but few are chosen."[^65]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Diminished or Distorted View of Covenant

The confession's doctrine of the covenants is not a speculative framework; it is a pastoral necessity. Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by this chapter.[^66]

**Crisis 1: The Moralistic Assumption That Good Works Earn Salvation**

The confession's insistence that even perfect obedience cannot earn eternal life (paragraph 1) directly confronts the deep-seated human assumption that we can merit God's favor by our efforts. This is the default setting of the human heart. Pastorally, this manifests as:

- **Works-righteousness**: Believers who try to earn God's approval through prayer, Bible reading, or service.
- **Guilt and despair**: When performance inevitably falls short, the moralist despairs.
- **Judgmentalism**: Those who believe they have earned God's favor tend to judge others who do not meet their standards.

The cure is the covenant of grace: salvation is by grace through faith, not by works. Our acceptance before God rests entirely on the obedience of another.[^67]

**Crisis 2: The Antinomian Misunderstanding of Grace**

At the opposite extreme is antinomianism: the belief that grace nullifies the law. The confession's affirmation of the covenant of works (even in its broken state) and the ongoing validity of the moral law guards against this error.[^68] The covenant of grace does not abolish the law; it establishes it (Romans 3:31). Believers are not saved by the law, but they are sanctified by the law as the standard of holy living. The law is not a ladder to heaven; it is a mirror showing us our need for Christ.[^69]

**Crisis 3: The Dispensational Discontinuity**

Some dispensational systems teach that there are different ways of salvation in different dispensations (law for Israel, grace for the church). The confession's affirmation that there is one covenant of grace and one way of salvation for all the redeemed ("all the posterity of fallen Adam that ever were saved") directly confronts this error.[^70] Abraham was saved by grace through faith (Genesis 15:6; Romans 4:1-25). David was saved by grace through faith (Romans 4:6-8). The thief on the cross was saved by grace through faith (Luke 23:42-43). There is one gospel from Genesis to Revelation.[^71]

**Crisis 4: The Arminian Denial of Sovereign Grace**

The confession's affirmation that the Holy Spirit is given "to make them willing and able to believe" directly confronts the Arminian assumption that fallen human beings have the innate ability to believe without divine enabling. The unregenerate are "dead in trespasses and sins" (Ephesians 2:1), not merely sick or morally neutral. They need a divine gift of faith.[^72]

**Crisis 5: The Hyper-Calvinist Denial of the Free Offer**

At the opposite extreme from Arminianism is hyper-Calvinism, which denies that the gospel should be freely offered to all who hear it. The confession's explicit affirmation that God "freely offereth unto sinners life and salvation by Jesus Christ, requiring of them faith in him, that they may be saved" is a clear rejection of hyper-Calvinism.[^73] As Waldron writes, "We must preach to all that God sincerely calls them. He wants and commands them to repent and believe the gospel."[^74]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Covenants

The confession's doctrine of the covenants is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are seven practical steps derived from Chapter 7.[^75]

**Step 1: Understand That Even Perfect Obedience Cannot Merit Eternal Life**

The confession's first paragraph humbles the proud. If Adam, in a state of original righteousness, could not earn eternal life by his obedience, how much less can fallen sinners earn God's favor by their imperfect obedience? Let this truth drive you to the covenant of grace—the only ground of hope.[^76]

**Step 2: Rest in the Covenant of Grace, Not Your Performance**

When you sin (and you will sin every day), do not despair. Your acceptance before God does not depend on your performance but on Christ's. He has kept the covenant of works in your place. Therefore, there is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus (Romans 8:1).[^77]

**Step 3: Rejoice in the Free Offer of the Gospel**

If you are unsure whether you are elect, do not attempt to read your name in the Lamb's Book of Life. Instead, look to the free offer. God commands all who hear the gospel to repent and believe. That command includes you. Therefore, trust in Christ today. And if you trust in Christ, you have the witness of the Spirit that you are a child of God.[^78]

**Step 4: Depend on the Holy Spirit for the Ability to Believe**

If you struggle to believe, do not try to manufacture faith by your own effort. Faith is a gift. Pray: "Lord, I believe; help my unbelief!" (Mark 9:24). The same Spirit who raised Christ from the dead is at work in you to make you willing and able to believe.[^79]

**Step 5: Preach One Gospel from Genesis to Revelation**

The covenant of grace is revealed progressively, but it is one covenant. Therefore, when you read the Old Testament, do not read it as a book of law without grace. Read it as a book of gospel promises, types, and shadows, all pointing to Christ. Christ is the seed of the woman (Genesis 3:15), the Passover Lamb (Exodus 12), the serpent lifted up (Numbers 21:9), and the suffering servant (Isaiah 53).[^80]

**Step 6: Affirm Both the Free Offer and Sovereign Grace**

Do not pit God's sovereignty against His sincerity. The confession holds both truths together. Preach the gospel as if everything depended on you, but trust in God's election as if everything depended on Him. For in fact, everything depends on Him, and He uses your preaching as the means to save His elect.[^81]

**Step 7: Live as a Covenant Child**

If you are in Christ, you are not merely a forgiven sinner; you are a child of the covenant. The same covenant of grace that secures your justification also secures your sanctification and your final glorification. Therefore, live with hope. Your future is not uncertain; it is written in the eternal covenant transaction between the Father and the Son. You are safe in the hands of a covenant-keeping God.[^82]

### Conclusion: The Architecture of Redemption

Chapter 7 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth‑century federal theology. It is the believer's blueprint for understanding how God saves sinners. The doctrine of the covenants—the covenant of works broken by Adam, the covenant of grace provided by God, and the covenant of redemption executed by the Father and the Son from all eternity—is at once the deepest mystery and the sweetest comfort of the Christian faith.[^83]

It is a mystery because we cannot fully comprehend how God can be both sovereign in election and sincere in His free offer, nor can we penetrate the eternal counsels of the Trinity. Yet it is a comfort because it assures us that our salvation is not an afterthought, not a contingency plan, but the eternal purpose of the Triune God. The same God who purposed our salvation before the foundation of the world will keep us to the end.[^84]

The confession's concluding words are not despair but hope: "It is alone by the grace of this covenant that all the posterity of fallen Adam that ever were saved did obtain life and blessed immortality."[^85] There is no other way. But that way is sure. It is a covenant of grace, freely offered, sovereignly applied, and eternally secured.

May we, like the Puritan poet, rest in the covenant of grace:

> "The covenant of grace hath no condition to be performed by the sinner, but faith; and that faith is not the sinner's own act, but the gift of God. So that the covenant of grace is not a covenant of works at all; it is a covenant of pure grace."[^86]

**A Prayer from Chapter 7** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O most wise and gracious God: We confess that we have often tried to earn what can only be received. We have labored under the covenant of works, striving to merit Your favor by our imperfect obedience. We have despaired when we have failed. We have been proud when we have succeeded. Forgive us.*

*We thank Thee for the covenant of grace, established before the foundation of the world in the eternal transaction between the Father and the Son. We thank Thee for the free offer of salvation to all who hear the gospel. We thank Thee for the gift of the Holy Spirit, who makes us willing and able to believe.*

*Teach us to rest in the covenant of grace—not in our performance, but in the finished work of Christ. Teach us to preach the free offer of the gospel to all, trusting Thee for the sovereign application of its blessings. Teach us to live as covenant children, secure in the hands of a covenant-keeping God.*

*For Jesus Christ's sake. Amen.*[^87]
