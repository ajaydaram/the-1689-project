# The Freedom That Serves: Why Christian Liberty Is the Servant of Grace, Not a License for the Flesh

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 21 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Grace That Frees for Service

Chapter 21 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled “Of Christian Liberty and Liberty of Conscience,” stands as a crucial bridge between the objective doctrines of salvation and the practical life of the believer.[^1] Having explained the blessings that God works in His people—effectual calling (Chapter 10), justification (Chapter 11), adoption (Chapter 12), sanctification (Chapter 13)—and the graces they exercise—saving faith (Chapter 14), repentance (Chapter 15), and good works (Chapter 16)—the confession now turns to the *nature of the freedom* that believers possess in Christ.[^2]

The chapter comprises three paragraphs, each addressing a distinct aspect of Christian liberty. Paragraph 1 defines the *substance* of Christian liberty—both its negative aspect (freedom from guilt, condemnation, the curse of the law, and the dominion of sin) and its positive aspect (freedom to access God and obey Him with filial love). Paragraph 2 declares the *liberty of conscience*—that God alone is Lord of the conscience, and that human commands contrary to or beyond Scripture cannot bind it. Paragraph 3 warns against the *abuse of liberty*—that it must not be used as a pretext for sin, for its true purpose is holy service to God.[^3]

The chapter stands against two errors: **legalism** (which binds the conscience with human traditions) and **antinomianism** (which uses liberty as a license for sin).[^4] As one expositor notes, “Christian liberty is not the right to do what you want; it is the power to do what you ought.”[^5]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Christian Liberty

Chapter 21 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on the nature, extent, and limits of Christian freedom.[^6]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Substance of Christian Liberty

The confession opens with a declaration of the twofold nature of Christian liberty: **“The liberty which Christ hath purchased for believers under the gospel consists in their deliverance from the guilt of sin, the condemning wrath of God, the rigor and curse of the law, and in their being delivered from this present evil world, bondage to Satan, and dominion of sin; from the evil of afflictions, the sting of death, the victory of the grave, and everlasting damnation. As also in their free access to God, and their yielding obedience unto him, not out of slavish fear, but a child-like love and willing mind.”**[^7]

**1. The Source of Liberty: Purchased by Christ.** “The liberty which Christ hath purchased.”[^8] Christian liberty is not a natural right or a political achievement; it is a purchased gift. Galatians 3:13 declares, “Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us.”[^9] As Sam Waldron notes, “The word ‘purchased’ grounds liberty in the objective work of Christ, not in human striving or merit.”[^10]

**2. The Negative Aspect: Freedom From.** The confession lists the specific things from which believers are delivered:

- **From the guilt of sin and the condemning wrath of God** (Romans 8:1 – “There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus”).[^11]
- **From the rigor and curse of the law** (Galatians 3:13 – “Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law”).[^12]
- **From this present evil world** (Galatians 1:4 – Christ “gave himself for our sins to deliver us from the present evil age”).[^13]
- **From bondage to Satan** (Acts 26:18 – “to turn them from darkness to the power of Satan to God”).[^14]
- **From the dominion of sin** (Romans 6:14 – “Sin will have no dominion over you”).[^15]
- **From the evil of afflictions, the sting of death, the victory of the grave, and everlasting damnation** (Romans 8:28; 1 Corinthians 15:54-57; 2 Thessalonians 1:10).[^16]

**3. The Positive Aspect: Freedom To.** “As also in their free access to God, and their yielding obedience unto him, not out of slavish fear, but a child-like love and willing mind.”[^17] Believers have bold access to God (Ephesians 2:18; 3:12; Hebrews 4:16). Their obedience is transformed from slavish compulsion to filial delight. Romans 8:15 declares, “You have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, ‘Abba! Father!’”[^18] Luke 1:73-75 speaks of being delivered “that we, being delivered from the hand of our enemies, might serve him without fear, in holiness and righteousness before him all our days.”[^19]

**4. The Liberty of All True Believers.** “All true believers, as such, enjoy this liberty; it is common also to believers under the Old Testament, and was, for the substance of it, held forth in the promises, types, and sacrifices of the law.”[^20] The confession affirms the unity of the covenant of grace. Old Testament believers enjoyed the same essential liberty: they were justified by faith, had access to God, and were freed from the curse. Galatians 3:9,14 declares that believers are “blessed along with Abraham” and that the blessing of Abraham comes to the Gentiles in Christ.[^21]

**5. The Distinctives of the New Testament.** “Yet believers under the New Testament have a greater liberty, in that they are freed from the yoke of the ceremonial law, and have greater boldness of access to the throne of grace, and are endowed with more abundant communications of the free Spirit of God than believers under the law did ordinarily partake of.”[^22] The confession identifies three New Testament distinctives:

- **Freedom from the ceremonial law** (Galatians 5:1 – “For freedom Christ has set us free; stand firm therefore, and do not submit again to a yoke of slavery”).[^23]
- **Greater boldness of access** (Hebrews 10:19-21 – “we have confidence to enter the holy places by the blood of Jesus”).[^24]
- **More abundant communications of the Spirit** (John 7:38-39 – “Out of his heart will flow rivers of living water… this he said about the Spirit, whom those who believed in him were to receive”).[^25]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Liberty of Conscience

Paragraph 2 addresses the crucial matter of conscience and human authority: **“God alone is Lord of the conscience, and has left it free from the doctrines and commandments of men which are in any thing contrary to his word, or not contained in it. So that to believe such doctrines, or to obey such commands out of conscience, is to betray true liberty of conscience; and the requiring of an implicit faith, an absolute and blind obedience, is to destroy liberty of conscience and reason also.”**[^27]

**1. The Sole Lordship of God Over the Conscience.** “God alone is Lord of the conscience.”[^28] This is the Reformation’s thunderous affirmation. No pope, priest, pastor, parent, or magistrate has absolute authority over the conscience. James 4:12 declares, “There is only one lawgiver and judge, he who is able to save and to destroy.”[^29] Romans 14:4 asks, “Who are you to pass judgment on the servant of another? It is before his own master that he stands or falls.”[^30]

**2. The Freedom from Human Doctrines and Commandments.** “Has left it free from the doctrines and commandments of men which are in any thing contrary to his word, or not contained in it.”[^31] The confession protects the conscience from two kinds of human impositions: those *contrary* to Scripture (which command what God forbids or forbid what God commands) and those *not contained* in Scripture (which add requirements beyond what God has commanded).[^32] Matthew 15:9 records Jesus’ rebuke: “In vain do they worship me, teaching as doctrines the commandments of men.”[^33] Colossians 2:20-23 warns against human precepts: “Why, as if you were still alive in the world, do you submit to regulations—‘Do not handle, Do not taste, Do not touch’… these have indeed an appearance of wisdom in promoting self-made religion.”[^34]

**3. The Betrayal of Liberty: Believing or Obeying Such Doctrines.** “To believe such doctrines, or to obey such commands out of conscience, is to betray true liberty of conscience.”[^35] When believers treat human traditions as divine commands, they surrender their God-given liberty.[^36]

**4. The Destruction of Liberty: Implicit Faith and Blind Obedience.** “The requiring of an implicit faith, an absolute and blind obedience, is to destroy liberty of conscience and reason also.”[^37] “Implicit faith” refers to believing something without evidence or simply because an authority says it.[^38] 1 Corinthians 3:5 and 2 Corinthians 1:24 clarify that ministers are servants, not lords, over the faith of believers.[^39]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Limits and Purpose of Liberty

Paragraph 3 warns against the abuse of liberty and declares its true purpose: **“They who, upon pretence of Christian liberty, do practice any sin, or cherish any lust, do thereby destroy the end of Christian liberty, which is, that being delivered out of the hands of our enemies, we might serve the Lord without fear, in holiness and righteousness before him, all the days of our life.”**[^41]

**1. The Abuse of Liberty: A Pretext for Sin.** “They who, upon pretence of Christian liberty, do practice any sin, or cherish any lust.”[^42] Galatians 5:13 warns, “For you were called to freedom, brothers. Only do not use your freedom as an opportunity for the flesh.”[^13] Romans 6:1-2 asks, “Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means!”[^43]

**2. The Consequence: Destroying the End of Liberty.** “Do thereby destroy the end of Christian liberty.”[^44] Those who abuse liberty do not merely misuse it; they destroy its very purpose. 2 Peter 2:18-21 warns of those who “promise them freedom, but they themselves are slaves of corruption.”[^45]

**3. The True Purpose of Liberty: Holy Service.** The confession cites Luke 1:73-75: “That we, being delivered out of the hand of our enemies, might serve him without fear, in holiness and righteousness before him, all the days of our life.”[^46] Christian liberty is not freedom *from* obedience but freedom *for* obedience—not freedom to sin but freedom to serve.[^47]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Romans 6:1-2; Galatians 5:13; 2 Peter 2:18,21.[^48]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Christian Liberty

Several theological pillars support the confession’s doctrine of Christian liberty.

**Pillar 1: Liberty Is Purchased, Not Earned.** Christian liberty is grounded in the objective work of Christ, not in human effort or natural right.[^49]

**Pillar 2: Liberty Is Both From and To.** The confession carefully balances the negative (freedom from sin, law, death, and Satan) and the positive (freedom to access God and obey Him with filial love).[^50]

**Pillar 3: The Substance of Liberty Is One Across Testaments.** Old and New Testament believers enjoy the same essential liberty—justification, access to God, and freedom from the curse—though the New Testament administration is more glorious and extensive.[^51]

**Pillar 4: God Alone Is Lord of the Conscience.** No human authority—ecclesiastical or civil—has absolute jurisdiction over the conscience. This is the Reformation principle of *sola Scriptura* applied to the inner man.[^52]

**Pillar 5: Implicit Faith Destroys Liberty.** Requiring belief without evidence or on mere human authority is an assault on both conscience and reason.[^53]

**Pillar 6: The Purpose of Liberty Is Holy Service.** Christian liberty is not a license to sin but a liberation for joyful obedience. The end of liberty is not self-indulgence but worship and service.[^54]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Liberty

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 21.

**Crisis 1: Legalism and Human Traditions.** Many churches bind consciences with human traditions not commanded in Scripture. The confession’s declaration that the conscience is “free from the doctrines and commandments of men which are… not contained in” Scripture directly confronts legalism.[^55]

**Crisis 2: Antinomian License.** Others use Christian liberty as a pretext for sin, claiming that grace nullifies moral obligations. The confession’s third paragraph firmly rejects this abuse.[^56]

**Crisis 3: Authoritarianism in Church Leadership.** Some pastors and elders demand implicit obedience, treating their opinions as if they carried divine authority. The confession insists that ministers are servants, not lords, over the conscience.[^57]

**Crisis 4: Civil Overreach.** The state has no authority to command what God forbids or forbid what God commands. The confession’s principle that “God alone is Lord of the conscience” has profound implications for religious liberty.[^58]

**Crisis 5: The Loss of the Positive Purpose of Liberty.** Many believers understand liberty only negatively (freedom from) but neglect liberty’s positive purpose (freedom for holiness). The confession’s citation of Luke 1:73-75 restores the biblical balance.[^59]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Christian Liberty

**Step 1: Rest in the Liberty Christ Purchased.** You are no longer under the curse of the law, the dominion of sin, or the fear of death. Let this truth free you from anxiety and self-condemnation.[^60]

**Step 2: Distinguish Scripture from Human Tradition.** When a church or leader commands something, ask: “Is this commanded in Scripture?” If not, it cannot bind your conscience.[^61]

**Step 3: Reject Implicit Faith.** Do not believe something simply because an authority says it. Test all teachings by the Word of God.[^62]

**Step 4: Do Not Use Liberty as a Pretext for Sin.** Examine your life: are you using your freedom to indulge the flesh? If so, you are destroying the very purpose of liberty.[^63]

**Step 5: Use Liberty for Holy Service.** Christian freedom is not freedom *from* obedience but freedom *for* obedience. Serve the Lord without fear, in holiness and righteousness.[^64]

**Step 6: Respect the Consciences of Others.** While you are free in Christ, do not despise those whose consciences are weaker. Love may require limiting the exercise of liberty (1 Corinthians 8:9-13).[^65]

**Step 7: Rejoice in the Greater Liberty of the New Covenant.** You have been freed from the ceremonial law, have bold access to God, and have received the Spirit abundantly. Let these privileges lead to greater joy and more fervent worship.[^66]

### Conclusion: The Freedom That Serves

Chapter 21 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer’s charter of liberty. Christian liberty is purchased by Christ, not earned by works. It is freedom *from* the guilt, curse, and dominion of sin. It is freedom *to* access God and obey Him with filial love. It is freedom *of conscience* from human doctrines not contained in Scripture. It is freedom that must not be abused as a pretext for sin, for its true purpose is holy service.[^67]

The same liberty that liberates from the law’s curse also liberates for joyful obedience. The same Spirit who gives freedom also produces holiness. And the same Christ who purchased liberty will one day bring His people to the full enjoyment of liberty in glory.[^68]

**A Prayer from Chapter 21** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O God, who alone art Lord of the conscience: We thank Thee for the liberty which Christ hath purchased for us. We thank Thee that we are delivered from the guilt of sin, the condemning wrath of God, the curse of the law, the dominion of sin, the sting of death, and the victory of the grave. We thank Thee that we have free access to Thee, and may yield obedience not out of slavish fear but out of child-like love.*

*Grant us to use our liberty as servants, not as rebels. Keep us from the legalism that binds where Thou hast loosed, and from the license that looses where Thou hast bound. Let our consciences be captive to Thy Word alone. And may we, being delivered out of the hand of our enemies, serve Thee without fear, in holiness and righteousness before Thee, all the days of our life. For Christ’s sake. Amen.*[^69]
