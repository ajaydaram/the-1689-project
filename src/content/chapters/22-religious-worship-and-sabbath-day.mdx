# The Day That Is Holy to the Lord: Why the Regulative Principle of Worship and the Christian Sabbath Are the Believer's Delight

### Introduction: The Worship Question That Shapes the Church

Chapter 22 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled “Of Religious Worship and the Sabbath Day,” stands as one of the most detailed and pastorally practical chapters in the entire confession.[^1] If Chapter 21 declares the believer’s liberty of conscience, Chapter 22 now addresses the **proper exercise** of that liberty in the worship of God.[^2]

The chapter comprises **eight paragraphs**, which can be grouped under four major headings:[^3]

- **Paragraphs 1–2**: The foundation, nature, and exclusive object of religious worship
- **Paragraphs 3–5**: The constitutive elements and proper manner of worship
- **Paragraph 6**: The location of worship under the New Covenant
- **Paragraphs 7–8**: The Lord’s Day as the Christian Sabbath

The chapter is a direct application of the **Regulative Principle of Worship**: that God is to be worshipped only as He has commanded in His Word, and that whatever is not commanded is forbidden.[^4] As one expositor notes, “Chapter 22 is dealing with how we ought to worship God, dealing with that and the Sabbath day.”[^5]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Religious Worship and the Sabbath

Chapter 22 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on the nature, elements, object, and time of worship.[^6]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Light of Nature and the Necessity of Special Revelation

The confession opens: **“The light of nature shows that there is a God, who has lordship and sovereignty over all; is just, good, and does good to all; and is therefore to be feared, loved, praised, called upon, trusted in, and served, with all the heart and all the soul, and with all the might. But the acceptable way of worshiping the true God is instituted by himself, and so limited by his own revealed will, that he may not be worshiped according to the imagination and devices of men, or the suggestions of Satan, under any visible representation, or any other way not prescribed in the Holy Scripture.”**[^7]

**1. The Light of Nature.** “The light of nature shows that there is a God.”[^8] Romans 1:19-20 declares that God’s eternal power and divine nature have been clearly perceived ever since the creation of the world. Acts 14:15-17 testifies that God did not leave Himself without witness. Natural revelation establishes the **obligation** to worship. As one exposition notes, “The light of nature commands us that we need to worship, that we should worship, our obligation to worship.”[^9]

**2. The Limits of Natural Revelation.** The light of nature tells us **that** we must worship but not **how** to worship acceptably. Matthew 15:9 records Jesus’ rebuke: “In vain do they worship me, teaching as doctrines the commandments of men.”[^10]

**3. The Regulative Principle Stated.** “The acceptable way of worshiping the true God is instituted by himself, and so limited by his own revealed will.”[^11] Deuteronomy 12:32 commands, “Everything that I command you, you shall be careful to do. You shall not add to it or take from it.”[^12]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Romans 1:19-20; Acts 14:15-17; Psalm 19:1-4; Matthew 15:9; Deuteronomy 12:32; Exodus 20:4-6.[^13]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Exclusive Object and Necessary Mediator

Paragraph 2 states: **“Religious worship is to be given to God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, and to him alone; not to angels, saints, or any other creatures; and since the fall, not without a mediator, nor in the mediation of any other but Christ alone.”**[^14]

**1. The Exclusive Object of Worship.** “To God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, and to him alone.” Matthew 4:10 declares, “You shall worship the Lord your God and him only shall you serve.”[^15] Revelation 19:10 warns, “You must not do that! I am a fellow servant with you… Worship God.”[^16]

**2. The Trinitarian Ground.** Worship is given to all three persons equally because they share one undivided essence. This guards against any tendency to diminish the deity of the Son or the Spirit.[^17]

**3. The Necessary Mediator.** “And since the fall, not without a mediator.” All worship must be offered through the mediation of Jesus Christ. John 14:6 declares, “I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me.”[^18]

**4. The Exclusive Mediator.** “Nor in the mediation of any other but Christ alone.” The confession explicitly rejects the intercession of Mary, the saints, or angels as mediators of worship. 1 Timothy 2:5 declares, “There is one God, and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus.”[^19]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Matthew 4:10; Revelation 19:10; John 14:6; 1 Timothy 2:5.[^20]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Elements of Worship: Prayer

Paragraph 3 begins the enumeration of the specific acts of worship: **“Prayer with thanksgiving being one special part of religious worship, is by God required of all men; and that it may be accepted, it is to be made in the name of the Son, by the help of the Spirit, according to his will; with understanding, reverence, humility, fervency, faith, love, and perseverance; and when with others, in a known tongue.”**[^21]

**1. Prayer with Thanksgiving: A Required Element.** “Prayer with thanksgiving being one special part of religious worship.” Philippians 4:6 commands, “Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God.”[^22]

**2. Prayer in the Name of the Son.** John 14:13-14 declares, “Whatever you ask in my name, this I will do.”[^23]

**3. Prayer by the Help of the Spirit.** Romans 8:26 affirms, “The Spirit helps us in our weakness. For we do not know what to pray for as we ought, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us.”[^24]

**4. Prayer According to His Will.** 1 John 5:14 states, “This is the confidence that we have toward him, that if we ask anything according to his will he hears us.”[^25]

**5. The Posture of Prayer.** The confession lists the character qualities required in prayer: “with understanding, reverence, humility, fervency, faith, love, and perseverance.”[^26]

**6. Public Prayer in a Known Tongue.** “And when with others, in a known tongue.” 1 Corinthians 14:15-16 instructs that prayer in public worship should be intelligible.[^27]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Philippians 4:6; John 14:13-14; Romans 8:26; 1 John 5:14; 1 Timothy 2:8; 1 Corinthians 14:15-16.[^28]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Elements of Worship: Word and Preaching

Paragraph 4 states: **“The reading of the Scriptures, preaching and hearing the Word of God, teaching and admonishing one another in psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, singing with grace in our hearts to the Lord, as also the administration of baptism and the Lord’s supper, are all parts of religious worship of God, to be performed in obedience to him, with understanding, faith, reverence, and godly fear.”**[^29]

**1. Reading of the Scriptures.** 1 Timothy 4:13 exhorts, “Until I come, devote yourself to the public reading of Scripture.”[^30]

**2. Preaching and Hearing the Word.** 2 Timothy 4:2 commands, “Preach the word; be ready in season and out of season.”[^31]

**3. Singing of Psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs.** The confession cites Colossians 3:16: “Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly, teaching and admonishing one another in all wisdom, singing psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, with thankfulness in your hearts to God.”[^32] Ephesians 5:19 adds, “Addressing one another in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing and making melody to the Lord with your heart.”[^33]

**4. The Sacraments as Worship.** “As also the administration of baptism and the Lord’s supper.”[^34]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** 1 Timothy 4:13; 2 Timothy 4:2; Colossians 3:16; Ephesians 5:19; Matthew 28:19-20; 1 Corinthians 11:23-29.[^35]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Circumstances of Worship: Order and Decency

Paragraph 5 states: **“The circumstances of religious worship, and the whole order and decent management thereof, are to be ordered and managed according to the light of nature and Christian prudence, following the general rules of the Word, which are always to be observed.”**[^36]

**1. Distinction Between Elements and Circumstances.** The confession distinguishes between the **elements** of worship (commanded in Scripture) and the **circumstances** (time, place, order, which the Word leaves to Christian wisdom).[^37]

**2. The Light of Nature and Christian Prudence.** 1 Corinthians 14:40 commands, “Let all things be done decently and in order.”[^38]

**3. The General Rules of the Word.** Even circumstances must be guided by general biblical principles: edification, order, and decency.[^39]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** 1 Corinthians 14:40; 1 Corinthians 14:26, 33.[^40]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Location of Worship

Paragraph 6 addresses the New Covenant freedom regarding place: **“Neither prayer, nor any other part of religious worship, is now, under the gospel, tied unto, or made more acceptable by any place in which it is performed, or towards which it is directed; but God is to be worshipped everywhere in spirit and truth; as in private families daily, and in secret each one by himself; so more solemnly in the public assemblies, which are not carelessly nor wilfully to be neglected or forsaken, when God, by his word or providence, calleth thereunto.”**[^41]

**1. The Abrogation of the Temple-Centric Worship.** No place is holy in itself. John 4:21-23 records Jesus’ words to the Samaritan woman: “Woman, believe me, the hour is coming when neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem will you worship the Father… the true worshipers will worship the Father in spirit and truth.”[^42]

**2. Private and Secret Worship.** God is to be worshipped “everywhere” – in private families daily, and in secret by each individual (Matthew 6:6; Acts 10:2).[^43]

**3. Public Worship Not to Be Neglected.** “So more solemnly in the public assemblies.” Hebrews 10:25 commands, “Not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another.”[^44]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** John 4:21-23; Malachi 1:11; 1 Timothy 2:8; Matthew 6:6; Acts 10:2; Hebrews 10:25; Acts 2:42.[^45]

#### Paragraph 7 – The Appointment of the Lord’s Day

Paragraph 7 grounds the Christian Sabbath in creation and the resurrection: **“As it is the law of nature, that, in general, a proportion of time, by God’s appointment, be set apart for the worship of God; so by his Word, in a positive, moral, and perpetual commandment, binding all men in all ages, he has particularly appointed one day in seven for a Sabbath, to be kept holy unto him; which from the beginning of the world to the resurrection of Christ was the last day of the week; and from the resurrection of Christ was changed to the first day of the week, which is called the Lord’s Day; and is to be continued to the end of the world as the Christian Sabbath, the observation of the last day of the week being abolished.”**[^46]

**1. The Law of Nature Requires a Proportion of Time for Worship.** The confession grounds the Sabbath not only in special revelation but also in “the law of nature.” As Sam Waldron explains, the law of nature “requires an appointed day for worship. First, God must be worshiped publicly and corporately by men.”[^47]

**2. One Day in Seven: A Positive, Moral, Perpetual Commandment.** The confession argues that the **ratio** (one day in seven) of the Sabbath is **moral and perpetual**, while the **specific day** (which day of the week) is **positive** and has been changed.[^48] Exodus 20:8-11 commands, “Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy.”[^49]

**3. The Old Testament Sabbath: The Last Day of the Week.** From the beginning to the resurrection, the Sabbath was observed on the seventh day, commemorating God’s rest from creation (Genesis 2:2-3).[^50]

**4. The Change to the First Day.** “And from the resurrection of Christ was changed to the first day of the week, which is called the Lord’s Day.” 1 Corinthians 16:1-2 records the church gathering on the first day. Acts 20:7 states, “On the first day of the week, when we were gathered together to break bread.” Revelation 1:10 speaks of “the Lord’s Day.”[^51]

**5. The Basis of the Change: The Resurrection of Christ.** The first day of the week is the day of Christ’s resurrection (Matthew 28:1; Mark 16:2; Luke 24:1; John 20:1).[^52]

**6. The Lord’s Day to Continue to the End of the World.** The Christian Sabbath is not a temporary institution but abiding until the end of the age.[^53]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 7:** Exodus 20:8-11; Genesis 2:2-3; 1 Corinthians 16:1-2; Acts 20:7; Revelation 1:10.[^54]

#### Paragraph 8 – The Holy Observation of the Lord’s Day

The final paragraph describes how the Lord’s Day is to be kept holy: **“The sabbath is then kept holy unto the Lord, when men, after a due preparing of their hearts, and ordering their common affairs aforehand, do not only observe an holy rest all the day, from their own works, words, and thoughts about their worldly employments and recreations, but also are taken up the whole time in the public and private exercises of his worship, and in the duties of necessity and mercy.”**[^55]

**1. Preparation Before the Day.** “After a due preparing of their hearts, and ordering their common affairs aforehand.” The confession calls for Sabbath preparation. Exodus 16:22-26 describes the gathering of manna before the Sabbath.[^56]

**2. Holy Rest from Worldly Works, Words, and Thoughts.** The Sabbath rest is not merely external abstinence from labor; it extends to “words, and thoughts about their worldly employments and recreations.” Isaiah 58:13-14 calls the Sabbath “a delight, holy to the LORD.”[^57]

**3. The Whole Time in Worship.** “Taken up the whole time in the public and private exercises of his worship.” The confession rejects the notion that a brief church service fulfills the Sabbath obligation.[^58]

**4. Works of Necessity and Mercy Permitted.** The confession allows two exceptions: duties of necessity (caring for basic needs) and mercy (acts of charity and healing). Matthew 12:11-12 records Jesus’ teaching: “It is lawful to do good on the Sabbath.”[^59]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 8:** Exodus 16:22-26; Exodus 20:8; Leviticus 23:3; Isaiah 58:13-14; Matthew 12:11-12.[^60]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Worship and the Sabbath

Several theological pillars support the confession’s doctrine of worship and the Sabbath.[^61]

**Pillar 1: The Regulative Principle of Worship.** God must be worshipped only as He has commanded in Scripture. Worship is not a matter of human creativity or preference but of divine revelation.[^62]

**Pillar 2: The Trinity Is the Object of Worship.** Worship is given equally to Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.[^63]

**Pillar 3: The Exclusivity of Christ’s Mediatorship.** No worship is acceptable apart from the mediation of Jesus Christ.[^64]

**Pillar 4: The Distinct Elements of Worship.** The confession identifies prayer, Scripture reading, preaching, singing, baptism, and the Lord’s supper as commanded elements.[^65]

**Pillar 5: The Distinction Between Elements and Circumstances.** While the elements of worship are prescribed, the circumstances (time, place, order) are left to Christian prudence guided by general biblical principles.[^66]

**Pillar 6: The Abrogation of Temple-Centric Worship.** Under the New Covenant, no place is holy in itself. Worship is to be offered “in spirit and truth.”[^67]

**Pillar 7: The Fourth Commandment Is Perpetual.** The law of nature and special revelation together require one day in seven for worship.[^68]

**Pillar 8: The Change of the Day.** The day was changed from the seventh to the first day of the week in commemoration of Christ’s resurrection.[^69]

**Pillar 9: The Lord’s Day as Christian Sabbath.** The first day is to be observed as the Christian Sabbath until the end of the world.[^70]

**Pillar 10: Holy Rest and Worship.** The Sabbath is kept holy by rest from worldly employments and by devoting the whole day to public and private worship and works of necessity and mercy.[^71]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Corrupted Worship

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 22.

**Crisis 1: The Normative Principle vs. the Regulative Principle.** Many churches operate under the “normative principle” – whatever is not forbidden is permitted. The confession insists that only what is commanded is permissible.[^72]

**Crisis 2: The Denial of the Sabbath.** Many Christians have abandoned the observance of the Lord’s Day altogether. The confession’s affirmation of the Fourth Commandment as “a positive, moral, and perpetual commandment” directly addresses this.[^73]

**Crisis 3: The Antinomian View of Worship.** Some argue that any style or form of worship is acceptable as long as the heart is sincere. The confession declares that worship must be limited by God’s revealed will.[^74]

**Crisis 4: The Erosion of Distinct Worship Elements.** Many churches have abandoned preaching, singing, baptism, or the Lord’s supper. The confession identifies these as “parts of religious worship” not to be neglected.[^75]

**Crisis 5: The Neglect of the Whole Day.** Even among those who observe Sunday, many treat a brief church service as sufficient. The confession calls for the “whole time” to be taken up in worship.[^76]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Worship and the Sabbath

**Step 1: Worship God Only as He Has Commanded.** Examine your church’s worship. Does it add elements not found in Scripture? Does it neglect elements Scripture commands? Return to the Regulative Principle.[^77]

**Step 2: Pray in the Name of Christ, by the Spirit, According to the Word.** Let your prayers be informed by Scripture, offered through Christ, and empowered by the Spirit.[^78]

**Step 3: Sing the Word.** Let the songs of the church be filled with Scripture – psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs that teach and admonish.[^79]

**Step 4: Prepare for the Lord’s Day.** Order your common affairs beforehand. Prepare your heart through prayer and self-examination before the Sabbath arrives.[^80]

**Step 5: Rest from Worldly Employments and Recreations.** Set aside not only labor but also worldly thoughts, words, and recreations. Let the day be distinct.[^81]

**Step 6: Devote the Whole Day to Worship.** Do not limit worship to a morning service. Let the Lord’s Day be filled with public worship, private devotion, family worship, and acts of mercy.[^82]

**Step 7: Perform Works of Necessity and Mercy.** The Sabbath is not a day of idleness but of holy activity. Acts of charity, visiting the sick, and works of mercy are not violations of the Sabbath but fulfillments of it.[^83]

### Conclusion: The Day That Is Holy to the Lord

Chapter 22 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer’s guide to the worship that God accepts and the day that He has blessed. Worship is not to be invented by human imagination but received from divine revelation. The Lord’s Day is not a human tradition but a divine appointment – the Christian Sabbath, to be kept holy by rest and worship from the rising of the sun to its setting.[^84]

The same God who commanded, “Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy” (Exodus 20:8), is the God who raised His Son from the dead on the first day of the week. The same Spirit who inspired the Scriptures guides the church in acceptable worship. And the same Christ who is the sole Mediator of worship will receive the worship of His people on the day that bears His name.[^85]

**A Prayer from Chapter 22** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O God, who art to be feared, loved, praised, called upon, trusted in, and served with all the heart and all the soul: We thank Thee that Thou hast not left us to our own imaginations but hast revealed the acceptable way of worship in Thy Word. We thank Thee that we may come to Thee through the mediation of Thy Son, by the help of Thy Spirit, according to Thy will. We thank Thee for the Lord’s Day, the Christian Sabbath, set apart for Thy worship.*

*Forgive us for the times we have worshipped according to our own devices, neglected the gathering of the saints, or failed to keep Thy day holy. Grant us to prepare our hearts beforehand, to rest from worldly employments and thoughts, and to be taken up the whole day in public and private exercises of worship and in duties of necessity and mercy. Let Thy day be a delight, and let Thy worship be our joy. For Christ’s sake. Amen.*[^86]
