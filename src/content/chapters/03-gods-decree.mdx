# The Deepest Mystery and Sweetest Comfort: Why the Doctrine of God's Decree Is the Believer's Anchor

(1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 3 – With Complete Footnotes)

## Introduction: The Doctrine That Humbles and Comforts

Chapter 3 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of God's Decree," stands as perhaps the most contested—yet most pastorally necessary—chapter in the entire document.[^1] It addresses the eternal plan of God, the blueprint of all that comes to pass. Following the confession's logical order (Chapter 1: the principle of knowing; Chapter 2: the principle of being), Chapter 3 moves to the works of God, beginning with the internal, eternal decree before treating creation and providence in subsequent chapters.[^2]

The chapter's seven paragraphs unfold like a theological symphony, moving from the general decree (paragraphs 1–2) to the specific decree of election and reprobation (paragraphs 3–7), all while carefully guarding biblical truth against fatalism, determinism, and human pride.[^3] As the Puritan theologian John Owen wrote, "The doctrine of predestination is the marrow of the gospel, not a bone of contention."[^4]

## Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of God's Eternal Purpose

Chapter 3 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on divine sovereignty and human responsibility. The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677–1689—anchored each assertion in an array of biblical texts, refusing to speculate beyond what is written.[^5]

### Paragraph 1 – The General Decree: "All Things Whatsoever Comes to Pass"

The confession opens with a declaration of breathtaking scope:

<ConfessionBox>
"God hath decreed in himself, from all eternity, by the most wise and holy counsel of his own will, freely and unchangeably, all things, whatsoever comes to pass."[^6]
</ConfessionBox>
 This language echoes the apostle Paul's affirmation in Ephesians 1:11, where he writes that God "works all things according to the counsel of his will."[^7]

The confession adds four crucial modifiers to prevent misunderstanding:[^8]

"In himself": The decree is internal to God, not dependent on any external condition.[^9]
"From all eternity": The decree is eternal, not a response to events in time.[^10]
"Freely": The decree flows from God's own good pleasure, not from any compulsion.[^11]
"Unchangeably": The decree is immutable; God does not alter His plan (Numbers 23:19).[^12]
Yet the confession immediately qualifies this absolute statement with three safeguarding clauses:

#### First Safeguard: God Is Not the Author of Sin
"Yet so as thereby is God neither the author of sin nor hath fellowship with any therein."[^13] This clause addresses the most pressing objection to divine sovereignty: if God decrees all things, including sin, is He not the author of evil? The confession answers with an emphatic negative, citing James 1:13 ("Let no one say when he is tempted, 'I am being tempted by God'") and 1 John 1:5 ("God is light, and in him is no darkness at all").[^14]

As Sam Waldron explains, "The Bible makes clear that God's motives in decreeing sin are entirely different than those of the creatures who commit or author sin. He decrees sin to happen for His own glory. They commit it to deny His glory."[^15] The classic illustration is Joseph's brothers: "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (Genesis 50:20).[^16] God decreed the same act for a holy purpose; the brothers performed it for an evil purpose. The cross provides the ultimate example: "there were gathered together against your holy servant Jesus... to do whatever your hand and your plan had predestined to take place" (Acts 4:27-28).[^17]

#### Second Safeguard: Violence Is Not Offered to the Creature's Will
"Nor is violence offered to the will of the creature."[^18] The confession insists that God's decree does not coerce human choices. Creatures act according to their nature, desires, and circumstances—all of which are within God's decree, yet without external force. The confession defines free will not as the ability to act contrary to God's decree (which would make God contingent upon the creature), but as "the ability to will whatever one wants and act on that will to accomplish that want without external constraints."[^19] When Judas betrayed Christ, he did so willingly, not because God forced him against his inclination.[^20]

#### Third Safeguard: Liberty and Contingency of Second Causes Are Established
"Nor yet is the liberty or contingency of second causes taken away, but rather established."[^21] God's sovereign decree does not render human choices meaningless or the laws of nature irrelevant. Instead, it establishes them. The confession cites Acts 4:27-28 (the predestined crucifixion) and John 19:11 (Pilate's authority given from above) to demonstrate that God's decree operates through, not against, secondary causation.[^22] God ordains the end and also ordains the means by which that end comes to pass.[^23] This is not fatalism (where means are irrelevant) but biblical compatibilism.[^24]

#### The Conclusion of Paragraph 1
"in which appears his wisdom in disposing all things, and power and faithfulness in accomplishing his decree."[^25] The doctrine of the decree is not a speculative puzzle to be solved but a doxological reality to be worshipped. It displays God's wisdom (how He orchestrates countless events toward a single glorious end), His power (nothing can thwart His plan), and His faithfulness (He keeps every promise).[^26]

### Paragraph 2 – The Basis of the Decree: Not Foreseen Conditions

Having established what God decrees, the confession now addresses how He decrees it. Paragraph 2 states: "Although God knoweth whatsoever may or can come to pass upon all supposed conditions, yet hath he not decreed anything because he foresaw it as future, or as that which would come to pass upon such conditions."[^28]

This paragraph rejects the doctrine of "middle knowledge" (Latin: scientia media), which teaches that God knows what every free creature would do in any possible set of circumstances, and then bases His decree of election on that foreseen response.[^29] According to this view, God looks down the corridors of time, sees who will believe, and then chooses them accordingly.[^30] The confession denies this because it makes God's decree dependent on the creature rather than on His own sovereign will.[^31]

The confession cites Romans 9, where Paul repeatedly grounds election in God's own purpose, not in human works or foreseen merit: "though they were not yet born and had done nothing either good or bad—in order that God's purpose of election might continue, not because of works but because of him who calls" (Romans 9:11).[^32]

The distinction is crucial: God foreknows because He has decreed; He does not decree because He foreknows. Foreknowledge in Scripture is not mere advance awareness but relational, covenantal knowledge—a choosing, loving knowledge (Romans 8:29; 1 Peter 1:2).[^33] Peter tells us that believers were chosen "according to the foreknowledge of God the Father," not according to their foreseen faith.[^34]

### Paragraphs 3–7 – The Specific Decree: Election and Reprobation

Having established the general decree that covers all things, the confession now narrows its focus to the decree of God regarding rational creatures—angels and human beings.[^36]

### Paragraph 3 – Double Predestination Stated
"By the decree of God, for the manifestation of his glory, some men and angels are predestinated, or foreordained to eternal life through Jesus Christ, to the praise of his glorious grace; others being left to act in their sin to their just condemnation, to the praise of his glorious justice."[^37]

This is the doctrine of double predestination: God has sovereignly chosen some to salvation (election) and passed over others (reprobation).[^38] The confession is careful to state the purpose—"for the manifestation of his glory"—and the outcomes: the elect receive grace; the reprobate receive justice.[^39] The two groups are not symmetrically treated: God actively works grace in the elect, but He merely "leaves" the reprobate to act according to their own sinful natures, which justly condemns them.[^40]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** 1 Timothy 5:21; Matthew 25:34; Ephesians 1:5-6; Romans 9:22-23; Jude 4.[^41]

### Paragraph 4 – The Cause of Election: Grace Alone, Not Works
"As God hath appointed the elect unto glory, so he hath, by the eternal and most free purpose of his will, foreordained all the means thereunto."[^42] This paragraph emphasizes that election is unconditional—not based on foreseen faith or good works.[^43] Those whom God predestined, He also called, justified, and glorified (Romans 8:30). The same decree that appoints the end (salvation) also appoints the means (faith, repentance, perseverance).[^44]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** 2 Thessalonians 2:13-14; Ephesians 1:4; John 6:37-39.[^45]

### Paragraph 5 – The Unconditional Nature of Election
"Those of mankind that are predestinated to life, God, before the foundation of the world was laid, according to his eternal and immutable purpose, and the secret counsel and good pleasure of his will, hath chosen in Christ, unto everlasting glory, out of his mere free grace and love, without any foresight of faith or good works, or perseverance in either of them, or any other thing in the creature, as conditions or causes moving him thereunto."[^46]

This is the confession's strongest affirmation of unconditional election. The phrase "without any foresight of faith or good works" directly counters the Arminian claim that God elects based on foreseen belief.[^47] As Louis Berkhof writes, "Election is that eternal act of God whereby He, in sovereign good pleasure, and on account of no foreseen merit in them, chooses a certain number of men to be the recipients of special grace and of eternal salvation."[^48]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Romans 8:28-30; 2 Timothy 1:9; Ephesians 1:5-6, 11.[^49]

### Paragraph 6 – The Fixed Number of the Elect
The confession affirms that the number of the elect is "so certain and definite, that it cannot be either increased or diminished."[^50] This flows from the immutability of God's decree (Romans 8:28-30; John 10:28-29).[^51]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** John 10:28-29; 2 Timothy 2:19; Romans 8:28-30.[^52]

### Paragraph 7 – Practical Uses of the Doctrine of Predestination
The final paragraph addresses the pastoral purpose of this teaching: "The doctrine of the high mystery of predestination is to be handled with special prudence and care... that men attending the will of God revealed in his Word, and yielding obedience thereunto, may, from the certainty of their effectual vocation, be assured of their eternal election."[^53]

The confession warns against two errors: (1) speculative curiosity that pries into God's secret will, and (2) carnal security that uses election as a license for sin.[^54] Instead, believers are to seek assurance through the means of grace—faith, obedience, and the Spirit's witness.[^55]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 7:** Romans 11:33-36; 1 Peter 5:10-11; 2 Peter 1:10-11; Romans 9:20.[^56]

## Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Robust Doctrine of the Decree

The confession's doctrine of the decree rests on several theological pillars, each essential to maintaining biblical orthodoxy.[^57]

#### Pillar 1: The Unity and Simplicity of the Decree

The confession speaks of "the decree" in the singular, not "the decrees." This reflects the theological principle that God's eternal purpose is a single, unified act of His will, encompassing all that comes to pass.[^58] As Berkhof writes, "The decree of God is His eternal purpose, whereby He has foreordained whatsoever comes to pass."[^59] There are not multiple divine plans layered atop one another. The decree is one comprehensive blueprint, though we may distinguish its parts—election, reprobation, creation, providence—for the sake of human understanding.[^60]

#### Pillar 2: The Aseity and Freedom of the Decree

Because God is self-existent (aseity), His decree flows from His own nature, not from any external influence.[^61] The confession emphasizes that God decrees "freely"—not because He was compelled by anything outside Himself. As Octavius Winslow observed, "What doctrine is more emptying, humbling, and therefore sanctifying, than predestination? It lays the axe at the root of all human boasting."[^62]

#### Pillar 3: The Compatibility of Divine Sovereignty and Human Responsibility (Compatibilism)

The confession refuses to resolve the biblical tension between God's sovereign decree and human accountability by denying either pole. Instead, it affirms both, trusting that God has revealed both truths even if human reason cannot fully reconcile them.[^63] This is compatibilism (not to be confused with philosophical determinism): God's decree and human freedom are compatible because God works through, not against, human choices.[^64] The confession's three "fences" in paragraph 1—denying that God is the author of sin, denying violence to the will, and affirming contingency—establish this compatibilist framework.[^65]

#### Pillar 4: The Unconditional Character of Election

Paragraph 5's affirmation that election is not based on foreseen faith or works is the confession's most explicit declaration of unconditional election.[^66] This does not mean that God's choice is arbitrary or capricious; it means that the ground of election is found in God's own good pleasure, not in any quality or action of the creature.[^67] The Reformed tradition has consistently maintained that God's love for the elect is the cause, not the effect, of their faith.[^68]

#### Pillar 5: The Integration of Ends and Means

The confession refuses to separate the end (salvation) from the means (faith, repentance, perseverance). Paragraph 4 affirms that God "hath, by the eternal and most free purpose of his will, foreordained all the means thereunto."[^69] This guards against antinomianism, which might argue that since God has chosen the elect, their behavior doesn't matter. On the contrary, election ensures the means as surely as it ensures the end. Those whom God predestines, He also calls, justifies, and sanctifies (Romans 8:29-30).[^70]

#### Pillar 6: The Ultimate Purpose: The Glory of God

Chapter 3 repeatedly emphasizes that the decree aims at "the manifestation of his glory" (paragraphs 3, 6, 7).[^71] This is the doxological center of the doctrine. God's eternal purpose is not ultimately about human salvation but about the display of His own perfections—His grace in the elect, His justice in the reprobate.[^72] The believer's proper response is not speculation or pride but worship. As John Piper writes, "God's ultimate goal is to display his glory in the highest possible way, and that happens through the manifestation of both his mercy and his justice."[^73]

## Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Diminished or Distorted View of the Decree

The doctrine of God's decree is not a theological luxury for academic specialists. It addresses deep pastoral crises, both in the life of the individual believer and in the health of the local church.[^74]

#### Crisis 1: The Fear That History Is Out of Control

When believers face tragedy, injustice, or suffering, the natural human response is to wonder: "Is anyone in charge? Does anything matter?"[^75] The doctrine of God's decree answers with a resounding yes. Nothing—not the fall of a sparrow, not the rise of a tyrant, not the diagnosis of cancer—falls outside God's eternal purpose.[^76] This is not fatalistic resignation but hopeful trust. As the confession states, in the decree "appears his wisdom in disposing all things, and power and faithfulness in accomplishing his decree."[^77]

#### Crisis 2: The Loss of Assurance

Many believers struggle to know whether they are truly saved. They look to their fluctuating feelings, their imperfect obedience, and their ongoing struggles with sin, and they despair.[^78] The doctrine of unconditional election offers a different ground for assurance: not our grip on God, but God's grip on us.[^79] If election depends on God's sovereign choice before the foundation of the world, then it does not depend on the variable quality of our performance today. Paragraph 7 explicitly states that believers "may, from the certainty of their effectual vocation, be assured of their eternal election."[^80]

#### Crisis 3: The Arrogance of Self-Sufficiency

The human heart naturally gravitates toward self-justification. We want to believe that our salvation is at least partly our own doing—that we chose God, that we believed, that we persevered.[^81] The confession's doctrine of unconditional election humbles such pride. As Winslow observed, it "lays the axe at the root of all human boasting."[^82] If we were chosen "without any foresight of faith or good works," then we have nothing to boast in except the Lord (1 Corinthians 1:31).[^83]

#### Crisis 4: The Antinomian Temptation

The flip side of humility is license. Some might argue, "If God has unconditionally elected me, then my behavior doesn't matter. I can sin freely and still be saved."[^84] The confession anticipates and rejects this error in paragraphs 4 and 7. Because God has ordained the end (salvation), He has also ordained the means (faith, repentance, good works). Those whom God has predestined, He effectually calls, regenerates, justifies, and sanctifies.[^85] Election is not a license to sin; it is a motivation for holiness (Ephesians 1:4).[^86]

#### Crisis 5: The Rejection of God's Justice

Perhaps the most difficult pastoral challenge posed by the doctrine of the decree concerns the fate of the non-elect. Some object that reprobation makes God unjust.[^87] The confession answers by distinguishing between grace (which is unearned favor) and justice (which is receiving what we deserve). If God gave all people what they deserve, all would be condemned. The wonder is not that some are reprobated, but that any are elected.[^88] Paragraph 3 states that the reprobate are "left to act in their sin to their just condemnation, to the praise of his glorious justice."[^89] As Paul asks, "Is there injustice on God's part? By no means!" (Romans 9:14).[^90]

## Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of God's Eternal Decree

The doctrine of the decree is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are seven practical steps derived from Chapter 3.[^91]

#### Step 1: Handle the Doctrine with Prudence and Care

Paragraph 7 warns that the doctrine of predestination is a "high mystery" to be handled "with special prudence and care."[^92] This means:

Avoid speculative curiosity: Do not waste time trying to peer into God's secret will. Ask instead, "What has God revealed?" (Deuteronomy 29:29).[^93]
Avoid harsh or provocative language: When discussing election with those who disagree, speak with gentleness and respect (1 Peter 3:15).[^94]
Avoid using the doctrine to wound others: Election is meant to humble and comfort, not to divide or intimidate.[^95]
#### Step 2: Ground Your Assurance in Effectual Calling, Not Speculation

Paragraph 7 directs believers to attend "the will of God revealed in his Word" and to seek assurance "from the certainty of their effectual vocation."[^96] This means:

Do not try to read your name in the Lamb's Book of Life. Instead, look for the marks of grace in your life: faith in Christ, love for the brethren, a desire for holiness, perseverance in trial.[^97]
Use the means of grace: prayer, Word, sacraments, fellowship. These are the channels through which effectual calling comes.[^98]
#### Step 3: Let Election Humble You, Not Puff You Up

If you are a believer, remember that you were chosen "without any foresight of faith or good works." You were not chosen because you were smarter, more spiritual, or more deserving than others. The only difference between you and the most hardened sinner is the sheer, sovereign grace of God.[^99] Let this reality produce:

Gratitude: "Why me?" becomes a daily hymn of praise.
Humility: You have nothing to boast in except Christ.
Compassion for the lost: Since you were saved by grace alone, you cannot look down on those who are not yet saved.[^100]
#### Step 4: Trust God's Sovereignty in Times of Suffering

When life falls apart, the doctrine of the decree is not an abstract puzzle but an anchor for the soul. Joseph's words to his brothers become your own: "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (Genesis 50:20).[^101] Your suffering is not random; it is part of God's eternal purpose for your good and His glory (Romans 8:28).[^102] This does not mean suffering is easy, but it means suffering is not meaningless.

#### Step 5: Reject Fatalism and Embrace Means

God's decree does not mean that your choices don't matter. On the contrary, because God has decreed the end, He has also decreed the means.[^103] If you are elected to salvation, you are elected to faith, repentance, prayer, and obedience. Do not use election as an excuse for passivity. Instead:

Pray: Because God has decreed the end, your prayers are part of His ordained means.
Evangelize: Because God has elected some, the gospel must be preached to all (Romans 10:14-15).[^104]
Obey: Because God has chosen you for holiness, pursue it with all your might (Ephesians 1:4).
#### Step 6: Rest in the Immutability of God's Decree

Because God's decree is "unchangeably" fixed, your salvation does not depend on your fluctuating feelings or faltering obedience. If you are in Christ, you are secure. Jesus said, "No one will snatch them out of my hand" (John 10:28).[^105] The same eternal decree that chose you before the foundation of the world will preserve you until the day of glory (Romans 8:29-30).[^106]

#### Step 7: Preach the Decree Doxologically, Not Polemically

Pastors and teachers must preach predestination as Paul preached it: as a source of praise, not a weapon for division.[^107] The confession's paragraphs repeatedly point to doxology: election is "to the praise of his glorious grace" and reprobation is "to the praise of his glorious justice."[^108] When the doctrine of the decree is properly understood, it leads not to controversy but to worship. As Paul exclaims, "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God!" (Romans 11:33).[^109]

## Conclusion: The Deepest Mystery and Sweetest Comfort

Chapter 3 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's anchor in a chaotic world. The doctrine of God's decree—His eternal, free, and unchangeable purpose to bring all things to pass for His own glory—is at once the deepest mystery and the sweetest comfort of the Christian faith.[^110]

It is a mystery because we cannot fully reconcile God's sovereignty with human responsibility, nor can we penetrate the secret counsels of the Almighty. Yet it is a comfort because it assures us that history is not spinning out of control, that our salvation does not depend on our fickle wills, and that the same God who chose us before the foundation of the world will keep us to the end.[^111]

The confession's concluding paragraph (7) offers wise pastoral counsel: "the doctrine of the high mystery of predestination is to be handled with special prudence and care." It is not for speculative curiosity but for "praise, reverence, and admiration of God." It is not for carnal security but for "humility, diligence, and abundant consolation" to all who sincerely obey the gospel.[^112]

May we, like the apostle Paul, fall to our knees in worship when we contemplate this great truth: "For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be glory forever. Amen" (Romans 11:36).[^113]

## A Prayer from Chapter 3 (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

O Lord, who art from everlasting to everlasting: We confess that we have often stumbled at the mystery of Thy decree. We have been tempted to make Thee the author of sin, or to deny Thy sovereignty altogether. Forgive us. Teach us to tremble and to trust—to tremble before Thy holiness, and to trust in Thy goodness.

We thank Thee that Thou hast chosen us in Christ before the foundation of the world, not because of any foreseen merit in us, but out of Thine own mere free grace and love. We thank Thee that our salvation does not depend on our fickle wills but on Thy unchangeable purpose.

Grant us, by Thy Spirit, to handle this high mystery with prudence and care—not for speculation, but for praise; not for pride, but for humility; not for carnal security, but for abundant consolation.

In the name of Jesus Christ, our elect and eternal Redeemer. Amen.[^114]
