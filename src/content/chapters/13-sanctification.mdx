# The Saint's Ongoing Battle: Why Sanctification Is the Believer's Journey from Grace to Grace

Chapter 13 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Sanctification," stands as the necessary companion to justification and adoption. If Chapter 11 declares the believer's legal standing before God's court, and Chapter 12 announces that the justified sinner is welcomed into God's family as a beloved child, then Chapter 13 describes the *transformative process* by which God conforms His children to the image of His Son.[^1] As Sam Waldron notes, "Sanctification is not an optional extra for Christians; it is an essential part of the salvation God gives to all who are in union with his Son."[^2]

The chapter comprises three paragraphs, each building upon the previous. Paragraph 1 describes the *nature and substance of sanctification*—its character, source, means, substance, and necessity. Paragraph 2 addresses the *struggle with remaining sin*—the "continual and irreconcilable war" between the flesh and the Spirit that marks the believer's earthly pilgrimage. Paragraph 3 speaks of *progress in grace*—the certainty and quality of the saint's growth in holiness, even in the midst of conflict.[^3]

As the Confession states in its opening lines, sanctification belongs to those who are "united to Christ, effectually called, and regenerated."[^4] It is not a separate class of Christians or an optional second blessing; it is the universal experience of every true believer.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Sanctification

Chapter 13 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on how God actually transforms His people into holiness.[^5]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Nature and Substance of Sanctification

The confession opens with a declaration that links sanctification inseparably to union with Christ: **"They who are united to Christ, effectually called, and regenerated, having a new heart and a new spirit created in them through the virtue of Christ's death and resurrection, are also farther sanctified, really and personally, through the same virtue, by his Word and Spirit dwelling in them; the dominion of the whole body of sin is destroyed, and the several lusts of it are more and more weakened and mortified, and they more and more quickened and strengthened in all saving graces, to the practice of all true holiness, without which no man shall see the Lord."**[^6]

**1. The Foundation: Union with Christ.** "They who are united to Christ."[^7] Sanctification is not a human achievement but a divine gift that flows from union with Christ. As the Puritan Walter Marshall wrote in his classic *The Gospel-Mystery of Sanctification*, "The only way to true holiness is to receive it from Christ by faith, as a grace flowing from our union with him."[^8] The confession cites Acts 20:32 ("I commend you to God and to the word of his grace, which is able to build you up and to give you the inheritance among all those who are sanctified") and Romans 6:5-6 ("For if we have been united with him in a death like his, we shall certainly be united with him in a resurrection like his").[^9]

**2. The Preconditions: Effectual Calling and Regeneration.** "Effectually called, and regenerated, having a new heart and a new spirit created in them."[^10] Sanctification presupposes the new birth. A dead person cannot grow; a stone cannot be made warm. Only those who have been given a heart of flesh (Ezekiel 36:26) can pursue holiness.[^11]

**3. The Basis: The Virtue of Christ's Death and Resurrection.** "Through the virtue of Christ's death and resurrection."[^12] The same power that raised Christ from the dead is at work in the believer's sanctification (Ephesians 1:19-20). Christ's death broke the power of sin (Romans 6:10); Christ's resurrection secured new life (Romans 6:4).[^13]

**4. The Character: "Really and Personally."** The confession is emphatic that sanctification is not merely positional or external. It is "really and personally" —a genuine, inward transformation of the whole person.[^14]

**5. The Means: "By His Word and Spirit Dwelling in Them."** God works through two instruments: the external Word and the internal Spirit. The confession cites John 17:17 ("Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth"), Ephesians 3:16-19, and 1 Thessalonians 5:21-23.[^15] The Spirit does not sanctify apart from the Word nor merely alongside it; He works *through* it.

**6. The Negative Aspect: Mortification.** "The dominion of the whole body of sin is destroyed, and the several lusts of it are more and more weakened and mortified."[^16] The confession distinguishes between the *reign* of sin (destroyed at conversion) and the *remnants* of sin (progressively weakened). Romans 6:14 declares, "Sin will have no dominion over you."[^17] Galatians 5:24 affirms, "Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires."[^18]

**7. The Positive Aspect: Vivification.** "They more and more quickened and strengthened in all saving graces."[^19] Sanctification has two inseparable parts: mortification (putting sin to death) and vivification (being made alive to righteousness). Colossians 1:11 speaks of being "strengthened with all power, according to his glorious might, for all endurance and patience with joy."[^20]

**8. The Goal: Practice of True Holiness.** "To the practice of all true holiness."[^21] Sanctification is not merely internal feelings or intentions; it issues in concrete obedience.[^22]

**9. The Necessity: Without Which No Man Shall See the Lord.** The confession cites 2 Corinthians 7:1 ("Let us cleanse ourselves from every defilement of body and spirit, bringing holiness to completion in the fear of God") and Hebrews 12:14 ("Strive for peace with everyone, and for the holiness without which no one will see the Lord").[^23] Holiness is not the ground of salvation but the necessary evidence of it.

#### Paragraph 2 – The Struggle with Remaining Sin

Paragraph 2 addresses the reality of indwelling sin in the life of the believer: **"This sanctification is throughout the whole man, yet imperfect in this life; there abides still some remnants of corruption in every part, wherefrom arises a continual and irreconcilable war; the flesh lusting against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh."**[^25]

**1. The Comprehensiveness of Sanctification.** "Throughout the whole man."[^26] Sanctification is not limited to the soul or the spirit; it affects every part of the believer's being. 1 Thessalonians 5:23 declares, "May the God of peace himself sanctify you completely, and may your whole spirit and soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ."[^27]

**2. The Imperfection of Sanctification.** "Yet imperfect in this life."[^28] No believer in this life attains sinless perfection. The confession rejects the error of perfectionism. Romans 7:18-23 describes the ongoing struggle of the regenerate person: "For I know that nothing good dwells in me, that is, in my flesh... I see in my members another law waging war against the law of my mind."[^29]

**3. The Remnants of Corruption.** "There abides still some remnants of corruption in every part."[^30] Sin is not eradicated in this life; it remains in every faculty of the believer's being.[^12] The confession cites Romans 7:18 and 7:23 in support.[^31]

**4. The Continual and Irreconcilable War.** "Wherefrom arises a continual and irreconcilable war."[^32] The Christian life is not a playground but a battlefield. Galatians 5:17 declares, "The desires of the flesh are against the Spirit, and the desires of the Spirit are against the flesh, for these are opposed to each other, to keep you from doing the things you want to do."[^33] 1 Peter 2:11 exhorts believers to "abstain from the passions of the flesh, which wage war against your soul."[^34]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** 1 Thessalonians 5:23; Romans 7:18, 23; Galatians 5:17; 1 Peter 2:11.[^35]

#### Paragraph 3 – Progress in Grace

The confession concludes with the certainty and quality of the saint's growth: **"In which war, although the remaining corruption for a time may much prevail, yet, through the continual supply of strength from the sanctifying Spirit of Christ, the regenerate part does overcome; and so the saints grow in grace, perfecting holiness in the fear of God, pressing after an heavenly life, in evangelical obedience to all the commands which Christ as Head and King, in his Word has prescribed to them."**[^36]

**1. The Difficulty of the War.** "Although the remaining corruption for a time may much prevail."[^37] The confession does not minimize the reality of defeat and discouragement in the Christian life. Romans 7:23 describes the "law of sin" that wages war against the believer's mind.[^38]

**2. The Certainty of Victory.** "Yet, through the continual supply of strength from the sanctifying Spirit of Christ, the regenerate part does overcome."[^39] The believer may lose battles, but the war is won. Romans 6:14 declares, "Sin will have no dominion over you."[^40] The Spirit supplies strength continuously.

**3. The Growth of the Saints.** "The saints grow in grace."[^41] Sanctification is progressive. 2 Peter 3:18 exhorts, "Grow in the grace and knowledge of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ."[^42]

**4. The Quality of Progress.** "Perfecting holiness in the fear of God, pressing after an heavenly life, in evangelical obedience to all the commands which Christ as Head and King, in his Word has prescribed to them."[^43] The confession cites 2 Corinthians 7:1 ("bringing holiness to completion in the fear of God"), 2 Corinthians 3:18 ("we all... are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another"), and Ephesians 4:15-16 ("we are to grow up in every way into him who is the head, into Christ").[^44]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Romans 7:23; Romans 6:14; Ephesians 4:15-16; 2 Corinthians 3:18; 2 Corinthians 7:1.[^45]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Sanctification

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of sanctification.

**Pillar 1: Definitive and Progressive Sanctification.** Waldron notes that the confession distinguishes between *definitive sanctification* (the initial break with sin at conversion) and *progressive sanctification* (the ongoing growth in holiness).[^46] The confession states that "the dominion of the whole body of sin is destroyed" (definitive) and "the several lusts of it are more and more weakened and mortified" (progressive).[^47]

**Pillar 2: Union with Christ as the Source.** Sanctification flows from union with Christ. As one expositor explains, "It is only as we are in Him that the grace of justification, of adoption, of sanctification flows to us from Christ."[^48] The confession begins with union with Christ because all holiness derives from Him.

**Pillar 3: Mortification and Vivification.** Sanctification has two inseparable parts: mortification (putting sin to death) and vivification (being made alive to righteousness). John Owen famously wrote, "Be killing sin, or sin will be killing you."[^49]

**Pillar 4: The War Between Flesh and Spirit.** The confession describes the Christian life as a "continual and irreconcilable war." This is not a sign of abnormality but of authenticity. The presence of conflict is evidence of life.[^50]

**Pillar 5: The Necessity of Holiness.** Without holiness, no one will see the Lord. Holiness is not the ground of salvation but the necessary evidence of it.[^51]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Sanctification

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 13.

**Crisis 1: The Perfectionist Error.** Some traditions teach that believers can achieve sinless perfection in this life. The confession declares that sanctification is "imperfect in this life" and that "remnants of corruption" remain.[^52] Perfectionism leads either to despair or hypocrisy.

**Crisis 2: The Antinomian Error.** At the opposite extreme, some argue that since justification is by faith alone, holiness does not matter. The confession declares that without holiness, no one will see the Lord.[^53]

**Crisis 3: The Quietistic Error.** Some teach that believers should "let go and let God" without active exertion. The confession speaks of "mortifying" and "quickening"—active verbs that require the believer's engagement.[^54]

**Crisis 4: The Despair of Struggling Believers.** Many believers become discouraged when they discover that the Christian life is a war, not a vacation. The confession assures them that the presence of conflict is evidence of life.[^55]

**Crisis 5: The Neglect of the Means of Grace.** Sanctification does not happen in a vacuum. The confession affirms that God sanctifies "by his Word and Spirit dwelling in them." Neglect of the Word, prayer, and the sacraments is neglect of the means of grace.[^56]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Sanctification

**Step 1: Rest in Definitive Sanctification.** Remember that sin's dominion has been broken. You are no longer a slave to sin. Fight from victory, not for victory.[^57]

**Step 2: Engage in the Mortification of Sin.** Daily put sin to death by the Spirit. Identify specific sins—not generalities. Pray for the Spirit's help. Use the Word as your sword.[^58]

**Step 3: Pursue Vivification.** Do not merely avoid evil; pursue righteousness. Cultivate the graces of the Spirit: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control.[^59]

**Step 4: Use the Means of Grace.** Immerse yourself in the Word. Pray without ceasing. Gather with the saints. Partake of the Lord's Supper. These are the channels through which the Spirit sanctifies.[^60]

**Step 5: Embrace the War.** Do not be surprised by the struggle. The presence of conflict is evidence that you are alive. When you fall, get up. Confess your sin. Return to the battle.[^61]

**Step 6: Look Forward to Final Perfection.** The war will not last forever. The day is coming when you will be fully and finally sanctified—"without spot or wrinkle or any such thing" (Ephesians 5:27). Let that hope fuel your present pursuit.[^62]

### Conclusion: The Saint's Ongoing Battle

Chapter 13 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's battle plan for the Christian life. Sanctification is not optional; it is essential. It is not passive; it requires active engagement. It is not instantaneous; it is progressive. And it is not solitary; it flows from union with Christ and is accomplished by the Spirit through the Word.

The confession calls believers to pursue holiness with all their might, while resting in the finished work of Christ. It acknowledges that the war is real and the enemy is fierce, but it declares that the regenerate part will overcome through the Spirit's power. The same grace that justified will sanctify. The same power that raised Christ from the dead works in believers to will and to do for His good pleasure.

**A Prayer from Chapter 13** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father of mercies, who hast united us to Christ by faith: We thank Thee that Thou dost not only justify us but sanctify us. We thank Thee that the dominion of sin is broken, and that its remnants are being weakened day by day. Grant us to mortify the deeds of the body by Thy Spirit, and to be quickened and strengthened in all saving graces. When we fall, raise us up. When we are weary, renew our strength. And bring us at last to that perfect holiness without which no one sees Thee. In Jesus' name. Amen.*[^63]
