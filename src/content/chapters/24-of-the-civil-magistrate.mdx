# The Sword That Is Not Ours: Why the Civil Magistrate Is God's Servant for Justice, Not the Lord of the Conscience

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 24 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: A Necessary but Neglected Chapter

Chapter 24 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Civil Magistrate," addresses the relationship between the church and the civil government.[^1] In an era of increasing state overreach and the erosion of religious liberty, this chapter is as relevant as ever. The confession's authors—Particular Baptists who had suffered persecution under both Anglican establishment and Puritan rule—crafted a careful statement affirming the legitimacy of civil government while firmly resisting any claim of the state over the conscience or the church.[^2]

The chapter is relatively brief, comprising just three paragraphs. Paragraph 1 affirms the divine institution of civil government and the Christian's duty to pray for and obey magistrates. Paragraph 2 defines the proper limits of the magistrate's authority, specifically denying any power over the church or the conscience. Paragraph 3 declares that the pope has no civil jurisdiction and that the magistrate may lawfully wage war.[^3]

The chapter stands against two errors: **theocratic overreach** (the claim that the state has authority over the church) and **antinomian anarchy** (the denial that the state has any legitimate authority at all).[^4] As one expositor notes, "The 1689 Confession presents a balanced view that the magistrate is God's servant for justice but not the lord of the conscience."[^5]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Civil Government

Chapter 24 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, limits, and purpose of civil authority.[^6]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Divine Institution of Civil Government and the Christian's Duty

The confession opens: <ConfessionBox>
"God, the supreme Lord and King of all the world, has ordained civil magistrates to be under him, over the people, for his own glory and the public good; and to this end has armed them with the power of the sword, for defense and encouragement of them that do good, and for the punishment of evil doers."
</ConfessionBox>[^7]

**1. God as the Supreme Lord.** "God, the supreme Lord and King of all the world."[^8] The confession grounds civil authority in God's sovereign ordination, not in any social contract or human will. Romans 13:1 declares, "Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God."[^9]

**2. The Purpose of Civil Government.** "For his own glory and the public good."[^10] The two ends of government are (a) God's glory and (b) the public good. The magistrate is God's servant. Romans 13:4 states, "He is God's servant for your good."[^11]

**3. The Power of the Sword.** "Has armed them with the power of the sword, for defense and encouragement of them that do good, and for the punishment of evil doers."[^12] The confession affirms that the civil magistrate legitimately possesses coercive power, including the power of capital punishment. Romans 13:4 continues: "But if you do wrong, be afraid, for he does not bear the sword in vain. For he is the servant of God, an avenger who carries out God's wrath on the wrongdoer."[^13] 1 Peter 2:13-14 commands, "Be subject for the Lord's sake to every human institution, whether it be to the emperor as supreme, or to governors as sent by him to punish those who do evil and to praise those who do good."[^14]

**4. The Christian's Duty: Prayer and Obedience.** The confession continues: <ConfessionBox>
"It is the duty of Christians to pray for magistrates, to honor their persons, to pay them tribute and other dues, to obey their lawful commands, and to be subject to their authority, for conscience' sake. Infidelity or difference in religion does not make void the magistrate's just and legal authority, nor free the people from their due obedience to him; from which ecclesiastical persons are not exempted, much less has the Pope any power or jurisdiction over them in their dominions, or over any of their people; and least of all to deprive them of their dominions or lives, if he shall judge them to be heretics, or upon any other pretense whatsoever."
</ConfessionBox>[^15]

- **Pray for magistrates.** 1 Timothy 2:1-2 exhorts, "First of all, then, I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions."[^16]
- **Honor their persons.** 1 Peter 2:17 commands, "Honor the emperor."[^17]
- **Pay tribute and dues.** Romans 13:6-7 states, "For because of this you also pay taxes, for the authorities are ministers of God... Pay to all what is owed to them: taxes to whom taxes are owed."[^18]
- **Obey lawful commands.** Titus 3:1 commands, "Remind them to be submissive to rulers and authorities, to be obedient."[^19]
- **For conscience' sake.** Romans 13:5 declares, "Therefore one must be in subjection, not only to avoid God's wrath but also for the sake of conscience."[^20]

**5. Infidelity Does Not Void Authority.** "Infidelity or difference in religion does not make void the magistrate's just and legal authority."[^21] Even a pagan or ungodly magistrate possesses legitimate authority, because authority comes from God, not from the moral character of the officeholder.[^22]

**6. Ecclesiastical Persons Are Not Exempt.** "From which ecclesiastical persons are not exempted."[^23] Pastors and church officers are subject to the civil magistrate just like any other citizen.

**7. The Pope Has No Civil Jurisdiction.** The confession emphatically rejects papal claims to depose kings or release subjects from allegiance. The Pope has "no power or jurisdiction over them in their dominions, or over any of their people; and least of all to deprive them of their dominions or lives, if he shall judge them to be heretics, or upon any other pretense whatsoever."[^24] This reflects the historical experience of the Reformers, who faced excommunication and threats of deposition from Rome.[^25]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Limits of the Magistrate's Authority

Paragraph 2 states: <ConfessionBox>
"It is the duty of civil magistrates to protect the church of our Lord, without giving the preference to any one denomination of Christians above the rest, in such manner that all ecclesiastical persons whatever shall enjoy the full, free, and unquestioned liberty of dispensing the word of God, of managing their worship and discipline, and of being subject to Christ as their only Head and King; without any manner of force upon their consciences, or the least countenance given to those who invade the rights of Christ, and the just liberties of his church. But as the civil magistrate is not authorized to assume the government of the church, nor to take upon him the administration of its ordinances, or the power of the keys, so neither is he empowered to determine controversies of faith, or to compel any man to believe or worship contrary to his conscience; yet as nursing fathers, it is his duty to preserve the unity, peace, and purity of the church; to suppress blasphemy and heresy; to prevent or reform all corruptions and abuses in worship and discipline; and to preserve and advance the true religion and all its ordinances, according to the word of God."
</ConfessionBox>[^27]

**1. The Positive Duty: Protect the Church, Not Prefer One Denomination.** "It is the duty of civil magistrates to protect the church of our Lord, without giving the preference to any one denomination of Christians above the rest."[^28] The confession forbids the establishment of a state church. The magistrate should protect all Christians equally, not favor one group over another.[^29]

**2. The Liberty of Ecclesiastical Persons.** "All ecclesiastical persons whatever shall enjoy the full, free, and unquestioned liberty of dispensing the word of God, of managing their worship and discipline, and of being subject to Christ as their only Head and King."[^30] This is the Baptist insistence on the freedom of the church from state control. The church's only head is Christ.[^31]

**3. No Force Upon Consciences.** "Without any manner of force upon their consciences."[^32] The confession explicitly denies the magistrate any power to coerce the conscience. This is a direct repudiation of the medieval and Reformed practice of compelling religious conformity by civil penalties.[^33]

**4. The Negative Limit: No Authority Over the Church.** "The civil magistrate is not authorized to assume the government of the church, nor to take upon him the administration of its ordinances, or the power of the keys."[^34] The confession distinguishes between the civil magistrate's sword (coercive power) and the church's keys (spiritual authority).[^35]

**5. No Authority to Determine Controversies of Faith.** "Neither is he empowered to determine controversies of faith, or to compel any man to believe or worship contrary to his conscience."[^36] This is the confession's robust affirmation of the liberty of conscience (see Chapter 21). Only God is Lord of the conscience.[^37]

**6. The Positive Duty as Nursing Fathers.** "Yet as nursing fathers, it is his duty to preserve the unity, peace, and purity of the church; to suppress blasphemy and heresy; to prevent or reform all corruptions and abuses in worship and discipline; and to preserve and advance the true religion and all its ordinances, according to the word of God."[^38] The confession draws on Isaiah 49:23, where kings and queens are called "nursing fathers" and "nursing mothers" to the church.[^39]

**7. The Apparent Tension.** The confession acknowledges a tension: the magistrate is not to coerce consciences, yet he is to suppress blasphemy and heresy. The resolution lies in the distinction between civil punishment for external acts that disturb public order (blasphemy, heresy that leads to sedition) and the internal coercion of belief. The magistrate may restrain actions, but he may not compel faith.[^40]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Legitimacy of War and the Rejection of Papal Jurisdiction

The confession concludes: <ConfessionBox>
"And although the magistrate is to defend the church, and to manage all civil and just administrations, yet he ought not to intermeddle with ecclesiastical affairs, otherwise than as a nursing father, and to provide for the peace and prosperity of the commonwealth, in all things. We further confess, that it is lawful for Christians, in a just and necessary war, to bear arms, and to serve in the wars, upon the command of the magistrate; and that the Pope, neither of himself, nor by any authority of the church, or see of Rome, hath any power or jurisdiction whatsoever over the civil magistrates, or their subjects, or any of their dominions. And we utterly deny, that he, or any other foreign power, can lawfully depose the magistrate, or absolve the people from their allegiance, upon any pretence whatsoever."
</ConfessionBox>[^42]

**1. The Magistrate Not to Intermeddle with Ecclesiastical Affairs.** "He ought not to intermeddle with ecclesiastical affairs, otherwise than as a nursing father."[^43] The confession reiterates the principle that the magistrate's role is civil, not ecclesiastical.

**2. The Legitimacy of Just and Necessary War.** "It is lawful for Christians, in a just and necessary war, to bear arms, and to serve in the wars, upon the command of the magistrate."[^44] This is a rejection of pacifism (the view that Christians may never bear arms). The confession cites Luke 3:14, where John the Baptist does not tell soldiers to leave the army but to "be content with your wages."[^45]

**3. The Pope Has No Civil Jurisdiction.** The confession repeats its rejection of papal authority: "The Pope, neither of himself, nor by any authority of the church, or see of Rome, hath any power or jurisdiction whatsoever over the civil magistrates, or their subjects, or any of their dominions."[^46]

**4. No Foreign Power Can Depose the Magistrate.** "We utterly deny, that he, or any other foreign power, can lawfully depose the magistrate, or absolve the people from their allegiance, upon any pretence whatsoever."[^47] This is a direct repudiation of the papal doctrine of *deposing power* — the claim that the Pope could release subjects from their oath of allegiance to a heretical ruler.[^48]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Biblical View of Civil Government

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of the civil magistrate.

**Pillar 1: Civil authority is Ordained by God.** The magistrate is not a necessary evil but a divine institution for the public good.[^50]

**Pillar 2: The Power of the Sword.** The magistrate legitimately possesses coercive power, including the power of capital punishment and war.[^51]

**Pillar 3: The Christian's Duty of Submission.** Christians must pray for, honor, pay taxes to, and obey civil magistrates for conscience' sake.[^52]

**Pillar 4: The Distinction Between Civil and Ecclesiastical Authority.** The magistrate has no authority over the church's worship, discipline, or doctrine. The church's only head is Christ.[^53]

**Pillar 5: Liberty of Conscience.** The magistrate may not compel anyone to believe or worship contrary to his conscience.[^54]

**Pillar 6: The Magistrate as Nursing Father.** The magistrate has a positive duty to preserve the peace, suppress blasphemy, and advance true religion, but without coercing conscience.[^55]

**Pillar 7: Just War.** Christians may lawfully bear arms and serve in wars commanded by the magistrate, provided the war is just and necessary.[^56]

**Pillar 8: Rejection of Papal Deposing Power.** The Pope has no jurisdiction over civil magistrates, nor can he release subjects from their allegiance.[^57]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted View of Civil Government

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 24.

**Crisis 1: The Erosion of Respect for Civil Authority.** Many Christians have become cynical about government and refuse to pray for leaders or pay taxes. Paragraph 1 reminds believers of their duty.[^58]

**Crisis 2: Theocratic Overreach.** Some Christians believe the state should enforce religious conformity. Paragraph 2 denies the magistrate any authority to coerce consciences.[^59]

**Crisis 3: The Separation of Church and State Misunderstood.** The confession supports a separation of the *institutions* of church and state, but not a separation of faith from public life. The magistrate is to be a "nursing father" to the church.[^60]

**Crisis 4: Pacifism and the Denial of Just War.** Some Christians believe bearing arms is always sinful. Paragraph 3 affirms the legitimacy of just war.[^61]

**Crisis 5: The Revival of Papal Claims.** Ecumenical movements sometimes minimize the historic Protestant rejection of papal jurisdiction. Paragraph 3 restates the Reformation position.[^62]

**Crisis 6: The Temptation to Anarchism.** Christians who believe government is inherently evil refuse all submission. Paragraph 1 declares that government is God's servant for good.[^63]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Civil Magistrate

**Step 1: Pray for Your Government Leaders.** Even if you disagree with them politically, pray for them by name. God has placed them in authority for His purposes.[^64]

**Step 2: Honor and Obey Lawful Commands.** Pay your taxes, obey traffic laws, and submit to legitimate authority — not only to avoid punishment but for conscience' sake.[^65]

**Step 3: Distinguish Between Civil and Spiritual Authority.** Do not look to the state for spiritual guidance, and do not look to the church for civil coercion. Render to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's.[^66]

**Step 4: Resist Unjust Laws with Conscience, Not Violence.** When the magistrate commands what God forbids or forbids what God commands, you must obey God rather than men (Acts 5:29). But resist through lawful means and suffering, not through insurrection.[^67]

**Step 5: Support Just and Necessary War.** Do not embrace pacifism as a Christian absolute. The magistrate bears the sword for a reason.[^68]

**Step 6: Reject Papal Claims to Civil Authority.** No foreign power—religious or secular—has jurisdiction over your civil allegiance. The Pope has no authority to depose magistrates or absolve subjects from their oath.[^69]

**Step 7: Engage as a Citizen, Not as a Theocrat.** Work for the public good, vote, advocate for justice, but do not seek to establish a theocracy. The kingdom of Christ is not of this world (John 18:36).[^70]

### Conclusion: The Sword That Is Not Ours

Chapter 24 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century political theology. It is the believer's guide to the complex relationship between the kingdom of God and the kingdoms of this world. The civil magistrate is God's servant, ordained for the public good, armed with the sword for the punishment of evildoers. Christians must pray for, honor, pay taxes to, and obey magistrates for conscience' sake. Yet the magistrate has no authority over the church or the conscience. The church's only head is Christ. The Pope has no jurisdiction over civil rulers. And Christians may lawfully bear arms in just and necessary war.[^71]

The confession calls believers to pray for their leaders, to honor and obey lawful commands, to distinguish civil from spiritual authority, to resist unjust laws with conscience, to support just war, to reject papal claims to civil authority, and to engage as citizens without seeking to establish a theocracy. The same God who ordains the magistrate also limits his power. The same Christ who is King of kings is also Head of the church. And the same Spirit who gives liberty of conscience also calls us to submit to lawful authority for the Lord's sake.[^72]

**A Prayer from Chapter 24** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O God, the supreme Lord and King of all the world: We thank Thee for the institution of civil government, ordained for Thy glory and the public good. We thank Thee for magistrates who bear the sword not in vain, as Thy servants for our good. Grant us to pray for our leaders, to honor their persons, to pay tribute, and to obey their lawful commands for conscience' sake.*

*Keep us from the errors of those who would make the state the lord of the conscience, and from those who would deny all civil authority. Protect the church from the overreach of the magistrate, and protect the magistrate from the overreach of the Pope. Give us wisdom to render to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's. And when the magistrate commands what Thou dost forbid, give us courage to obey Thee rather than men. For Christ's sake. Amen.*[^73]
