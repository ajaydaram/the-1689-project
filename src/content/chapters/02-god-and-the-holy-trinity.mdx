**The Foundation of All Theology: Why the Doctrine of God Is the Articulus Primus**

---

### Introduction: The Necessary Starting Point

Before we speak of salvation, the church, or the last things, we must speak of God. The doctrine of God—*theologia propria*—is not one doctrine among many. It is the doctrine upon which all others depend. As Stephen Charnock observed, "It is the knowledge of God alone that can make us like Him, or bring us to the enjoyment of Him."[^1]

Chapter 2 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, titled "Of God and of the Holy Trinity," serves as the confession's *articulus primus*—its first and foundational article.[^2] Following the epistemological groundwork of Chapter 1 (Holy Scripture), Chapter 2 declares *what kind of God* this Scripture reveals. It is divided into three paragraphs: (1) the divine attributes, (2) God's relation to creation, and (3) the doctrine of the Trinity.[^3]

Waldron notes that Chapter 2 epitomizes "the Classical Christian theism of the historic tradition of the church" and itemizes "the specific attributes of that majestic God of the Bible."[^4] The chapter does not merely inform; it transforms. To confess the God of Scripture is to be brought low in humility and lifted up in worship.

The chapter's structure follows a logical progression:

- **Paragraph 1**: Who God is in Himself (theology in the absolute sense)
- **Paragraph 2**: Who God is in relation to creation (theology in the relative sense)
- **Paragraph 3**: Who God is as triune (theology in the personal sense)

Each paragraph builds on the previous, moving from the unity of the divine essence to the diversity of the divine persons, all while grounding every assertion in Scripture.[^5]

---

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Divine Revelation

Chapter 2 is a mosaic of biblical citations and allusions, each clause chiseled from the quarry of Holy Writ. The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677—deliberately crafted a document that would serve not as a substitute for Scripture but as a faithful summary of its teaching.[^6] To grasp their intention, we must examine the exegetical grounding of each paragraph.

---

#### Paragraph 1 – The Attributes of God: Who God Is in Himself

The confession opens with a declaration of divine unity: "The Lord our God is but one only living and true God."[^7] This echoes the *Shema* of Deuteronomy 6:4—"Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one"—which served as Israel's daily confession of faith. The Hebrew word *'eḥad* signifies a composite unity, not a mathematical singularity, leaving room for the later revelation of the Trinity.[^8]

The confession then affirms God's *aseity* (self-existence): "whose subsistence is in and of Himself."[^9] The Latin term *a se* ("from Himself") captures the biblical teaching that God has no origin, no cause, and no dependency. Isaiah 48:12 declares, "I am He; I am the first, and I am the last."[^10] And John 5:26 affirms that the Father "has life in Himself"—a prerogative He shares with the Son.[^11]

The confession proceeds to enumerate a cascade of divine attributes, each drawn from specific scriptural texts:

| Attribute | Confession's Phrase | Scripture Proof |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Infinity | "infinite in being and perfection" | Psalm 145:3 ("His greatness is unsearchable") |
| Incomprehensibility | "essence cannot be comprehended by any but Himself" | Job 11:7–9 ("Can you discover the depths of God?") |
| Spirituality | "a most pure spirit, invisible, without body, parts, or passions" | John 4:24 ("God is spirit"); 1 Timothy 1:17 ("invisible") |
| Immortality | "only hath immortality" | 1 Timothy 6:16 ("who alone has immortality") |
| Immutability | "immutable" | Malachi 3:6 ("I the LORD do not change") |
| Eternity | "eternal" | Psalm 90:2 ("from everlasting to everlasting you are God") |
| Almightiness | "almighty" | Revelation 4:8 ("Lord God Almighty") |
| Holiness | "most holy" | Isaiah 6:3 ("Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts") |

The clause "without body, parts, or passions" is particularly significant.[^12] It guards against anthropomorphism (attributing human physicality to God) and patripassianism (suggesting the Father suffered on the cross). The simplicity of God—that He is not composed of parts—is an inference drawn from His aseity: if God were composite, the components would be prior to Him and more ultimate than He.[^13]

The confession then pivots to God's moral attributes: "most wise, most free, most absolute, working all things according to the counsel of his own immutable and most righteous will, for his own glory."[^14] This reflects Paul's doxology in Ephesians 1:11—"works all things according to the counsel of his will"—and the repeated biblical assertion that God acts "for his own name's sake" (Ezekiel 36:22).[^15]

Finally, the confession balances God's justice and mercy: "most loving, gracious, merciful, long-suffering, abundant in goodness and truth, forgiving iniquity, transgression, and sin... and withal most just and terrible in his judgments, hating all sin, and who will by no means clear the guilty."[^16] This juxtaposition mirrors Exodus 34:6–7, where God proclaims His name to Moses: "The LORD, the LORD, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness... yet by no means clearing the guilty."[^17]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Deuteronomy 6:4; 1 Corinthians 8:4–6; Isaiah 48:12; John 5:26; Job 11:7–9; Psalm 139:6; John 4:24; 1 Timothy 1:17; 1 Timothy 6:15–16; Psalm 90:2; Malachi 3:6; Genesis 17:1; Revelation 4:8; Isaiah 6:3; Psalm 115:3; Ephesians 1:11; Acts 15:18; Exodus 34:6–7; Hebrews 11:6; Nehemiah 9:32–33.[^18]

#### Paragraph 2 – God's Relation to Creation: Who God Is for Us

Whereas paragraph 1 focuses on God's intrinsic attributes, paragraph 2 addresses God's extrinsic relations—how the self-sufficient God relates to the world He made. The confession identifies five dimensions of this relationship:

**1. Self-Sufficient Independence:** "God, having all life, glory, goodness, blessedness, in and of himself, is alone in and unto himself all-sufficient, not standing in need of any creature which he hath made, nor deriving any glory from them, but only manifesting his own glory in, by, unto, and upon them."[^19] This echoes Paul's sermon at the Areopagus: "He is not served by human hands, as though he needed anything, since he himself gives to all mankind life and breath and everything" (Acts 17:25).[^20] The confession rejects the notion that God created because He was lonely or incomplete—a sentiment sometimes (and wrongly) expressed in Sunday school lessons.[^21]

**2. Fountain of All Being:** "He is the alone fountain of all being, of whom, through whom, and to whom are all things."[^22] This trinitarian formula echoes Romans 11:36: "For from him and through him and to him are all things."[^23] God is not merely the originator of creation (*ek tou*) but its sustaining principle (*di' autou*) and ultimate goal (*eis auton*).

**3. Sovereign Dominion:** "He hath most sovereign dominion over all creatures, to do by them, for them, or upon them whatsoever himself pleaseth."[^24] The confession grounds dominion in creation: because God is the Maker, He is the Owner. Psalm 115:3 declares, "Our God is in the heavens; he does all that he pleases."[^25]

**4. Infinite and Infallible Knowledge:** "In his sight all things are open and manifest, his knowledge is infinite, infallible, and independent upon the creature, so as nothing is to him contingent or uncertain."[^26] This affirms divine omniscience against any form of "open theism" that would limit God's knowledge of the future.[^27] The confession's language echoes Hebrews 4:13: "No creature is hidden from his sight, but all are naked and exposed to the eyes of him to whom we must give account."[^28]

**5. Intrinsic Claims on Creatures:** "He is most holy in all his counsels, in all his works, and in all his commands... To him is due from angels and men whatsoever worship, service, or obedience as creatures they owe unto the creator."[^29] Because God is holy, He is worthy. Because He is Creator, He is owed worship. The confession ties ontology to obligation: God's being grounds God's claim.[^30]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Acts 17:24–25; Romans 11:36; Psalm 115:3; Hebrews 4:13; Ezekiel 11:5; Acts 15:18; Psalm 145:17; Revelation 5:12–14.[^31]

---

#### Paragraph 3 – The Holy Trinity: Who God Is as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit

Having established the unity of the divine essence and the glory of God's attributes, the confession now unfolds the mystery of the Trinity: one God in three persons. "In this divine and infinite Being there are three subsistences, the Father, the Word or Son, and Holy Spirit, of one substance, power, and eternity, each having the whole divine essence, yet the essence undivided."[^32]

**Terminological Precision:** The confession employs careful terminology:

- **Subsistence** (Latin *subsistentia*): a distinct mode of existence within the Godhead, not a separate essence.
- **Substance** (Latin *substantia*): the one undivided divine essence shared fully by all three persons.[^33]

The confession affirms that these three "are distinguished by their personal properties."[^34] That is, the persons are not different *kinds* of being but distinct *ways* of being the one God:

- The Father is "of none" (unbegotten, uncaused)
- The Son is "eternally begotten of the Father" (John 1:14, 18)
- The Holy Spirit is "proceeding from the Father and the Son" (John 15:26)[^35]

**Full Deity of Each Person:** The confession insists that the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God.[^36] This guards against Arianism (denying Christ's full deity) and subordinationism (making the Son or Spirit lesser beings). Waldron emphasizes: "They are God in the maximum meaning of that word. Each has the whole divine essence. This means that each person possesses all the attributes which describe that divine essence."[^37]

**Against Modalism and Tritheism:** The confession rejects two opposite errors: modalism (which collapses the persons into mere "modes" of a single person) and tritheism (which makes the three persons three separate gods).[^38] The language of "one substance, three subsistences" preserves both the unity of God (contra tritheism) and the real personal distinctions (contra modalism).[^39]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** 1 John 5:7; Matthew 28:19; 2 Corinthians 13:14; John 1:14, 18; John 15:26; Galatians 4:6; Hebrews 1:2–3.[^40]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of the Doctrine of God

The confession's doctrine of God rests on several theological pillars, each essential to maintaining biblical orthodoxy.

---

**Pillar 1: Divine Unity (Monotheism)**

The confession begins with "one only living and true God." This is not a bare numerical assertion but a covenantal one. The God of Israel is the only God; all other "gods" are idols (Psalm 96:5). Monotheism is not merely philosophical—it is doxological: "Hear, O Israel, the LORD our God, the LORD is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4) is followed immediately by "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart" (Deuteronomy 6:5).[^41] The unity of God demands the singularity of worship. The confession stands against polytheism (many gods), henotheism (many gods but one supreme), and tritheism (three separate gods).[^42]

---

**Pillar 2: Divine Aseity (Self-Existence)**

Aseity is the logical foundation of all other attributes. Because God exists from Himself, He depends on nothing. This means:

- **No external cause**: God was not created; He simply is.
- **No internal need**: God does not require creation for His happiness or completeness.[^43]

The confession's assertion that God is "alone in and unto himself all-sufficient" means that love, joy, and blessedness are intrinsic to God's nature. The Trinity ensures that God is eternally relational *within Himself*—Father, Son, and Spirit delighting in one another before the foundation of the world.[^44]

---

**Pillar 3: Divine Simplicity**

Divine simplicity—that God is not composed of parts—is an inference from aseity.[^45] If God were composed of parts, the parts would be prior to God and thus more ultimate than God. The confession's language of "without body, parts, or passions" affirms that:

- **Without body**: God is spirit (John 4:24), not extended in space.
- **Without parts**: God's attributes are not separable components but identical with His essence.
- **Without passions**: This does not mean God is unfeeling but that His emotions are not unstable, reactive, or involuntary like human passions.[^46]

---

**Pillar 4: Divine Immutability (Unchangeability)**

"Immutable" means God does not change in His being, attributes, or purposes.[^47] Malachi 3:6 declares, "I the LORD do not change." This guards against:

- **Process theology**: that God evolves or learns.
- **Open theism**: that God changes His mind or does not know the future.[^48]

Immutability does not mean God is static or inactive; rather, His actions in time flow from His unchanging character and eternal decree.

---

**Pillar 5: Divine Sovereignty and Providence**

The confession affirms that God "works all things according to the counsel of his own immutable and most righteous will." This is not fatalism (which denies human responsibility) nor deism (which denies God's ongoing involvement). Rather, it is biblical compatibilism: God sovereignly ordains all things while humans remain genuinely responsible for their choices.[^49]

The confession's language of God's "most absolute" character echoes the Reformed emphasis on divine freedom. God is "most free"—not constrained by anything outside Himself. He acts according to His own righteous will, not capriciously but perfectly.

---

**Pillar 6: The Creator-Creature Distinction**

The confession's first two paragraphs jointly establish the absolute distinction between God and creation.[^50] God is "infinite, eternal, immutable"; creation is finite, temporal, mutable. This distinction is the non-negotiable foundation of biblical religion. To collapse it (as in pantheism, which makes God identical with creation) or to erode it (as in panentheism, which makes God dependent on creation) is to lose the God of the Bible.[^51]

---

**Pillar 7: The Doctrine of the Trinity**

The Trinity is the distinctive Christian doctrine of God.[^52] The confession's phrasing—"three subsistences, of one substance"—echoes the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed (AD 381). Key implications:

- **The Father is God, the Son is God, the Spirit is God** (against Arianism).[^53]
- **The three are not three gods** (against tritheism).[^54]
- **The three are distinguished by personal properties** (eternal generation for the Son, procession for the Spirit).
- **The divine essence is undivided** (against partialism, which would assign one-third of the essence to each person).[^55]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Diminished View of God

The confession's majestic portrait of God is not merely theoretical; it diagnoses several pastoral crises in contemporary Christianity.[^56]

---

**Crisis 1: The Loss of the Fear of God**

Modern evangelicalism has often domesticated God, emphasizing His love while minimizing His holiness.[^57] The confession's language—"most terrible in his judgments, hating all sin, and who will by no means clear the guilty"—is difficult for contemporary ears. But without the fear of the Lord, worship becomes entertainment, prayer becomes wish-list recitation, and sin becomes a minor inconvenience.[^58]

Pastorally, the loss of the fear of God leads to:

- **Casual worship**: treating Sunday mornings as performance rather than encounter.
- **Presumptuous sin**: assuming God will forgive without genuine repentance.
- **Shallow discipleship**: pursuing self-improvement rather than transformation by the living God.[^59]

The cure is not to abandon God's love but to recover His holiness. The same Exodus 34 passage that declares God's mercy also declares that He "will by no means clear the guilty."[^60]

---

**Crisis 2: Anthropomorphism and the "Buddy Jesus"**

When the confession insists that God is "without body, parts, or passions," it confronts the tendency to remake God in our own image.[^61] The contemporary church often speaks of God as if He were a larger, kinder human being—a "cosmic cheerleader" rather than the consuming fire of Hebrews 12:29.[^62]

Pastoral symptoms include:

- **Prayer addressed to a "buddy" rather than the King of kings**.
- **Worship songs that focus almost exclusively on God's utility to us** ("What can you do for me?" rather than "Worthy are You, O Lord").
- **Theological language that flattens divine transcendence**.[^63]

---

**Crisis 3: Open Theism and Denial of Divine Omniscience**

Some contemporary theologians, seeking to protect human freedom or excuse God from evil, have denied that God knows the future exhaustively.[^64] The confession's affirmation—"his knowledge is infinite, infallible, and independent upon the creature, so as nothing is to him contingent or uncertain"—directly confronts this error.[^65]

Pastoral implications of open theism include:

- **Loss of assurance**: If God does not know the future, His promises are uncertain.
- **Loss of comfort**: If God can be surprised, He cannot be trusted in times of crisis.[^66]
- **Loss of worship**: A God who learns is not the God of Isaiah 46:10, who "declares the end from the beginning."[^67]

---

**Crisis 4: Practical Atheism in the Believer's Heart**

The confession describes a God who is "all-sufficient, not standing in need of any creature." Yet believers often live as practical atheists—trusting in money, health, reputation, or relationships rather than in God alone.[^68]

Wilhelmus à Brakel observed: "Many confess God with their mouths but deny Him with their deeds. They are afraid of everything except God; they trust in everything except God."[^69] The remedy is not more information about God but a renewed dependence on God as He is revealed in Scripture.

---

**Crisis 5: Tritheistic or Modalist Tendencies in Worship and Prayer**

Even among orthodox believers, Trinitarian piety can go awry. Some pray almost exclusively to Jesus (in effect treating the Father as remote or irrelevant). Others pray to "God" generically, without conscious address to the Father through the Son in the Spirit.[^70]

The confession's careful Trinitarian formulation invites believers to pray as Jesus taught: "Our Father in heaven" (Matthew 6:9), in the name of the Son (John 14:13–14), by the power of the Spirit (Romans 8:26–27).[^71]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Doctrine of God

The confession's doctrine of God is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are seven practical steps derived from Chapter 2.[^72]

---

**Step 1: Worship with Awe and Reverence**

Because God is "most terrible in his judgments" and "dwells in the light which no man can approach unto," our worship must reflect holy fear.[^73] This does not mean cowering but bowing—recognizing our place before the King.

- **In corporate worship**: Guard against entertainment-driven services. Prioritize silence, Scripture reading, and the reverent singing of psalms and hymns.
- **In private worship**: Begin each quiet time with a moment of silence, remembering the One before whom you stand.[^74]

---

**Step 2: Pray Trinitarianly**

Use the confession's Trinitarian grammar to shape your prayers. Address the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit.[^75]

- **Model prayer**:
  "O Father, I thank You for adopting me in Christ. Lord Jesus, I ask this in Your name, for You have purchased every blessing. Holy Spirit, help my weakness as I pray."[^76]

---

**Step 3: Trust God's Sovereignty in Suffering**

Because God "works all things according to the counsel of his own immutable and most righteous will," nothing enters your life outside His control.[^77]

- **In affliction**: Say with Job, "The Lord gave, and the Lord has taken away; blessed be the name of the Lord" (Job 1:21). Not fatalistic resignation, but trust in a sovereign, good, and holy God.[^78]

---

**Step 4: Confess Your Dependence Daily**

Because God is "alone in and unto himself all-sufficient, not standing in need of any creature," you are the needy one.[^79]

- **Morning confession**:
  "I begin this day in utter dependence. I cannot breathe without You. I cannot love without You. I cannot serve without You. I am Your creature; You are my Creator."

---

**Step 5: Reject All Forms of Idolatry**

Because "the Lord our God is but one only living and true God," He alone is worthy of ultimate loyalty.[^80] Identify your functional gods—money, approval, comfort, achievement—and repent of them.

- **Diagnostic question**: What do you most fear losing? What do you most hope for? What do you turn to for comfort? These are your functional gods.[^81]

---

**Step 6: Study God's Attributes Meditatively**

The confession lists over eighteen attributes. Do not rush past them. Take one attribute per week for meditation.

- **Example**: Meditate on God's holiness. Read Isaiah 6. Consider what it means that God is "separate" from all sin. Pray through the implications for your life.[^82]

---

**Step 7: Preach God's Majesty from the Pulpit and in the Home**

Pastors and parents must resist the temptation to "make God accessible" by shrinking Him.[^83]

- **For pastors**: Preach the full-orbed biblical portrait: God's mercy *and* His justice, His love *and* His holiness, His nearness *and* His transcendence.
- **For parents**: Teach your children the "great unlike"—a God who is not like anything in creation. Use the catechism to implant Trinitarian grammar early.[^84]

### Conclusion: The God We Must Know

Chapter 2 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the church's lifeline.[^85] A diminished God yields diminished worship, diminished hope, and diminished holiness. But a right view of God—as He has revealed Himself in Scripture—produces doxology, dependence, and courage.

The confession invites us to confess with the psalmist: "Such knowledge is too wonderful for me; it is high; I cannot attain it" (Psalm 139:6)—and yet to seek it still. We will never fully comprehend the infinite God. But we can know Him truly, love Him deeply, and trust Him completely.

As John Owen wrote: "To know God is life eternal. To know Him as He has revealed Himself in Christ is the highest exercise of our minds in this world. And to know Him as we shall be known is our eternal blessedness."[^86]

**A Prayer from Chapter 2** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Lord our God, who are but one only living and true God: We confess that we have made You small in our imaginations. We have spoken of You casually. We have prayed to You carelessly. We have worshiped You as if You were like us. Forgive us.*

*You are infinite in being and perfection—we are dust. You are a most pure spirit, invisible—we are bound by flesh. You are immutable—we are changeable. You are most holy—we are defiled. You are most just—we are guilty.*

*Yet You are also most loving, gracious, merciful, and abundant in goodness and truth. You forgive iniquity, transgression, and sin. You reward those who diligently seek You.*

*Teach us to tremble and to trust. Teach us to bow and to draw near. Teach us to fear Your majesty and to rest in Your mercy.*

*Father, we come through the Son, by the Spirit. Amen.*[^87]
