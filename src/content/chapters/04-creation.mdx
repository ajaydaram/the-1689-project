# The Foundation of All Life: Why the Doctrine of Creation Is the Beginning of Wisdom

(1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 4 – With Complete Footnotes)

## Introduction: The Necessary Beginning

Chapter 4 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Creation," serves as the theological bridge between the eternal decree of God (Chapter 3) and the unfolding of that decree in time and space.[^1] If Chapter 3 declares what God has purposed from eternity, Chapter 4 announces the first mighty act by which He executes that purpose: the creation of all things out of nothing.[^2] It is the doctrine upon which everything else depends, for without a Creator, there can be no fall, no covenant, no Redeemer, and no redemption.[^3]

The chapter is brief but remarkably dense, comprising just three paragraphs. Yet within these few lines, the confession compresses the whole scope of biblical teaching on the origin of all things: the Trinitarian nature of the creative act, the ex nihilo character of creation, the six‑day framework, the original goodness of all that God made, and—at the apex of the creative week—the unique creation of humanity as male and female, bearing the image of God, endowed with knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness, placed under a covenant of works with the promise of life and the threat of death.[^4]

The arrangement is deliberate: paragraph 1 treats the creation of the universe; paragraph 2 treats the creation of humanity as the crown of creation; paragraph 3 treats the positive law given to our first parents as the test of their obedience.[^5]

## Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Creation

Chapter 4 is woven from the threads of Scripture, each clause anchored in explicit biblical warrant. The compilers of the confession—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—deliberately anchored every assertion in the Word of God, refusing to speculate beyond what is written.[^6]

### Paragraph 1 – The Creation of the Universe: Ex Nihilo, Ex Trinitate, Ex Pulchritudine

The confession opens with words that echo the opening of Genesis itself:

<ConfessionBox>
"In the beginning it pleased God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, for the manifestation of the glory of His eternal power, wisdom, and goodness, to create or make the world, and all things therein, whether visible or invisible, in the space of six days, and all very good."[^7]
</ConfessionBox>

This single sentence contains seven distinct affirmations about the nature of creation.[^8]

1. The Temporal Beginning: "In the beginning" (Genesis 1:1) marks the moment when time itself began.[^9] The confession thereby rejects the eternity of matter (a Greek philosophical error) and affirms that the universe had a definite starting point.[^10] As Herman Bavinck observes, "The confession of creation is the confession that the world is not eternal but had a beginning; it is not divine but created; it is not self-sufficient but dependent on God."[^11]

2. The Trinitarian Agent: The confession specifically names "God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit" as the agent of creation.[^12] This is a deliberate and polemical Trinitarianism, grounding the doctrine of creation in the full deity of the Son and the Spirit. The proof texts are carefully chosen: John 1:2‑3 ("All things were made through him"), Hebrews 1:2 ("through whom also he created the world"), and Job 26:13 ("By his Spirit the heavens were made beautiful").[^13] The confession thus rejects both Arianism (which would make the Son a creature) and any form of subordinationism that would exclude the Spirit from the creative act.[^14] The Father is the fountain, the Son is the agent, and the Spirit is the perfecting power—all three Persons of the Godhead actively involved in the one work of creation.[^15]

3. The Doxological Purpose: The creation exists "for the manifestation of the glory of His eternal power, wisdom, and goodness."[^16] This is perhaps the most theologically significant clause in the paragraph. The universe is not the product of divine need, loneliness, or compulsion. God created freely, out of His own good pleasure, for His own glory.[^17] Romans 1:20 declares that God's "eternal power and divine nature" are clearly perceived in what has been made, and the confession echoes this Pauline doxology.[^18] As John Calvin wrote, "The world is a mirror in which we may contemplate God."[^19]

4. The Extent of Creation: "The world, and all things therein, whether visible or invisible."[^20] This clause encompasses the entire created order: the material universe (visible) and the spiritual realm of angels and celestial beings (invisible). The confession cites Colossians 1:16, which declares that "by him all things were created, in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities."[^21] The confession thereby excludes any dualism that would posit an uncreated spiritual realm alongside the material creation.[^22]

5. The Mode of Creation: The phrase "to create or make" reflects the Hebrew distinction between bara (creating out of nothing) and asah (making out of pre‑existing material).[^23] The confession's compilers intend to affirm creatio ex nihilo—creation out of nothing, without the use of pre‑existing matter.[^24] This distinguishes the Christian doctrine from all forms of dualism, pantheism, and emanationism.[^25] God did not fashion the world from pre‑existent chaos or from His own substance; He spoke, and it stood fast (Psalm 33:9).[^26]

6. The Duration of Creation: "In the space of six days."[^27] The confession takes the Genesis account as literal, chronological history—not myth, not allegory, not a literary framework. The creation week consisted of six ordinary days, each with an evening and a morning (Genesis 1:5, 8, 13, 19, 23, 31).[^28] This affirmation, shared by the Reformed orthodox, stands against theistic evolution and long‑age interpretations that would collapse the distinction between divine creation and naturalistic development.[^29] As Waldron explains, "The confession affirms that the world was created in the space of six days, a phrase which undoubtedly means six literal days."[^30]

7. The Result of Creation: "And all very good."[^31] The confession cites Genesis 1:31, where God surveys His completed work and pronounces it "very good" (tov me'od).[^32] This affirmation guards against any form of dualism that would locate evil in the material creation.[^33] The goodness of creation is not merely moral but ontological: creation is good because it is the work of a good God, because it reflects His glory, and because it serves His purposes.[^34]

### Paragraph 2 – The Creation of Humanity: The Image of God

Having established the creation of the universe, the confession now turns to its crown and climax: the creation of human beings. Paragraph 2 states:

<ConfessionBox>
"After God had made all other creatures, He created man, male and female, with reasonable and immortal souls, rendering them fit unto that life to God for which they were created; being made after the image of God, in knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness; having the law of God written in their hearts, and power to fulfill it, and yet under a possibility of transgressing, being left to the liberty of their own will, which was subject to change."[^36]
</ConfessionBox>

1. The Chronological Priority of Humanity: "After God had made all other creatures."[^37] Humanity is not an afterthought but the intentional apex of creation. The creation of Adam and Eve on the sixth day, after the animals and the plants, indicates that humanity is not an evolutionary development from lower life forms but a distinct, special act of divine creation (Genesis 2:7).[^38]

2. The Duality of the Sexes: "He created man, male and female."[^39] The confession explicitly affirms the creation of both sexes as part of the original creative order. This is not an incidental detail but a foundational affirmation for biblical anthropology, marriage, and sexuality. The proof text, Genesis 1:27, declares that God created them "male and female."[^40] This affirmation, in the context of the confession, stands against all forms of Gnostic dualism that would denigrate the body or the sexual distinction as somehow inferior or fallen.[^41]

3. The Nature of the Human Soul: "With reasonable and immortal souls."[^42] This clause summarizes the biblical doctrine of the soul: it is rational (capable of reason, choice, and moral judgment) and immortal (not subject to death or dissolution). The confession cites Genesis 2:7, where God breathes into Adam's nostrils the "breath of life," and man becomes a "living soul" (nephesh chayyah).[^43] The immortality of the soul is not, however, an inherent property of the soul itself (only God has immortality in Himself, 1 Timothy 6:16), but a gift of God's sustaining grace.[^44] The soul does not cease to exist at bodily death; it continues in conscious existence, awaiting the resurrection (LBCF 31.1).[^45]

4. The Teleology of Human Existence: "Rendering them fit unto that life to God for which they were created."[^46] Humanity was created for a purpose: a life lived unto God. The confession thereby grounds human teleology in divine creation. Our purpose is not self-defined; it is given by our Maker.[^47] As the Westminster Shorter Catechism famously declares, "Man's chief end is to glorify God and to enjoy Him forever."[^48]

5. The Image of God: "Being made after the image of God, in knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness."[^49] This is the most theologically dense clause in the paragraph. The confession adopts the classic Reformed definition of the image of God, drawing on Ephesians 4:24 ("the new self, created after the likeness of God in true righteousness and holiness") and Colossians 3:10 ("the new self, which is being renewed in knowledge after the image of its creator").[^50]

The Reformed tradition has distinguished between the image and the likeness of God (terms used interchangeably in Scripture), and has identified three elements of the image:[^51]

Knowledge: the intellectual capacity to know God, oneself, and the world rightly. This is the mind's apprehension of truth.
Righteousness: the moral capacity to act in accordance with God's law, in right relation to God, others, and creation.
True Holiness: the disposition of the heart toward God—pure, devoted, and undefiled.
These three correspond to the three faculties of the soul: the mind (knowledge), the will (righteousness), and the affections (holiness).[^52] The confession explicitly grounds the image in these moral and spiritual qualities rather than in any physical or biological property.[^53] As Waldron notes, "The image of God in man is not something that can be lost without destroying his humanity. It can, however, be corrupted and defaced."[^54]

6. The Law Written on the Heart: "Having the law of God written in their hearts."[^55] The confession cites Romans 2:14‑15, where Paul speaks of the Gentiles who "show the work of the law written in their hearts."[^56] This is the natural law, the moral order built into the fabric of creation and inscribed on the human conscience. Adam, even before the positive command not to eat of the tree, knew the moral law of God—to love God with all his being and to love his neighbor as himself (Romans 2:14).[^57] This natural law is not a second, independent source of revelation but the same moral law of God given in creation, which the Ten Commandments later codify for a fallen people.[^58]

7. The Power and the Possibility: "And power to fulfill it, and yet under a possibility of transgressing, being left to the liberty of their own will, which was subject to change."[^59] This clause is the confession's concise summary of the original state of man—the state of integrity (status integritatis) before the fall.[^60]

"Power to fulfill it": Adam was not created neutral or weak. He possessed the moral capacity to obey the law of God perfectly.[^61]
"Possibility of transgressing": Adam was posse peccare (able to sin) but not yet non posse peccare (not able to sin). The possibility of sin was real, though not necessary.[^62]
"Liberty of their own will, which was subject to change": Adam's will was free—not from God's decree, but from external compulsion. He was free to choose obedience or disobedience. Yet that freedom was not immutable; Adam could and would fall.[^63]
This is the classic Reformed distinction between the fourfold state of man: (1) created able to sin and able not to sin; (2) fallen able only to sin; (3) regenerated able to sin and able not to sin; (4) glorified not able to sin.[^64] Adam, in his original creation, possessed the freedom and power to obey, but not the immutability that belongs only to the redeemed in glory.[^65]

### Paragraph 3 – The Positive Law: The Test of Obedience

The confession concludes with the specific command given to Adam as the test of his obedience:

<ConfessionBox>
"Besides the law written in their hearts, they received a command not to eat of the tree of knowledge of good and evil, which while they kept, they were happy in their communion with God, and had dominion over the creatures."[^67]
</ConfessionBox>

1. The Positive Command: "Besides the law written in their hearts."[^68] The moral law (the natural law) was inherent to Adam's created nature. But God added a positive law—a command that had no intrinsic moral content but served as a test of obedience. The command not to eat of the tree was not inherently good or evil; it became the occasion for sin only when Adam disobeyed.[^69] This positive law functioned as a sacrament of the covenant of works: obedience would confirm Adam in righteousness and lead to eternal life; disobedience would bring death (Genesis 2:17).[^70]

2. The Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil: The confession cites Genesis 2:17. The name of the tree indicates its function: by eating, Adam would come to know good and evil experientially—not merely as abstract concepts, but as a reality lived. Good would be known through its loss; evil would be known through its painful consequence.[^71]

3. The Blessing of Obedience: "Which while they kept, they were happy in their communion with God, and had dominion over the creatures."[^72] Adam's original state was one of blessedness, fellowship, and authority. His communion with God was unbroken; his dominion over creation was unchallenged. The "very good" of creation included humanity's relationship with God and with the created order.[^73]

## Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Robust Doctrine of Creation

The confession's doctrine of creation rests on several interconnected theological pillars, each essential to maintaining biblical orthodoxy against the errors of ancient and modern speculation.[^75]

#### Pillar 1: Creatio Ex Nihilo (Creation Out of Nothing)

The affirmation that God created "the world, and all things therein" without the use of pre‑existing matter is the theological non‑negotiable of Christian doctrine.[^76] It distinguishes the biblical teaching from all forms of:

Dualism: the view that matter is eternal and co‑existent with God (Platonism, Gnosticism, Mormonism).[^77]
Pantheism: the view that the world is an emanation or extension of God's own being (Spinozism, some forms of Eastern mysticism).[^78]
Panentheism: the view that the world is contained within God and depends on Him, but is not distinct from Him (process theology, some forms of liberal Protestantism).[^79]
Creatio ex nihilo establishes the absolute distinction between Creator and creature—a distinction that is the precondition for any biblical understanding of sin, grace, and redemption.[^80]

#### Pillar 2: The Trinity as the Agent of Creation

The confession's explicit naming of "God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit" as the agent of creation is a deliberate anti‑Arian and anti‑Socinian affirmation.[^81] The Son is not a creature, and the Spirit is not a mere power or energy. All three Persons of the Godhead are actively involved in the one work of creation, distinguished only by their personal properties:[^82]

The Father is the fountain and source of all being.
The Son is the agent or mediator through whom all things are made (John 1:3; Colossians 1:16).
The Spirit is the perfecter, the one who brings beauty and order to creation (Genesis 1:2; Job 26:13).[^83]
This Trinitarian theology of creation has profound implications for worship: we do not worship a solitary monad but the triune God who created all things through His Son and by His Spirit.[^84]

#### Pillar 3: The Goodness of Creation

The confession's affirmation that all things were created "very good" stands against every form of Gnosticism, Manichaeism, and ascetic dualism that would denigrate the material world as inherently evil or inferior.[^85] The goodness of creation means:

The body is good: not a prison for the soul but the instrument of righteous living.[^86]
Marriage and sexuality are good: not a concession to sin but a creation ordinance (Genesis 2:24).[^87]
Work is good: not a post‑fall curse but a creational mandate (Genesis 2:15).[^88]
The physical world is good: not an illusion to be escaped but a theater of God's glory to be enjoyed and stewarded.[^89]
The fall corrupted creation but did not obliterate its fundamental goodness. Redemption, therefore, is not the escape from the material world but the renewal of it (Romans 8:18‑25).[^90]

#### Pillar 4: The Image of God as Relational, Not Substantial

The confession's definition of the image of God as consisting in "knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness" locates the image in the moral and spiritual faculties of the soul, not in any physical or biological property.[^91] This guards against two errors:

The identification of the image with the body: The image is not located in our physical form (though our bodiliness is part of our humanity), but in our capacity to know, will, and love as God knows, wills, and loves.[^92]
The identification of the image with rationality alone: Reason is essential, but it is not sufficient; the image also includes righteousness and holiness—moral and spiritual qualities.[^93]
The image of God is also relational: humanity is created for fellowship with God (vertical) and for community with other image‑bearers (horizontal). The confession's affirmation of male and female as equally bearing the image (Genesis 1:27) grounds the biblical doctrine of human dignity and equality.[^94]

#### Pillar 5: The Covenant of Works

The confession's third paragraph, though brief, is the confession's foundation for the covenant of works that Adam entered into with God.[^95] The positive law—the command not to eat of the tree—was not arbitrary but sacramental. It was the test of Adam's obedience in the covenant of life. The Reformed tradition has historically identified this arrangement as a covenant of works:[^96]

The parties: God the Creator and Adam the representative head of humanity.
The condition: Perfect, personal, and perpetual obedience.
The promise: Eternal life and confirmation in righteousness.
The penalty: Death—physical, spiritual, and eternal.[^97]
This covenant is essential to the gospel: Christ, the second Adam, fulfilled the covenant of works where the first Adam failed, earning the righteousness that is imputed to believers (Romans 5:12‑21).[^98]

#### Pillar 6: The Original State of Integrity

The confession describes Adam's original condition in terms that later Reformed theology would systematize as the status integritatis (state of integrity).[^99] Adam was:

Posse peccare, posse non peccare (able to sin, able not to sin): He had the power to obey but not the immutability to guarantee that obedience.[^100]
Not yet confirmed in righteousness: The tree of life (not mentioned in the confession but implied in Genesis 2:9 and 3:22) would have been the means by which Adam would have been confirmed in his holy state, had he obeyed.[^101]
Subject to change: Adam's will was mutable; he could and did fall.[^102]
This doctrine guards against two errors: Pelagianism (which denies the fall's effect on human nature) and fatalism (which would make the fall inevitable by divine decree).[^103] Adam fell freely, not because God forced him, but because he chose to disobey.[^104]

## Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Diminished View of Creation

The confession's doctrine of creation is not a speculative cosmology; it is a pastoral necessity. Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by this chapter.[^105]

#### Crisis 1: The Loss of Human Dignity in an Age of Materialism

If humanity is merely the product of blind evolutionary processes, then human dignity is a social construction—a useful fiction but not a metaphysical reality.[^106] The confession's affirmation that man was created "after the image of God, in knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness" declares that every human being, regardless of ability, race, or status, possesses an inherent dignity that cannot be earned or stripped away.[^107] This is the biblical foundation for:

The sanctity of human life from conception to natural death (Psalm 139:13‑16).[^108]
The condemnation of racism, since all humans bear the image of God regardless of ethnicity (Genesis 1:27).[^109]
The basis for human rights in a culture that grounds them in shifting political consensus rather than fixed divine creation.[^110]

#### Crisis 2: The Gnostic Denigration of the Body and Materiality

Much of contemporary spirituality, even within evangelicalism, retains a Gnostic impulse: the body is seen as a hindrance, sexuality is viewed with suspicion, and the physical world is treated as a disposable backdrop to "spiritual" pursuits.[^111] The confession's affirmation that creation is "very good" and that humanity was created "male and female" (not merely as disembodied souls) is a powerful corrective.[^112]

Pastoral symptoms include:

Ascetic legalism that treats bodily pleasures (food, drink, sex within marriage) as suspect.[^113]
Imbalanced eschatology that longs for the escape of the soul from the body rather than the resurrection of the body.[^114]
Neglect of environmental stewardship as if the material world were irrelevant to God's purposes.[^115]
The confession calls us to affirm the goodness of the body, the goodness of creation, and the hope of a new heavens and a new earth—not the annihilation of the material order (LBCF 32.1‑3).[^116]

#### Crisis 3: The Erosion of the Doctrine of the Image of God

The modern world has largely abandoned the biblical doctrine of the image of God, replacing it with various idols: the autonomous self, the therapeutic self, the consumer self.[^117] The result is a culture of:

Radical individualism: I define my own identity, values, and purpose.[^118]
Transgender ideology: The body is merely a vessel for a self‑defined "gender identity," denying the creational distinction of male and female that the confession affirms.[^119]
Euthanasia and abortion: Human life is disposable when it no longer meets a certain standard of quality or desire.[^120]
The confession's affirmation that humanity was created "male and female" in the image of God is a direct challenge to these cultural idols. Our identity is not self‑created; it is given by our Creator. Sexual distinction is not an accident of evolution but a creational ordinance. And every human life, from conception to death, bears the image of God and is therefore worthy of protection and dignity.[^121]

#### Crisis 4: The Collapse of Moral Absolutes

If humanity is not created with "the law of God written in their hearts," then morality is a human invention—subject to revision, renegotiation, and rejection.[^122] The confession's affirmation of natural law—the moral order inscribed on the human conscience—stands against:

Moral relativism: The claim that right and wrong are matters of personal or cultural preference.[^123]
Positivism: The claim that law is merely the command of the sovereign, with no transcendent grounding.[^124]
Situation ethics: The claim that moral rules are guidelines that can be suspended when love requires.[^125]
The confession does not ground morality in the decree of God alone (which would make it arbitrary) or in human reason alone (which would make it fallible). It grounds morality in the created order itself—in the nature God has given us and in the law He has written on our hearts.[^126]

#### Crisis 5: The Loss of Purpose and Teleology

When young people are taught that they are the products of random, purposeless processes, it is no surprise that many experience existential despair, anxiety, and a sense of meaninglessness.[^127] The confession's declaration that God created man "rendering them fit unto that life to God for which they were created" provides a teleology—a purpose and direction for human existence.[^128]

Our purpose is not self‑defined. It is not to maximize pleasure, accumulate wealth, or achieve fame. Our purpose is to live "unto God"—to know Him, to love Him, to serve Him, and to enjoy Him forever (Psalm 73:25‑26).[^129] This is the only foundation for a life of meaning and joy.

## Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Creation

The confession's doctrine of creation is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are seven practical steps derived from Chapter 4.[^130]

#### Step 1: Worship the Triune Creator

Because God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit created all things, our worship must be explicitly Trinitarian. We do not worship a generic "higher power" or an impersonal "force." We worship the Father who spoke the world into being, the Son through whom all things were made, and the Spirit who brings beauty and order to creation.[^131]

In corporate worship: Sing hymns that explicitly praise the Trinity as Creator (e.g., "Holy, Holy, Holy," "All Creatures of Our God and King").[^132]
In private worship: Begin each day by acknowledging the Trinity: "Father, I thank You for creating me. Son, I praise You for redeeming me. Spirit, I depend on You for sustaining me."[^133]

#### Step 2: Affirm the Goodness of the Body and Creation

Reject all forms of Gnostic dualism that would denigrate the body or the material world. Treat your body as a gift from God, to be cared for and used for His glory—not as a prison to be escaped.[^134]

In daily life: Eat, drink, and enjoy the goodness of creation with thanksgiving (1 Timothy 4:4‑5). Exercise. Sleep. Laugh. Receive God's good gifts without guilt.[^135]
In eschatology: Long for the resurrection of the body, not the escape of the soul. The Christian hope is not disembodied bliss but a new heavens and a new earth, with resurrected bodies (LBCF 32.1‑3).[^136]

#### Step 3: See Every Human Being as an Image‑Bearer

Because every human being is made in the image of God, treat all people—regardless of race, class, ability, or moral condition—with dignity, respect, and compassion.[^137]

In the pro‑life context: Defend the unborn as image‑bearers from the moment of conception (Psalm 139:13‑16).[^138]
In racial reconciliation: Reject all forms of racism as a denial of the image of God (Genesis 1:27).[^139]
In evangelism: Preach the gospel to all people, because all are made in God's image, corrupted by sin, and redeemable in Christ.[^140]

#### Step 4: Live as a Steward, Not a Consumer

Because God created the world and gave humanity dominion over it, our relationship to creation is one of stewardship, not exploitation.[^141]

In daily life: Reduce waste, recycle, and care for the environment—not as an end in itself, but as an act of obedience to the Creator (Genesis 2:15).[^142]
In economics: Resist the consumerist mentality that treats creation as a disposable commodity.[^143]

#### Step 5: Reject the Idolatry of the Self

Because our identity and purpose are given by our Creator, we are not free to define ourselves according to our own desires.[^144]

In gender and sexuality: Affirm the biblical teaching that God created humanity "male and female." Reject the cultural claim that gender is a social construct or that sexual identity is a matter of personal choice.[^145]
In vocation: Seek to live "unto God" in whatever work you do (Colossians 3:23).[^146]

#### Step 6: Teach the Doctrine of Creation to the Next Generation

The doctrine of creation is under constant assault in public education, popular media, and even some Christian institutions. Parents, pastors, and teachers must intentionally equip the next generation to defend and delight in the biblical teaching of creation.[^147]

For parents: Read Genesis 1‑2 with your children. Explain that God created the world in six days. Teach them that they are made in God's image.[^148]
For pastors: Preach through Genesis 1‑2. Do not avoid the difficult questions. Equip your congregation to answer the claims of evolution and materialism.[^149]

#### Step 7: Rest in the Goodness of Your Creation

If you struggle with shame about your body, your abilities, or your identity, return to the confession's affirmation: you were created "very good" by a good God. The fall has corrupted you, but it has not obliterated the image of God. You are not an accident. You are not a mistake. You are a creature of the living God, made for fellowship with Him. Rest in that truth.[^150]

## Conclusion: The Beginning of Wisdom

Chapter 4 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth‑century cosmology. It is the church's lifeline to the foundational truth upon which all other truths depend.[^151] If the universe is not created, then there is no Creator; if there is no Creator, there is no moral law; if there is no moral law, there is no sin; if there is no sin, there is no need of a Savior; if there is no Savior, there is no hope.[^152]

But because the confession's first words in this chapter are true—"In the beginning it pleased God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit to create or make the world, and all things therein"—the rest of the Christian faith stands on solid ground. We have a Creator. We have a purpose. We have a Redeemer who is the second Adam, who came to undo what the first Adam undid (Romans 5:12‑21).[^153]

And so the confession calls us not merely to believe these truths but to live them: to worship the triune Creator, to affirm the goodness of His creation, to honor the image of God in every human being, to live as stewards rather than consumers, to reject the idolatry of the self, to teach the next generation, and to rest in the goodness of our own creation. This is the beginning of wisdom (Psalm 111:10). This is the foundation of joy. This is the hope of the world.[^154]

## A Prayer from Chapter 4 (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

O Triune God—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit: We praise You as the Creator of all things. In the beginning, You spoke, and the heavens were made. You commanded, and the earth stood fast. You formed us from the dust of the ground and breathed into our nostrils the breath of life. You made us male and female, in Your own image, endowed with knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness. You placed us in a garden of delight, gave us dominion over the work of Your hands, and wrote Your law upon our hearts.

We confess that we have not lived as Your image‑bearers. We have denied our own dignity and the dignity of others. We have worshipped the creature rather than the Creator. We have denigrated the body You made good. We have forgotten that we were created to live unto You. Forgive us. Restore in us the image of Your Son. Teach us to live as creatures before You—humble, thankful, and obedient. For Jesus Christ's sake. Amen.[^155]
