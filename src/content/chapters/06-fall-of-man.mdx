# The Universal Tragedy: Why Original Sin Is the Doctrine Only the Gospel Can Answer**

Chapter 6 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Fall of Man, of Sin, and of the Punishment Thereof," stands at the theological crossroads between the goodness of creation (Chapter 4) and the necessity of redemption (Chapters 7-8).[^1] If Chapter 4 declares that humanity was created "very good" in the image of God, and Chapter 5 affirms God's providential governance of all things, Chapter 6 confronts the devastating reality that shattered that goodness: *the fall of our first parents and the consequent corruption of the entire human race.*[^2]

The chapter's six paragraphs unfold like a theological anatomy of sin, moving from the historical event of the fall (paragraph 1) to its immediate effects on Adam and Eve (paragraph 2), to the transmission of sin and guilt to all their posterity (paragraph 3), to the nature of original corruption (paragraph 4), to the persistence of indwelling sin even in believers (paragraph 5), and finally to the comprehensive punishment that sin entails (paragraph 6).[^3]

The arrangement is deliberate and pastoral. The confession does not merely describe sin in abstract terms; it traces sin from its origin to its ultimate consequence, demonstrating that the only remedy for this universal malady is Jesus Christ. As Sam Waldron notes, "This doctrine of our solidarity in Adam explains so much that otherwise would be inexplicable. Why does every human sin without exception? Why are men born sinful and go from the womb speaking lies? Why do even babies die?"[^4] The answer is found in Chapter 6: we are all in Adam, and in Adam we all fell.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Fall and Its Consequences

Chapter 6 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the origin, nature, and transmission of sin. The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—anchored each assertion in the Word of God, refusing to speculate beyond what is written.[^5] The chapter draws heavily on Genesis 3, Romans 5, Psalm 51, and Ephesians 2, weaving these texts into a coherent and devastating portrait of the human condition apart from Christ.

#### Paragraph 1 – The Historical Fall: From Integrity to Transgression

The confession opens with a description of humanity's original state and the tragic event that destroyed it: <ConfessionBox>
**"Although God created man upright and perfect, and gave him a righteous law, which had been unto life had he kept it, and threatened death upon the breach thereof, yet he did not long abide in this honour; Satan using the subtilty of the serpent to seduce Eve, then by her seducing Adam, who, without any compulsion, did wilfully transgress the law of their creation, and the command given to them, in eating the forbidden fruit, which God was pleased, according to his wise and holy counsel to permit, having purposed to order it to his own glory."**[^6]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Original Integrity of Man:** "God created man upright and perfect."[^7] The confession echoes Ecclesiastes 7:29: "God made man upright, but they have sought out many schemes." Adam was not created neutral, weak, or fallen. He possessed original righteousness—a state of moral and spiritual wholeness that enabled him to obey God fully.[^8] This affirmation stands against all forms of Pelagianism, which would deny that Adam had any special endowment of grace, and against all forms of evolutionary naturalism, which would reduce humanity to a product of blind forces.[^9]

**2. The Righteous Law and Its Stakes:** "Gave him a righteous law, which had been unto life had he kept it, and threatened death upon the breach thereof."[^10] The confession cites Genesis 2:16-17, where God commands Adam not to eat of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, with the sanction: "for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." This positive law was not arbitrary but sacramental. It was the test of the covenant of works: obedience would lead to confirmed eternal life; disobedience would bring death—physical, spiritual, and eternal.[^11]

**3. The Brevity of Integrity:** "He did not long abide in this honour."[^12] Adam's state of integrity was real but unstable. He was *posse peccare, posse non peccare* (able to sin, able not to sin) but not yet *non posse peccare* (not able to sin). His will was mutable, and he proved its mutability by falling quickly.[^13]

**4. The Occasion and Manner of the Fall:** "Satan using the subtilty of the serpent to seduce Eve, then by her seducing Adam."[^14] The confession traces the fall to three actors: Satan (the tempter), Eve (the first transgressor), and Adam (the federal head). The serpent's "subtilty" (Genesis 3:1) was its cunning deception—questioning God's word ("Did God actually say?"), denying God's judgment ("You will not surely die"), and slandering God's character ("God knows that when you eat it your eyes will be opened").[^15] Eve was deceived (1 Timothy 2:14); Adam was not deceived but willfully transgressed.[^16]

**5. The Essence of the Fall:** Adam "did wilfully transgress the law of their creation, and the command given to them, in eating the forbidden fruit."[^17] The sin was not merely an act of disobedience but a rejection of God's authority and a grasping after autonomy. Adam chose to believe the serpent's lie rather than God's truth. He chose to become the arbiter of good and evil rather than to trust God's definition.[^18]

**6. The Permission of the Fall:** The confession is careful to locate the fall within God's wise and holy providence: "which God was pleased, according to his wise and holy counsel to permit, having purposed to order it to his own glory."[^19] This clause guards against two errors: (1) the error that God is the author of sin (He is not; He *permitted* it, not caused it) and (2) the error that the fall was outside God's control (it was not; He "purposed to order it to his own glory").[^20] The cross is the ultimate example: the greatest evil—the murder of the Son of God—was permitted and ordered by God for the greatest good—the salvation of the world (Acts 2:23; 4:27-28).[^21]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Immediate Effects of the Fall: Loss of Original Righteousness and Communion

Having described the event of the fall, the confession now addresses its immediate effects on our first parents: <ConfessionBox>
**"Our first parents, by this sin, fell from their original righteousness and communion with God, and we in them whereby death came upon all: all becoming dead in sin, and wholly defiled in all the faculties and parts of soul and body."**[^23]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Loss of Original Righteousness:** "Fell from their original righteousness."[^24] Original righteousness was not merely a passive state of innocence but an active endowment of grace—knowledge, righteousness, and true holiness (LBCF 4.2). In the fall, Adam and Eve lost this endowment. They did not lose their humanity, but they lost the moral and spiritual perfections that made their humanity whole.[^25]

**2. The Loss of Communion with God:** "And communion with God."[^26] Before the fall, Adam and Eve enjoyed unbroken fellowship with their Creator. They walked with God in the garden. After the fall, they hid from His presence (Genesis 3:8). The intimate communion was shattered. Confession 6.2 cites Romans 3:23: "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."[^27]

**3. The Inclusion of All Humanity:** "And we in them."[^28] This brief phrase is the most theologically loaded in the paragraph. The confession affirms that Adam's fall was not merely his own private catastrophe but a disaster that engulfed the entire human race. We were *in Adam*—not merely biologically descended from him, but representatively present with him in his sin. As the old hymn puts it, "We blossom and flourish as leaves on the tree, and wither and perish—but not changeth Thee."[^29]

**4. The Consequence of Death:** "Whereby death came upon all."[^30] Death is not merely physical cessation but the comprehensive disintegration of human life—spiritual death (separation from God), physical death (separation of soul from body), and eternal death (the second death in hell). Romans 5:12 declares: "Therefore, just as sin came into the world world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned."[^31]

**5. The Extent of Defilement:** "All becoming dead in sin, and wholly defiled in all the faculties and parts of soul and body."[^32] The defilement is total—not in the sense that every human being is as evil as they could possibly be, but in the sense that sin has affected *every* part of our being: mind, will, emotions, body. As the confession cites Titus 1:15: "To the defiled and unbelieving, nothing is pure; but both their minds and their consciences are defiled."[^33]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Romans 3:23; Romans 5:12; Titus 1:15; Genesis 6:5; Jeremiah 17:9; Romans 3:10-19.[^34]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Transmission of Sin and Guilt: Imputation and Conveyance

Paragraph 3 is the theological heart of the chapter, explaining *how* Adam's sin becomes the sin of all his descendants. <ConfessionBox>
**"They being the root, and by God's appointment, standing in the room and stead of all mankind, the guilt of the sin was imputed, and corrupted nature conveyed, to all their posterity descending from them by ordinary generation, being now conceived in sin, and by nature children of wrath, the servants of sin, the subjects of death, and all other miseries, spiritual, temporal, and eternal, unless the Lord Jesus set them free."**[^35]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Representative Basis: Adam as Federal Head** "They being the root, and by God's appointment, standing in the room and stead of all mankind."[^36] Adam was not merely the biological ancestor of the human race; he was its covenantal representative. God appointed Adam to stand "in the room and stead" of all his posterity. As Waldron explains, "As our covenant head, Adam sinned representatively. Thus, his sin is accounted as ours and imputed to us."[^37] Paul makes this representative character clear in Romans 5:18: "So then as through one transgression there resulted condemnation to all men."[^38]

**2. The Dual Transmission: Guilt and Corruption** The confession identifies two distinct but inseparable results of Adam's sin that are passed to all his descendants:

- **Imputed Guilt:** "The guilt of the sin was imputed."[^39] The term *imputation* (Latin: *imputatio*) means "to credit to one's account." Adam's sin is legally charged to every human being who descends from him by ordinary generation. We are not merely born with a tendency to sin; we are born *guilty*—liable to punishment—because of what Adam did.[^40] This is the doctrine of *original guilt*, distinguished from *original corruption*.

- **Conveyed Corruption:** "And corrupted nature conveyed."[^41] In addition to the legal imputation of guilt, Adam's fallen nature is transmitted to his descendants. We inherit from Adam a nature that is inclined toward evil, averse to good, and corrupted in every faculty. This is the doctrine of *original corruption* (or *hereditary depravity*).[^42]

**3. The Exclusion of Christ** The phrase "descending from them by ordinary generation" explicitly excludes the Lord Jesus Christ, who was conceived by the Holy Spirit and born of the virgin Mary without the ordinary mode of generation. Christ did not inherit the guilt or corruption of Adam because He was not in Adam by ordinary generation (Hebrews 7:26).[^43]

**4. The Manifestations of the Fallen State** The confession catalogs the consequences of being in Adam:

- **"Conceived in sin"** : Psalm 51:5 declares, "Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity, and in sin did my mother conceive me."[^44] Sin is not merely an acquired habit but an inborn condition.
- **"By nature children of wrath"** : Ephesians 2:3 affirms that we "were by nature children of wrath, like the rest of mankind."[^45] "Nature" here means not merely habit but inherent constitution.
- **"The servants of sin"** : Romans 6:20: "For when you were slaves of sin, you were free in regard to righteousness."[^46] The unregenerate are not merely occasional sinners but enslaved to sin.
- **"The subjects of death, and all other miseries, spiritual, temporal, and eternal"** : Death entered the world through sin (Romans 5:12), and with death comes every form of misery—grief, disease, conflict, and ultimately the eternal punishment of hell.[^47]

**5. The Sole Remedy** "Unless the Lord Jesus set them free."[^48] This clause is the gospel note in a dark chapter. The same Jesus who was not in Adam came to free those who are in Adam. Hebrews 2:14-15 declares that Christ came to "destroy the one who has the power of death, that is, the devil, and deliver all those who through fear of death were subject to lifelong slavery."[^49]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Nature of Original Corruption: Total Depravity and Inability

Having established the transmission of original corruption, the confession now describes its nature. <ConfessionBox>
**"From this original corruption, whereby we are utterly indisposed, disabled, and made opposite to all good, and wholly inclined to all evil, do proceed all actual transgressions."**[^51]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Source of Actual Sin** "From this original corruption do proceed all actual transgressions."[^52] Actual sins (the specific thoughts, words, and deeds that violate God's law) flow from original sin as a stream flows from a poisoned spring. We do not sin because we are sinners; we are sinners because we sin, but also we sin because we are sinners. The two truths are mutually reinforcing.[^53] James 1:14-15 traces temptation to desire, and desire to conception and birth of sin. Matthew 15:19 declares that "out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery, sexual immorality, theft, false witness, slander."[^54]

**2. The Threefold Description of Original Corruption** The confession describes original corruption with three devastating adjectives:

- **"Utterly indisposed to all good"** : The fallen human being has no natural inclination or appetite for that which is spiritually good. As Paul declares in Romans 8:7, "the mind that is set on the flesh is hostile to God, for it does not submit to God's law; indeed, it cannot."[^55] This is not merely a lack of interest but active hostility.
- **"Disabled to all good"** : Original corruption does not merely remove desire; it removes *ability*. The unregenerate person *cannot* please God (Romans 8:8) and *cannot* submit to His law (Romans 8:7). This is the doctrine of *total inability* (though not *absolute* inability—the unregenerate retain the natural faculties of choice and action, but they cannot choose spiritual good).[^56]
- **"Made opposite to all good"** : The fallen nature is not neutral but actively *opposed* to God and His righteousness. Colossians 1:21 describes the unregenerate as "alienated and hostile in mind, doing evil deeds."[^57]

**3. The Positive Inclination** "And wholly inclined to all evil."[^58] The confession does not merely state what we *cannot* do; it states what we *can* and *will* do—embrace evil. The fallen heart is not empty but filled with concupiscence—a deep, pervasive inclination toward that which God forbids. As John Owen wrote, "Indwelling sin is a powerful and effective principle constantly working toward evil."[^59]

**4. Total Depravity Defined** This paragraph is the confession's statement of *total depravity*—not the error that every human being is as evil as he could possibly be, but the biblical truth that sin has affected every part of the human person (intellect, will, emotions, body) and that fallen humanity is incapable of any spiritual good apart from God's regenerating grace.[^60] As the confession earlier cited (paragraph 2), humanity is "wholly defiled in all the faculties and parts of soul and body."[^61]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Remaining Corruption in Believers: Indwelling Sin Even after Regeneration

One of the most pastorally important paragraphs in the entire confession, paragraph 5 addresses the reality of indwelling sin in the lives of those who have been born again. <ConfessionBox>
**"The corruption of nature, during this life, does remain in those that are regenerated; and although it be through Christ pardoned and mortified, yet both itself, and the first motions thereof, are truly and properly sin."**[^63]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Persistence of Corruption** "The corruption of nature, during this life, does remain in those that are regenerated."[^64] Regeneration does not eradicate the sinful nature. The believer remains in the *status mixtus*—the mixed state of having both the new nature (created in regeneration) and the old nature (remaining corruption). This is the clear teaching of Scripture: Romans 7:18 declares, "I know that nothing good dwells in me, that is, in my flesh." Ecclesiastes 7:20 affirms, "Surely there is not a righteous man on earth who does good and never sins." 1 John 1:8 warns, "If we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us."[^65]

**2. The Pardon and Mortification of Remaining Sin** "Although it be through Christ pardoned and mortified."[^66] The confession identifies two ways God deals with remaining sin in believers:

- **Pardon** (justification): The guilt of indwelling sin is fully forgiven for Christ's sake. Though the believer's nature remains corrupted, he is not condemned for that corruption (Romans 8:1).
- **Mortification** (sanctification): The power of indwelling sin is progressively weakened and put to death through the work of the Spirit. As John Owen famously wrote, "Be killing sin, or sin will be killing you."[^67]

**3. The Sinfulness of Indwelling Sin and Its First Motions** "Yet both itself, and the first motions thereof, are truly and properly sin."[^68] This clause guards against two errors: (1) the error that only outward acts are sinful (the Pharisaical error) and (2) the error that indwelling sin is not truly sin for believers (the antinomian error). The confession insists that:

- **The corruption itself is sin**—not merely a weakness or infirmity, but a violation of God's law.
- **The "first motions" (Latin: *primae motus*)** —the initial stirrings of sin that arise spontaneously before the will consents—are also truly sin.[^69]

As A. A. Hodge observed, "From its very essence, the moral law demands absolute perfection of the character and disposition as well as action. God requires us to be holy as well as to act rightly."[^70]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Punishment of Sin: All Miseries, Spiritual, Temporal, and Eternal

The confession concludes with a comprehensive statement of the wages of sin. <ConfessionBox>
**"Every sin, both original and actual, being a transgression of the righteous law of God, and contrary thereunto, doth, in its own nature, bring guilt upon the sinner, whereby he is bound over to the wrath of God, and curse of the law, and so made subject to death, with all miseries spiritual, temporal, and eternal."**[^72]
</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Definition of Sin** "Every sin, both original and actual, being a transgression of the righteous law of God, and contrary thereunto."[^73] This echoes 1 John 3:4: "Everyone who makes a practice of sinning also practices lawlessness; sin is lawlessness." Sin is not merely a social mistake or a psychological disorder; it is *lawlessness*—a violation of the holy, just, and good law of God (Romans 7:12).[^74]

**2. The Guilt and Wrath Incurred** "Doth, in its own nature, bring guilt upon the sinner, whereby he is bound over to the wrath of God, and curse of the law."[^75] The phrase "bound over" is a legal term meaning "adjudged liable." Sin is not merely an unfortunate condition but a crime that incurs a penalty. As Paul declares in Galatians 3:10, "Cursed is everyone who does not abide by all things written in the Book of the Law, and do them."[^76]

**3. The Comprehensiveness of Punishment** "And so made subject to death, with all miseries spiritual, temporal, and eternal."[^77] The punishment of sin is not limited to one dimension but pervades all of human existence:

- **Spiritual miseries**: Separation from God (Ephesians 4:18), hardness of heart (Ephesians 4:18), blindness of mind (2 Corinthians 4:4), bondage to sin (John 8:34).
- **Temporal miseries**: Physical pain, disease, labor, conflict, grief, and ultimately physical death.
- **Eternal miseries**: The second death in hell—eternal conscious punishment away from the presence of the Lord (2 Thessalonians 1:9; Matthew 25:46).[^78]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Robust Doctrine of Sin

The confession's doctrine of sin rests on several interconnected theological pillars, each essential to maintaining biblical orthodoxy.[^80]

**Pillar 1: The Historical Reality of the Fall**

The confession treats the account of Genesis 3 as literal history, not myth or allegory. There was a real Adam, a real serpent, a real tree, and a real transgression. This historical grounding is essential because, as Paul argues in Romans 5, the parallel between Adam and Christ depends on the historical reality of both. If Adam is not a real historical figure, then Christ's role as the second Adam collapses.[^81]

**Pillar 2: The Covenant of Works and Federal Headship**

Behind the confession's teaching on the transmission of sin lies the doctrine of the covenant of works. God established a covenant with Adam as the representative head of the human race: perfect obedience would lead to life; disobedience would bring death. When Adam sinned, he represented all his posterity. Therefore, his sin is *imputed* to us (charged to our account), and his corrupted nature is *conveyed* to us.[^82]

This is the classic Reformed doctrine of *immediate imputation*: Adam's sin is charged to his descendants not because they inherit a corrupt nature first, but directly as the judicial act of God.[^83] As Charles Hodge summarizes, "In virtue of the union, federal and natural, between Adam and his posterity, his sin, although not their act, is so imputed to them that it is the judicial ground of the penalty threatened against him coming also upon them."[^84]

**Pillar 3: Original Sin Defined**

The confession distinguishes between original sin (the inherited guilt and corruption) and actual sin (the specific thoughts, words, and deeds that proceed from original sin). Original sin includes:

- **Original guilt**: The judicial liability to punishment incurred by Adam's sin.
- **Original corruption**: The inherent moral pollution transmitted from Adam to his posterity.

These two aspects are inseparable. Every human being born by ordinary generation enters the world both guilty and defiled.[^85]

**Pillar 4: Total Depravity and Total Inability**

Paragraph 4 describes the fallen human condition as "utterly indisposed, disabled, and made opposite to all good, and wholly inclined to all evil." This is the doctrine of *total depravity*—not that every human being is as evil as possible, but that sin has affected every part of the human person and that fallen humanity is incapable of any spiritual good apart from God's regenerating grace.[^86]

The confession also affirms *total inability*: the unregenerate *cannot* submit to God's law or please Him (Romans 8:7-8). This inability is not a lack of natural faculties but a moral bondage: the will is enslaved to sin and cannot choose what is spiritually good until liberated by grace.[^87]

**Pillar 5: The Persistence of Indwelling Sin in Believers**

One of the confession's most distinctive contributions is its clear affirmation that indwelling sin remains in believers after regeneration. This stands against perfectionist heresies (such as the Wesleyan doctrine of entire sanctification in this life) and against antinomian denials that remaining sin is truly sinful. The believer lives in a state of warfare between the flesh and the Spirit (Galatians 5:17), and this warfare will continue until death.[^88]

**Pillar 6: The Comprehensive Punishment of Sin**

Paragraph 6 declares that sin brings "all miseries spiritual, temporal, and eternal." This threefold punishment corresponds to the three dimensions of human existence: our relation to God (spiritual), our life in this world (temporal), and our eternal destiny (eternal). The confession does not shrink from affirming the reality of hell as eternal conscious punishment—the just wages of sin against an infinitely holy God.[^89]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Denial or Diminishment of Sin

The confession's doctrine of sin is not a speculative anthropology; it is a pastoral necessity. Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by this chapter.[^90]

**Crisis 1: The Pelagian Denial of Original Sin**

Pelagianism is not a medieval heresy; it is the default setting of the modern heart. The common assumption that "people are basically good" and that we are born as blank slates (tabula rasa) is a direct denial of the confession's teaching that we are "conceived in sin" and "by nature children of wrath."[^91]

Pastoral implications include:

- **The false hope of moral self-improvement**: If we are merely culturally or environmentally damaged, then better education, better laws, or better therapy can save us. But if we are *dead* in sin (Ephesians 2:1), then only divine resurrection can help us.
- **The refusal of guilt**: The modern therapeutic culture encourages us to feel good about ourselves and to refuse any diagnosis that would pronounce us guilty. But the confession insists that we are guilty—and that only the gospel can provide genuine relief from that guilt.[^92]

**Crisis 2: The Minimization of Sin's Wages**

The confession's teaching on the punishment of sin—"all miseries spiritual, temporal, and eternal"—is deeply counter-cultural in an age that denies the reality of divine wrath. Pastoral symptoms include:

- **A sentimental view of God** that reduces His wrath to a mere metaphor or a projection of human anger.
- **A denial of hell** as eternal conscious punishment, replacing it with annihilationism or universalism.
- **A trivialization of sin's consequences**—treating sin as a mistake rather than a crime, a weakness rather than a rebellion.

The confession calls pastors to preach the reality of God's wrath and the horror of hell—not as a scare tactic, but as the necessary backdrop to the breathtaking grace of the gospel.[^93]

**Crisis 3: The Perfectionist Error**

Some streams of Christianity teach that believers can achieve a state of sinless perfection in this life. The confession's paragraph 5 explicitly rejects this, affirming that "the corruption of nature, during this life, does remain in those that are regenerated."[^94]

Pastoral implications include:

- **Honesty about remaining sin**: Believers must be taught to confess their sins daily, not to pretend they have no sin (1 John 1:8-10).
- **The need for ongoing mortification**: Sanctification is not passive but active. Believers must "put to death" the deeds of the body by the Spirit (Romans 8:13).
- **The danger of hypocrisy**: When believers are taught that they should be sinless, they are driven either to despair (when they inevitably fail) or to hypocrisy (when they pretend they have succeeded).[^95]

**Crisis 4: The Antinomian Error**

At the opposite extreme from perfectionism is antinomianism (literally "against law"). The antinomian argues that since we are saved by grace and not by works, the law has no relevance for the believer's life. The confession's paragraph 5 refutes this error by insisting that remaining sin—even the first motions of it—are "truly and properly sin."[^96]

Pastoral implications include:

- **The abiding validity of the moral law**: Though believers are not justified by the law, the law remains the standard for holy living.
- **The necessity of confession and repentance**: Antinomianism often leads to a careless attitude toward sin. The confession calls believers to vigilance, not license.
- **The pursuit of holiness**: Grace does not abolish the law; it empowers obedience.[^97]

**Crisis 5: The Denial of Human Depravity in Modern Psychology**

Much of modern psychology operates on the assumption that human beings are essentially good and that "problems" are caused by external factors (bad parenting, trauma, social structures) rather than by an internal sinful nature. The confession's teaching that we are "utterly indisposed, disabled, and made opposite to all good" is a direct challenge to this optimistic anthropology.[^98]

Pastoral implications include:

- **The need for biblical counseling** that takes sin seriously, not merely as a symptom to be managed but as a condition to be forgiven and mortified.
- **The insufficiency of therapy alone**: While psychological insights can be helpful, they cannot address the fundamental problem of the human heart: sin.
- **The necessity of the new birth**: Only regeneration—the work of the Holy Spirit—can change the human heart's disposition from hostility to love.[^99]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Fall

The confession's doctrine of sin is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are seven practical steps derived from Chapter 6.[^100]

**Step 1: Acknowledge Your Solidarity in Adam**

Reject the individualism of the modern age. You are not a lone moral agent standing or falling on your own; you are part of a race that fell in its first father. This is not merely a doctrine to be believed but an identity to be embraced: "In Adam, I fell."[^101]

- **In prayer**: Confess not only your actual sins but also your original sin. Say with David, "Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity, and in sin did my mother conceive me" (Psalm 51:5).
- **In humility**: Recognize that any difference between you and the most hardened criminal is not your own merit but only God's restraining grace.

**Step 2: Rejoice in the Second Adam**

The same chapter that declares our solidarity in Adam also points to the remedy: "unless the Lord Jesus set them free." As in Adam all die, so in Christ all who are united to Him by faith are made alive (1 Corinthians 15:22).[^102]

- **In evangelism**: Do not despair of the human condition. The same gospel that saves you can save anyone—because the same Christ who is the second Adam is able to save to the uttermost all who come to God through Him.

**Step 3: Pursue Honest Self-Knowledge**

The doctrine of remaining sin in believers (paragraph 5) calls us to honest self-examination. Do not pretend that you have no sin. Do not minimize the first motions of sin in your heart. Confess them.[^103]

- **Daily examination**: At the end of each day, ask: "Where did my heart stray? What first motions of sin did I harbor? Where did my corrupted nature express itself?"

**Step 4: Mortify Indwelling Sin**

Because indwelling sin remains, it must be mortified (put to death). This is not passive resignation but active warfare. As John Owen wrote, "Be killing sin, or sin will be killing you."[^104]

- **Practical means**: Pray for the Spirit's help. Feed on the Word. Avoid temptation. Practice accountability. Use the means of grace.
- **Gospel motivation**: Remember that you are already justified. You fight from victory, not for victory.

**Step 5: Preach the Sinfulness of the First Motions**

Paragraph 5 guards against the Pharisaical error that only outward acts are sinful. The first motions of sin—the spontaneous stirrings of anger, lust, greed, envy, pride—are "truly and properly sin."[^105]

- **In discipleship**: Teach younger believers that sin begins in the heart. Help them to identify not merely their outward transgressions but their inward corruptions.
- **In preaching**: Do not preach a shallow morality that only addresses external behavior. Preach to the heart.

**Step 6: Embrace God's Wrath as the Backdrop to Grace**

The confession's final paragraph (6) declares the comprehensive punishment of sin. This is not merely a doctrine to be feared but a truth that magnifies the glory of the cross. The gospel is only good news if the bad news is truly bad.[^106]

- **In worship**: Sing hymns that declare both the holiness of God and the horror of sin—and the grace that meets us in our need.
- **In pastoral care**: When ministering to those who are weighed down by guilt, do not minimize their sin; point them to the One who bore the curse for them.

**Step 7: Live in Hope of Final Deliverance**

The same chapter that describes the misery of our fallen state also points to the ultimate deliverance. The Lord Jesus will set us free—not only from the guilt of sin (justification) and the power of sin (sanctification), but finally from the *presence* of sin (glorification). The day is coming when indwelling sin will be no more.[^107]

- **In suffering**: When you struggle with remaining sin, look forward to the day when you will be fully delivered.
- **In hope**: Pray, "Even so, come, Lord Jesus."

### Conclusion: The Universal Tragedy and the Only Remedy

Chapter 6 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is the necessary dark backdrop against which the bright colors of the gospel shine. Without the doctrine of the fall, we cannot understand the human condition: Why is the world so broken? Why are our hearts so conflicted? Why does death reign over all? The answer is found in this chapter: we have all sinned in Adam, and in Adam we all fell.[^108]

But the same chapter that diagnoses the disease also whispers the cure. In every paragraph, the confession points beyond sin to the One who alone can set us free. Adam, the first man, brought condemnation and corruption to all his descendants. Christ, the second Adam, brings righteousness and life to all who are united to Him by faith.[^109]

The confession's concluding note is not despair but hope: "unless the Lord Jesus set them free." That "unless" is the hinge of history. For those who are in Adam, all the miseries—spiritual, temporal, and eternal—await. But for those who are in Christ, there is now no condemnation (Romans 8:1). The free gift is not like the trespass. For if many died through one man's trespass, much more have the grace of God and the free gift by the grace of that one man Jesus Christ abounded for many (Romans 5:15).[^110]

May we, like the apostle Paul, know both the depth of our sin and the height of God's grace. And may we proclaim to a fallen world the only remedy for the universal tragedy: the Lord Jesus Christ.

**A Prayer from Chapter 6** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O most holy and righteous God: We confess that we were conceived in sin and brought forth in iniquity. In Adam, we all fell. In Adam, we all died. From our first parents, we have inherited guilt and corruption. Our hearts are utterly indisposed, disabled, and made opposite to all good. We are wholly inclined to all evil. Every actual transgression proceeds from this original corruption.*

*We have sinned in thought, word, and deed. We have broken Your righteous law in countless ways. We are by nature children of wrath, servants of sin, subjects of death. We deserve all miseries—spiritual, temporal, and eternal. We have no hope in ourselves.*

*But we thank You for the second Adam, the Lord Jesus Christ. In Him, we have pardon for our guilt and power for our corruption. In Him, we are set free. Though indwelling sin remains in us during this life, it is through Christ pardoned and mortified. We look forward to the day when we shall be fully delivered from its presence.*

*Until that day, grant us to war against our indwelling sin, to confess our actual transgressions, and to rest in the finished work of our Savior. For Jesus Christ's sake. Amen.*[^111]
