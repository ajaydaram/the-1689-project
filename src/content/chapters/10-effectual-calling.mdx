# The Power That Cannot Fail: Why Effectual Calling Is the Believer's Only Hope

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 10 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Link Between Eternal Decree and Personal Salvation

Chapter 10 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled “Of Effectual Calling,” stands at the precise point where the eternal decree of God touches the sinner’s experience.[^1] If Chapter 3 declares God’s eternal purpose to save the elect, Chapter 6 diagnoses the universal bondage of fallen humanity, and Chapter 9 describes the will’s inability to choose spiritual good, then Chapter 10 explains *how* the elect are actually brought from death to life.[^2]

Samuel Waldron notes that someone studying the confession might be surprised to find no separate chapter on regeneration.[^3] That subject is, however, dealt with under the heading of effectual calling. “I think there is wisdom in this,” Waldron writes, “because discussions of regeneration sometimes become abstracted from the gospel. Effectual calling keeps the discussion of God’s mighty saving work in regeneration from becoming detached from the gospel of Christ.”[^4]

The chapter comprises four paragraphs, each addressing a distinct aspect of effectual calling. Paragraph 1 defines effectual calling—its recipients, its agent, its means, its nature, and its result. Paragraph 2 grounds effectual calling in God’s free and special grace alone, over against any human merit or agency. Paragraph 3 addresses a particular pastoral question: the salvation of elect infants and others incapable of being outwardly called by the ministry of the Word. Paragraph 4 states the necessary corollary of effectual calling: those not elected will not and cannot truly come to Christ, and therefore cannot be saved.[^5]

Waldron identifies five characteristics of effectual calling that the confession presupposes or teaches: its *factuality* (there is a call different from the general gospel call), its *individuality* (it comes to specific persons, not to humanity in general), its *priority* (logically and temporally prior to faith and conversion), its *efficacy* (it always saves those to whom it comes), and its *instrumentality* (God works through the Word to effect this call).[^6]

The confession’s compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—grounded every assertion in Scripture, drawing especially on the Gospel of John, the Epistles of Paul, and the Old Testament prophecies of the new covenant.[^7]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Effectual Calling

Chapter 10 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on how God actually brings sinners to Himself.[^8]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Definition of Effectual Calling

The confession opens with a declaration of breathtaking scope: 

<ConfessionBox>
"Those whom God hath predestinated unto life, he is pleased in his appointed, and accepted time, effectually to call, by his Word and Spirit, out of that state of sin and death in which they are by nature, to grace and salvation by Jesus Christ; enlightening their minds spiritually and savingly to understand the things of God; taking away their heart of stone, and giving unto them a heart of flesh; renewing their wills, and by his almighty power determining them to that which is good, and effectually drawing them to Jesus Christ; yet so as they come most freely, being made willing by his grace."
</ConfessionBox>[^9]

**1. The Recipients of the Call: “Those whom God hath predestinated unto life.”**[^10] Effectual calling is not a general invitation to all who hear the gospel. It is a specific call addressed to the elect. The confession cites Romans 8:30 (“whom he predestined, them he also called”) and Ephesians 1:10-11 (“we have obtained an inheritance, having been predestined according to his purpose”).[^11] As Waldron explains, “The gospel call is real. It comes to all who hear the gospel. The gospel of Christ freely offers all who hear it salvation. But that is not what we mean by effectual calling. Effectual calling is different. It does not come to all offering salvation. It comes to some assuring their salvation.”[^12]

**2. The Author of the Call: “He is pleased.”**[^13] God the Father is the agent of effectual calling. The verb “pleased” indicates divine sovereignty and good pleasure. The confession cites 1 Corinthians 1:9, where Paul declares that believers “were called into the fellowship of his Son, Jesus Christ our Lord.”[^14] Waldron notes that “effectual calling is related to the work of Christ” and underscores the gospel-centered and Christ-centered nature of the doctrine.[^15]

**3. The Occasion of the Call: “In his appointed, and accepted time.”**[^16] The call occurs at the specific time of God’s choosing. The confession cites Galatians 4:4, where Paul declares that “when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son.”[^17] This is not an arbitrary time but the time of God’s good pleasure.

**4. The Manner of the Call: “Effectually to call.”**[^18] The Latin root *effectus* means “to bring about, accomplish.” The effectual call is not a mere invitation but a summons that actually accomplishes its purpose. Waldron identifies this as the *efficacy* of effectual calling. “By this I mean, of course, that the effectual calling saves, actually and powerfully, all those to whom it comes.”[^19]

**5. The Means of the Call: “By his Word and Spirit.”**[^20] God works through two instruments: the external Word (the gospel proclamation) and the internal Spirit. Waldron calls this the *instrumentality* of effectual calling.[^21] The Spirit does not work apart from the Word nor merely alongside it; He works *through* it. As Peter declares, “you have been born again, not of seed which is perishable, but imperishable, that is, through the living and enduring word of God” (1 Peter 1:23).[^22] The confession cites 2 Thessalonians 2:13-14: “God chose you as firstfruits to be saved, through sanctification by the Spirit and belief in the truth. To this he called you through our gospel.”[^23]

**6. The Transition Effected: “Out of that state of sin and death in which they are by nature, to grace and salvation by Jesus Christ.”**[^24] This is a radical relocation. Before the call, the sinner is “dead in trespasses and sins” (Ephesians 2:1). After the call, the same person is alive in Christ. The confession cites Ephesians 2:1-6 as proof: “And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked… but God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ.”[^25]

**7. The Operations of the Call.** The confession specifies three distinct works of God in effectual calling, each grounded in Scripture.

*First*, **enlightening the mind**: “enlightening their minds spiritually and savingly to understand the things of God.”[^26] The confession cites Acts 26:18, where Paul declares that Christ sent him “to open their eyes, so that they may turn from darkness to light.”[^27] Before regeneration, the mind is darkened (Ephesians 4:18). The natural person cannot understand the things of the Spirit (1 Corinthians 2:14). God’s call brings spiritual illumination—not merely intellectual information, but a saving apprehension of the truth.[^28]

*Second*, **renewing the heart**: “taking away their heart of stone, and giving unto them a heart of flesh.”[^29] This echoes Ezekiel 36:26, the classic Old Testament promise of the new covenant: “I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh.”[^30] The “heart of stone” is the unregenerate heart—hard, cold, unresponsive, unable to feel spiritual realities. The “heart of flesh” is the regenerate heart—soft, warm, responsive to God. The confession also cites Deuteronomy 30:6: “The LORD your God will circumcise your heart and the heart of your offspring, so that you will love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul, that you may live.”[^31]

*Third*, **renewing the will**: “renewing their wills, and by his almighty power determining them to that which is good, and effectually drawing them to Jesus Christ.”[^32] This is the confession’s answer to the question posed in Chapter 9: how can the will be freed? God renews the will, determines it to good, and draws it to Christ. The confession cites Ephesians 1:19: “the immeasurable greatness of his power toward us who believe.”[^33] As Waldron observes, “We owe our salvation to the sovereign grace that elected us and the sovereign grace that effectually called us to salvation.”[^34]

**8. The Result: Voluntary Coming.** “Yet so as they come most freely, being made willing by his grace.”[^35] The confession refuses to set divine sovereignty and human freedom in opposition. The same God who effectually draws makes the sinner willing. The sinner comes—really, voluntarily, freely—but he comes because God has made him willing. The confession cites Psalm 110:3: “Your people will offer themselves freely on the day of your power.”[^36] And Song of Solomon 1:4: “Draw me after you; let us run.”[^37] As Augustine famously prayed, “Give what you command, and command what you will.”[^38]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Ground of Effectual Calling: Free and Special Grace Alone

Paragraph 2 states: 

<ConfessionBox>
"This effectual call is of God’s free and special grace alone, not from anything at all foreseen in man, nor from any power or agency in the creature, being wholly passive therein, being dead in sins and trespasses, until being quickened and renewed by the Holy Spirit; he is thereby enabled to answer this call, and to embrace the grace offered and conveyed in it, and that by no less power than that which raised up Christ from the dead."
</ConfessionBox>[^40]

**1. The Basis of the Call: “Of God’s free and special grace alone.”**[^41] The confession insists that effectual calling is grounded in grace alone (Latin: *sola gratia*). It is “free” (not compelled by any external factor) and “special” (particular, not general).[^42] The confession cites 2 Timothy 1:9: “He has saved us and called us to a holy calling, not because of our works but because of his own purpose and grace.”[^43]

**2. The Negative Ground: Not Foreseen Merit.** “Not from anything at all foreseen in man, nor from any power or agency in the creature.”[^44] The confession explicitly rejects the Arminian notion that God’s call is based on foreseen faith or works. The proof text is 2 Timothy 1:9 (already cited) and Ephesians 2:8: “For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God.”[^45] As Waldron explains, “The effectual call is not based on anything God sees in the sinner. It is not based on foreseen faith. It is not based on foreseen merit. It is not based on anything at all in the creature.”[^46]

**3. The Condition of the Creature: “Being wholly passive therein, being dead in sins and trespasses.”**[^47] The unregenerate sinner is not merely sick or neutral; he is dead. Ephesians 2:1 declares, “You were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked.”[^48] A dead person cannot contribute to his own resurrection. The confession cites 1 Corinthians 2:14: “The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned.”[^49]

**4. The Divine Quickening: “Until being quickened and renewed by the Holy Spirit.”**[^50] The verb “quickened” (from the Greek *zōopoieō*, Ephesians 2:5) means “made alive.” God must first bring life before the dead can respond. The confession cites John 5:25: “Truly, truly, I say to you, an hour is coming, and is now here, when the dead will hear the voice of the Son of God, and those who hear will live.”[^51]

**5. The Enabled Response: “He is thereby enabled to answer this call, and to embrace the grace offered and conveyed in it.”**[^52] After quickening, the sinner is enabled to respond. This is not a contradiction of monergism but its necessary complement: the same Spirit who gives life enables the life to function. As Waldron states, “The creature is passive in the first moment of regeneration, but active in the subsequent response of faith.”[^53]

**6. The Measure of the Power: “By no less power than that which raised up Christ from the dead.”**[^54] The confession cites Ephesians 1:19-20: “the immeasurable greatness of his power toward us who believe, according to the working of his great might that he worked in Christ when he raised him from the dead.”[^55] The same resurrection power that conquered death is the power that brings dead sinners to life.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** 2 Timothy 1:9; Ephesians 2:8; 1 Corinthians 2:14; Ephesians 2:5; John 5:25; Ephesians 1:19-20.[^56]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Special Cases: Elect Infants and Those Incapable of Outward Call

Paragraph 3 addresses a delicate pastoral question: what about those who never hear the gospel? 

<ConfessionBox>
"Elect infants dying in infancy are regenerated and saved by Christ through the Spirit; who works when, and where, and how he pleases; so also are all elect persons, who are incapable of being outwardly called by the ministry of the Word."
</ConfessionBox>[^57]

**1. The Status of the Question.** Waldron notes that “the fact is that the Bible is silent on this issue. It would have been much better if the framers of the confession had said nothing.”[^58] Yet the confession does speak, and it does so carefully, limiting its assertion to *elect* infants and grounding their salvation in the sovereign work of the Spirit.

**2. The Assertion about Elect Infants.** “Elect infants dying in infancy are regenerated and saved by Christ through the Spirit.”[^59] The confession does not say that all infants who die are saved; it says *elect* infants are saved. The basis is not innocence (all infants are conceived in sin, Psalm 51:5) but divine election.[^60] As the Westminster Confession (10.3) similarly states, “Elect infants, dying in infancy, are regenerated and saved by Christ through the Spirit.”[^61] The confession cites John 3:3, 5, 6: “Unless one is born again he cannot see the kingdom of God… unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God. That which is born of the flesh is flesh, and that which is born of the Spirit is spirit.”[^62]

**3. The Freedom of the Spirit.** “Who works when, and where, and how he pleases.”[^63] The confession cites John 3:8: “The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it goes. So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit.”[^64] The Spirit is not bound by the ordinary means of the Word in the case of those incapable of receiving it.

**4. The Extension to Other Incapable Persons.** “So also are all elect persons, who are incapable of being outwardly called by the ministry of the Word.”[^65] This includes those with severe intellectual disabilities or other conditions rendering them incapable of understanding the gospel proclamation. As Waldron explains, “The confession is simply saying that the same principle applies to all those who are incapable of being called by the external ministry of the Word. The Spirit can regenerate them directly.”[^66]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** John 3:3, 5, 6; John 3:8.[^67]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Negative Corollary: The Fate of the Non-Elect

The confession concludes with the necessary corollary: 

<ConfessionBox>
"Others not elected, although they may be called by the ministry of the Word, and may have some common operations of the Spirit, yet not being effectually drawn by the Father, they neither will nor can truly come to Christ, and therefore cannot be saved: much less can men that do not receive the Christian religion be saved; be they never so diligent to frame their lives according to the light of nature and the law of that religion they do profess."
</ConfessionBox>[^68]

**1. The Nature of the General Call.** “Others not elected, although they may be called by the ministry of the Word, and may have some common operations of the Spirit.”[^69] The confession acknowledges that non-elect hearers of the gospel receive a *general* call (Matthew 22:14: “Many are called, but few are chosen”) and even “common operations of the Spirit” (Hebrews 6:4-5).[^70] These common operations include illumination, temporary faith, and religious affections—but they are not saving.

**2. The Inability of the Non-Elect.** “Yet not being effectually drawn by the Father, they neither will nor can truly come to Christ, and therefore cannot be saved.”[^71] The non-elect cannot come—not because God withholds an arbitrary power, but because their fallen nature remains unchanged. The confession cites John 6:44: “No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him.”[^72]

**3. The Exclusion of Natural Religion.** “Much less can men that do not receive the Christian religion be saved; be they never so diligent to frame their lives according to the light of nature and the law of that religion they do profess.”[^73] The confession explicitly rejects pluralism. “Light of nature” (natural revelation, Romans 1-2) and sincere devotion to non-Christian religions are insufficient for salvation. The confession cites Acts 4:12: “There is salvation in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved.”[^74]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Five Pillars of Effectual Calling

Waldron identifies five characteristics of effectual calling that the confession presupposes or teaches.[^76]

**Pillar 1: The Factuality of Effectual Calling.** There exists a call distinct from the general gospel call. Matthew 22:14 declares, “Many are called, but few are chosen.”[^77] Paul distinguishes “the called” (1 Corinthians 1:24) from those who hear but reject. The confession’s very use of the term “effectual call” presupposes that not every call is effectual.[^78]

**Pillar 2: The Individuality of Effectual Calling.** Effectual calling does not come to humanity generically. It comes to “those whom God hath predestinated unto life.”[^79] Romans 8:30 traces the golden chain: “Whom he predestined, them he also called.” The call is individual and specific.

**Pillar 3: The Priority of Effectual Calling.** Logically and temporally prior to faith and conversion. Waldron explains, “Effectual calling is logically prior to the blessings it leads to and discussed in the following chapters. It is also chronologically prior to the faith, repentance, and obedience that flow from it.”[^80] The confession’s order—election, then effectual calling, then justification, then glorification—follows Romans 8:30.

**Pillar 4: The Efficacy of Effectual Calling.** Effectual calling actually saves all to whom it comes. Waldron argues this from three lines of evidence: (1) the called are the saved (Romans 1:6-7; 1 Corinthians 1:2, 24); (2) effectual calling is the outworking of election (Romans 8:28-30); (3) God’s voice is powerful, not merely persuasive (John 5:25).[^81]

**Pillar 5: The Instrumentality of Effectual Calling.** God works through the Word to effect the call. Waldron illustrates: “The lamp in my study will not light up without the electricity flowing through the cord. But if you take away either the bulb or the electricity, I will remain in darkness. We need both the bulb of the Word and the electricity of the Spirit.”[^82] 1 Peter 1:23 declares that we are born again “through the living and enduring word of God.” The Spirit does not ordinarily regenerate apart from the Word.

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Denied or Distorted Effectual Calling

The confession’s doctrine of effectual calling is not a speculative theology; it is a pastoral necessity. Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 10.[^83]

**Crisis 1: The Pelagian Assumption that Humans Can Choose God without Grace.** The default setting of the modern heart is Pelagian: “I can decide for Christ whenever I want.” The confession’s teaching that the sinner is “dead in sins and trespasses” until “quickened and renewed by the Holy Spirit” confronts this deadly assumption.[^84] As Augustine famously prayed, “Give what you command, and command what you will.”[^85]

**Crisis 2: The Arminian Inconsistency.** Many evangelicals affirm total depravity in theory but deny it in practice, treating the gospel as mere persuasion and the will as neutral. The confession insists that effectual calling is an act of divine power, not mere persuasion.[^86] The same power that raised Christ from the dead raises dead sinners to life (Ephesians 1:19-20).

**Crisis 3: The Hyper-Calvinist Denial of the Free Offer.** Paragraph 4 acknowledges that non-elect hearers receive a genuine call and common operations of the Spirit. The confession rejects hyper-Calvinism, which would deny that the gospel should be preached indiscriminately.[^87] The general call is real and sincere, even if not effectual for all.

**Crisis 4: The Fear about the Salvation of Infants.** Grieving parents who lose a child in infancy need hope. The confession provides comfort: elect infants who die are saved—not because they are innocent (Psalm 51:5), but because of God’s electing grace and the Spirit’s sovereign work.[^88]

**Crisis 5: The Pluralistic Assumption that All Religions Lead to God.** The confession declares that apart from Christ there is no salvation. Sincere pagans are not saved by their sincerity. “Light of nature” cannot save. “Much less can men that do not receive the Christian religion be saved.”[^89] This is the exclusive claim of the gospel (Acts 4:12).

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Effectual Calling

The confession’s doctrine of effectual calling is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are five practical steps derived from Chapter 10.[^90]

**Step 1: Preach the Gospel Boldly.** Because God uses the Word as the instrument of effectual calling, we must preach. Effectual calling does not make preaching irrelevant; it makes preaching essential. As Paul declares, “How are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to hear without someone preaching?” (Romans 10:14).[^91]

**Step 2: Pray for the Spirit’s Power.** Preaching alone cannot raise the dead. Pray that the Spirit would accompany the Word with effectual power. The same Spirit who raised Christ from the dead (Ephesians 1:19-20) works through the proclamation of the gospel to raise dead sinners.[^92]

**Step 3: Examine Your Own Calling.** Peter commands, “Be all the more diligent to make your calling and election sure” (2 Peter 1:10). Do not assume you are called; look for the marks of grace: faith in Christ, love for the brethren, a desire for holiness, perseverance in trial.[^93]

**Step 4: Comfort Grieving Parents.** If you have lost an infant, the confession offers hope: elect infants who die in infancy are saved by Christ through the Spirit. God is sovereign and good. Trust His mercy.[^94]

**Step 5: Reject Pluralism.** Proclaim boldly that salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved (Acts 4:12). The exclusivity of Christ is not arrogance; it is the gospel.[^95]

### Conclusion: The Power That Cannot Fail

Chapter 10 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer’s assurance that salvation is not left to the uncertain response of the fallen will. The same God who predestined the elect from eternity effectually calls them in time. The call is not a mere invitation; it is a summons that accomplishes what it commands. The Spirit works through the Word, enlightening the mind, renewing the heart, determining the will, and drawing the sinner to Christ—and the sinner comes freely, because God has made him willing.[^96]

This is the power that cannot fail. It is the same power that raised Christ from the dead. And it is the power that will raise every elect sinner from the grave of sin to new life in Christ. To God alone be the glory.[^97]

**A Prayer from Chapter 10** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father, who hast predestined us unto life from before the foundation of the world: We thank Thee for effectual calling. In Thy appointed time, by Thy Word and Spirit, Thou didst call us out of that state of sin and death in which we were by nature, to grace and salvation in Jesus Christ. Thou didst enlighten our minds spiritually and savingly to understand the things of God. Thou didst take away our heart of stone and give us a heart of flesh. Thou didst renew our wills, and by Thine almighty power determine us to that which is good, effectually drawing us to Jesus Christ. And yet we came most freely, being made willing by Thy grace. We praise Thee for the immeasurable greatness of Thy power toward us who believe, according to the working of Thy great might that Thou workedst in Christ when Thou raisedst Him from the dead. Grant that we may live as those who have been called out of darkness into marvellous light. In Jesus’ name. Amen.*[^98]
