# The Soul's Solemn Anchor: Why Lawful Oaths and Vows Ground Truth in the Fear of God

### Introduction: A Forgotten but Vital Chapter

Chapter 23 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Lawful Oaths and Vows," addresses a subject that may seem foreign to modern Christians. As one pastor notes, "This chapter of the confession addresses a subject that may seem irrelevant, or if not trivial, in today's society. Why would a Christian confession of faith include a seven-paragraph chapter regarding oaths and vows?" The answer lies in the collapse of truth-telling. The very fact that the question seems strange reveals how far society has fallen in its understanding of oaths. In a world of casual promises, selective honesty, and postmodern relativism, the biblical doctrine of oath-taking stands as a prophetic witness that truth still matters—and that God still hears. The chapter stands firmly on the regulative principle of worship derived from Chapter 22. Since oaths are acts of worship that invoke the name of God, they must be performed only as God has commanded in His Word—no more, no less.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Lawful Oaths and Vows

Chapter 23 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, limits, and purpose of oaths and vows.

#### Paragraph 1 – The Definition of a Lawful Oath

The confession opens with a precise definition: **"A lawful oath is a part of religious worship, wherein the person swearing in truth, righteousness, and judgment, solemnly calls God to witness what he swears, and to judge him according to the truth or falseness thereof."**

**1. "A Lawful Oath Is a Part of Religious Worship."** The confession begins by locating oaths within the context of worship. When a person takes an oath, they are not merely making a promise; they are performing a religious act. Oaths are not to be taken lightly or frequently. But they are also not to be avoided altogether. The confession rejects both extremes: the error of the Anabaptists who refused any oath whatsoever, and the error of those who swore casually.

**2. "Swearing in Truth, Righteousness, and Judgment."** The confession identifies three essential qualifiers for a lawful oath:

- **In Truth**—what is sworn must be factually accurate. Jeremiah 4:2 declares, "You shall swear, 'As the LORD lives,' in truth, in justice, and in righteousness."
- **In Righteousness**—what is sworn must be morally just. An oath to do something evil is not lawful, regardless of sincerity.
- **In Judgment**—the oath must be taken with discernment, not rashly. The person must have soberly considered the weight of what they are swearing.

**3. "Solemnly Calls God to Witness What He Swears."** The essence of an oath is invoking God as a witness. An oath is a formal appeal to God's omniscience and justice. 2 Chronicles 6:22-23 records Solomon's prayer: "If a man sins against his neighbor and is made to take an oath and comes and swears his oath before your altar in this house, then hear from heaven and act and judge your servants."

**4. "To Judge Him According to the Truth or Falseness Thereof."** The confession makes clear that an oath invokes not only God's witness but also His judgment. A false oath calls down divine punishment. This is why oath-taking is so serious. The confession does not here mention vows; it defines only the oath. A vow is a promise of future behavior, while an oath is a promise that a statement is true. But the same principles of truth, righteousness, and judgment apply to both.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Exodus 20:7; Deuteronomy 10:20; Jeremiah 4:2; 2 Chronicles 6:22-23.

#### Paragraph 2 – The Sanctity and Exclusive Object of an Oath

Paragraph 2 states: **"The Name of God only is that by which men ought to swear; and therein it is to be used, with all holy fear and reverence; therefore to swear vainly or rashly by that glorious and dreadful name, or to swear at all by any other thing, is sinful, and to be abhorred; yet as in matter of weight and moment, for confirmation of truth, and ending all strife, an oath is warranted by the word of God; so a lawful oath being imposed by lawful authority in such matters, ought to be taken."**

**1. "The Name of God Only Is That by Which Men Ought to Swear."** The confession prohibits swearing by any creature—by heaven, by earth, by Jerusalem, by one's own head, or by any other thing. To swear by something else is to commit idolatry, because it ascribes to that thing an authority that belongs to God alone. When a person swears an oath, they are calling upon something greater than themselves to keep them true to their word. To swear by anything other than God is ultimately to call upon a false god.

**2. The Context of Matthew 5:34-37.** Jesus said, "Do not take an oath at all, either by heaven, for it is the throne of God, or by the earth, for it is his footstool... Let what you say be simply 'Yes' or 'No.'" Many rabbis in Jesus' day taught that a vow made in God's name was inviolable, but one made in the name of something else could be disregarded. Jesus condemned this casuistry, asserting that to swear by heaven, the earth, or Jerusalem is equivalent to swearing in God's name and that such oaths are just as binding. Christ did not forbid all oaths; He forbade the frivolous, evasive, and manipulative oath-taking that allowed people to lie without consequences. The confession takes the same position: oaths are lawful when they are necessary for confirmation of truth and for ending all strife.

**3. "With All Holy Fear and Reverence."** Because the oath invokes God's name, it must be taken with "holy fear and reverence." Swearing "vainly or rashly" is sinful and to be abhorred.

**4. The Warrant for Oaths.** The confession cites Hebrews 6:16: "For people swear by something greater than themselves, and in all their disputes an oath is final for confirmation." It also cites 2 Corinthians 1:23, where Paul swears, "I call God to witness against me." The apostle himself took oaths, demonstrating that oaths remain lawful under the New Testament.

**5. "Ought to Be Taken."** The confession uses softer language than the Westminster Confession, which declared that refusing a lawful oath is a sin. The Baptists softened this statement in recognition of Anabaptist scruples, saying instead that such an oath "ought to be taken" rather than declaring refusal a sin.[^5][^6][^7][^8][^9]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Matthew 5:34-37; James 5:12; Hebrews 6:16; 2 Corinthians 1:23; Nehemiah 13:25.

#### Paragraph 3 – The Solemnity of Oath-Taking

Paragraph 3 states: **"Whosoever takes an oath warranted by the word of God, ought duly to consider the weightiness of so solemn an act, and therein to avouch nothing but what he knows to be truth; for that by rash, false, and vain oaths, the Lord is provoked, and for them this land mourns."**

**1. "Duly to Consider the Weightiness."** The confession calls for sober consideration before taking an oath. Oath-taking is not a casual act. The person must reflect on what they are doing: they are invoking God as witness and judge.

**2. "To Avouch Nothing but What He Knows to Be Truth."** The confession requires certainty. A person may not swear to something they merely think is probably true. They must know it to be true.

**3. "The Lord Is Provoked, and for Them This Land Mourns."** The confession cites Leviticus 19:12: "You shall not swear by my name falsely, and so profane the name of your God." It also cites Jeremiah 23:10, where the prophet declares that "because of swearing the land mourns." False and vain oaths bring divine judgment not only on individuals but on entire nations.[^10][^11][^12]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Leviticus 19:12; Jeremiah 23:10.

#### Paragraph 4 – The Interpretation of Oaths

Paragraph 4 states: **"An oath is to be taken in the plain and common sense of the words, without equivocation or mental reservation."**

**1. "In the Plain and Common Sense of the Words."** The confession rejects casuistry and evasion. A person may not take an oath while secretly intending something different from what their words communicate.

**2. "Without Equivocation or Mental Reservation."** The confession condemns the practice of taking oaths with hidden meanings. Equivocation uses words that have a double meaning, intending one meaning while hoping the hearer understands another. A "mental reservation" is a silent qualification added in the mind that is not spoken aloud. The Roman Catholic doctrine of "mental reservation" allowed lying under oath as long as one silently added a qualification. The confession rejects this completely.

**3. Psalm 24:4 as the Standard.** "He who has clean hands and a pure heart, who does not lift up his soul to what is false and does not swear deceitfully." The oath must be taken with a pure heart and without deceit.[^13][^14][^15]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Psalm 24:4.

#### Paragraph 5 – The Nature of Lawful Vows

The confession concludes with the subject of vows: **"A vow, which is not to be made to any creature, but to God alone, is to be made and performed with all religious care and faithfulness; but popish monastical vows of perpetual single life, professed poverty, and regular obedience, are so far from being degrees of higher perfection, that they are superstitious and sinful snares, in which no Christian may entangle himself."**

**1. The Object of a Vow.** A vow is a promise of future behavior made to God alone. Unlike an oath, which is a promise to men made before God, a vow is a promise directed to God. The confession cites Psalm 76:11: "Make vows to the LORD your God and perform them."

**2. To Be Made with Religious Care and Faithfulness.** Vows must be made carefully and performed faithfully.

**3. The Rejection of Popish Monastical Vows.** The confession specifically condemns three types of monastic vows: vows of perpetual single life (celibacy), professed poverty, and regular obedience (submission to monastic rules). The confession denies that these are "degrees of higher perfection."

**4. Scriptural Arguments Against Perpetual Celibacy.** 1 Corinthians 7:2, 9 states, "But because of the temptation to sexual immorality, each man should have his own wife and each woman her own husband... But if they cannot exercise self-control, they should marry. For it is better to marry than to burn with passion."

**5. Scriptural Arguments Against Professed Poverty.** Ephesians 4:28 commands, "Let the thief no longer steal, but rather let him labor, doing honest work with his own hands, so that he may have something to share with anyone in need." Work and generosity—not vowed poverty—are the biblical pattern.

**6. Scriptural Arguments Against Monastic Obedience.** The vow of "regular obedience" binds the conscience to human rules not commanded in Scripture. This violates the liberty of conscience declared in Chapter 21. The confession declares that such vows are not only not higher perfection but are "superstitious and sinful snares."[^16][^17][^18][^19]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Psalm 76:11; Genesis 28:20-22; 1 Corinthians 7:2, 9; Ephesians 4:28; Matthew 19:1.

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Lawful Oaths and Vows

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of oaths and vows.

**Pillar 1: Oaths Are Acts of Religious Worship.** Because oaths invoke God as witness and judge, they are not merely civil acts but religious ones. The regulative principle of worship therefore applies: only oaths warranted by Scripture are lawful.

**Pillar 2: God Alone Is the Object of an Oath.** Swearing by any creature is idolatry, because it ascribes to that creature an authority that belongs to God alone.

**Pillar 3: Christ Did Not Abolish All Oaths.** Jesus' teaching in Matthew 5:34-37 forbids frivolous and evasive oath-taking, not solemn oaths taken in matters of weight and moment. The apostles themselves took oaths, confirming their continued lawfulness.

**Pillar 4: Truth, Righteousness, and Judgment Are the Conditions.** An oath must be factually true, morally just, and taken with sober discernment.

**Pillar 5: No Equivocation or Mental Reservation.** Oaths must be taken in the plain sense of the words, without hidden meanings or silent qualifications.

**Pillar 6: Vows Are Made to God Alone.** Vows are promises of future behavior directed to God, and they must be made with care and performed faithfully.

**Pillar 7: Monastic Vows Are Sinful Snares.** Vows of perpetual celibacy, professed poverty, and monastic obedience are not higher perfection but superstitious entanglements contrary to Scripture.

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted View of Oaths and Vows

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 23.

**Crisis 1: The Casual Lie.** In a culture of "post-truth" and casual dishonesty, the confession's insistence that oaths must be taken in truth confronts the normalization of lying.

**Crisis 2: The Anabaptist Rejection of All Oaths.** Some Christian traditions refuse any oath whatsoever. The confession affirms that oaths are warranted by the Word of God under the New Testament as well as under the Old.

**Crisis 3: The Reduction of Oaths to Mere Formality.** Many treat oaths as meaningless formalities. The confession calls for oaths to be taken with "all holy fear and reverence."

**Crisis 4: The Reluctance to Take Lawful Oaths.** The confession's affirmation that a lawful oath "ought to be taken" when imposed by lawful authority speaks to Christians who refuse to testify in court or serve on juries due to scruples about oath-taking.

**Crisis 5: The Misunderstanding of Monastic Vows.** Some still view monasticism as a higher form of spirituality. The confession declares that such vows are "superstitious and sinful snares."

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Lawful Oaths and Vows

**Step 1: Reserve Oaths for Matters of Weight and Moment.** Do not swear casually in everyday conversation. Let your "yes" be "yes" and your "no" be "no."

**Step 2: When You Take an Oath, Take It Seriously.** Before swearing an oath, consider the weightiness of the act. You are calling God as witness and judge.

**Step 3: Swear Only by God's Name.** Never swear by any creature—not by heaven, not by earth, not by your own life. Let your oath be rendered to God alone.

**Step 4: Take Oaths in Truth, Righteousness, and Judgment.** Ensure that what you swear is true, that it is morally just, and that you have soberly considered its weight.

**Step 5: Avoid Equivocation and Mental Reservation.** Mean what you say. Take oaths in the plain and common sense of the words.

**Step 6: Make Vows to God Carefully.** If you make a vow to God—whether in prayer, in worship, or in a covenant such as marriage—perform it faithfully.

**Step 7: Reject Monastic Vows as Sinful Snares.** Do not bind your conscience to vows of perpetual celibacy, vowed poverty, or obedience to human rules. Such vows are not higher perfection but entanglements contrary to Scripture.

### Conclusion: The Soul's Solemn Anchor

Chapter 23 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece. It is a prophetic witness that truth still matters and God still hears. Oaths are acts of worship invoking God as witness and judge. They must be taken only in God's name, with truth, righteousness, judgment, and without deceit. Vows are promises made to God alone, to be made with care and performed faithfully. And monastic vows—of perpetual celibacy, poverty, and obedience—are sinful entanglements. The same God who commands truth-telling also hears every oath and holds every swearer accountable.

**A Prayer from Chapter 23** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O God, whose name is glorious and dreadful: We confess that we have often taken oaths lightly and spoken words we did not mean. We have equivocated and hidden behind mental reservations. We have sworn by created things as if they had authority. Forgive us. Grant us to take oaths only in Thy name, with truth, righteousness, and judgment, and to perform our vows faithfully. Keep us from the superstitious snares of monastic vows, and let our yes be yes and our no be no. For Christ's sake. Amen.*
