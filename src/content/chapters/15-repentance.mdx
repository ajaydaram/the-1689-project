# The Door That Must Be Entered: Why Repentance Unto Life Is Inseparable from Saving Faith

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 15 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Other Half of the Gospel

Chapter 15 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled “Of Repentance Unto Life,” stands as the necessary complement to saving faith.[^1] If Chapter 14 describes the *hand* that receives Christ, Chapter 15 describes the *heart* that turns from sin. As the Baptist Catechism states, “Repentance unto life is a saving grace, whereby a sinner, out of a true sense of his sin, and apprehension of the mercy of God in Christ, doth, with grief and hatred of his sin, turn from it unto God, with full purpose of, and endeavor after, new obedience.”[^2]

The chapter comprises five paragraphs, each building on the previous. Paragraph 1 distinguishes between those born in infancy and those converted later in life, affirming that repentance is given to all who are effectually called. Paragraph 2 addresses the reality of sin even in believers and the provision for repentance in the covenant of grace. Paragraph 3 defines the nature of saving repentance, including its intellectual, emotional, and volitional elements. Paragraph 4 declares that repentance must continue throughout the believer’s life. Paragraph 5 affirms that no sin is too great for pardoning mercy, while warning against presumptuous sin.[^3]

As Sam Waldron notes, “Repentance is not a work that earns salvation; it is a grace that accompanies faith and flows from the new birth.”[^4]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Repentance

Chapter 15 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on the nature, necessity, and permanence of repentance.[^5]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Recipients of Repentance

The confession opens by addressing a question about the timing of conversion: **“Such of the elect as are converted at riper years, having sometime lived in the state of nature, and therein served divers lusts and pleasures, God in their effectual calling giveth them repentance unto life.”**[^6]

**1. The Distinction in Conversion.** “Such of the elect as are converted at riper years.”[^7] The confession acknowledges that not all believers are converted in childhood. Some live for a time “in the state of nature” (unregenerate), serving “divers lusts and pleasures.”[^8] The confession cites Titus 3:2-5: “For we ourselves were once foolish, disobedient, led astray, slaves to various passions and pleasures… but when the goodness and loving kindness of God our Savior appeared, he saved us.”[^9]

**2. Repentance Is Given, Not Earned.** “God in their effectual calling giveth them repentance unto life.”[^10] Repentance is not a human achievement or a precondition for salvation; it is a gift that accompanies effectual calling. Acts 11:18 declares, “Then to the Gentiles also God has granted repentance that leads to life.”[^11] 2 Timothy 2:25-26 speaks of God “perhaps granting them repentance leading to a knowledge of the truth.”[^12]

**3. Repentance Is “Unto Life.”** The phrase “repentance unto life” (Acts 11:18) distinguishes saving repentance from the mere remorse of Judas or Esau (Hebrews 12:17). Saving repentance leads to eternal life because it is united to faith in Christ.[^13]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Titus 3:2-5; Acts 11:18; 2 Timothy 2:25-26.[^14]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Provision for Repentance in the Covenant of Grace

Paragraph 2 addresses the reality of sin in believers and God’s gracious provision: **“Whereas there is no man that doth good and sinneth not; and the best of men may, through the power and deceitfulness of their corruption dwelling in them, with the prevalency of temptation, fall into great sins and provocations; God hath, in the covenant of grace, mercifully provided that believers so sinning and falling be renewed through repentance unto salvation.”**[^15]

**1. The Reality of Sin in Believers.** “There is no man that doth good and sinneth not.”[^16] The confession cites Ecclesiastes 7:20: “Surely there is not a righteous man on earth who does good and never sins.”[^17] Even “the best of men” may fall “into great sins and provocations” through “the power and deceitfulness of their corruption” and “the prevalency of temptation.”[^18]

**2. The Example of Peter.** The proof text, Luke 22:31-32, records Christ’s words to Peter: “Simon, Simon, behold, Satan demanded to have you, that he might sift you like wheat, but I have prayed for you that your faith may not fail. And when you have turned again, strengthen your brothers.”[^19] Peter’s denial was a great sin, but Christ’s prayer secured his repentance.

**3. The Provision in the Covenant of Grace.** “God hath, in the covenant of grace, mercifully provided that believers so sinning and falling be renewed through repentance unto salvation.”[^20] The covenant of grace is not merely a provision for initial conversion; it includes the ongoing mercy of repentance for believers who fall into sin.[^12]

**4. “Renewed unto Salvation.”** The confession carefully distinguishes between falling into sin and falling from salvation. Believers who sin are “renewed through repentance unto salvation”—not re-justified (their justification remains secure), but restored to fellowship and renewed in their assurance.[^21]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Ecclesiastes 7:20; Luke 22:31-32; 1 John 1:7, 9; 2 Corinthians 7:10.[^22]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Nature of Saving Repentance

Paragraph 3 is the theological heart of the chapter, defining the essential elements of repentance: **“This saving repentance is an evangelical grace, wherein a person, being by the Spirit of God made sensible of the manifold evils of his sin, doth, by faith in Christ, humble himself for it with godly sorrow, hatred of it, and self-abhorrency, praying for pardon and strength of grace, with a purpose and endeavor, by supplies of the Spirit, to walk before God unto all well-pleasing in all things.”**[^23]

This definition, drawn largely from the Westminster Larger Catechism, identifies three essential elements of repentance: intellectual, emotional, and volitional.[^24]

**1. Repentance Is “an Evangelical Grace.”** “Evangelical” (from *euangelion*, “gospel”) means that repentance is not a natural moral reformation but a grace that flows from the gospel.[^25] It is not a work that earns salvation but a gift that accompanies faith.[^26]

**2. The Intellectual Element: Sense of Sin’s Manifold Evils.** “A person, being by the Spirit of God made sensible of the manifold evils of his sin.”[^27] The first act of repentance is a Spirit-wrought awareness of the “manifold evils” of sin—not merely its external consequences, but its intrinsic wickedness as rebellion against a holy God. The confession cites Zechariah 12:10: “They will look on me whom they have pierced; they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child.”[^28]

**3. The Emotional Element: Godly Sorrow, Hatred, and Self-Abhorrency.** “Doth, by faith in Christ, humble himself for it with godly sorrow, hatred of it, and self-abhorrency.”[^29] The confession identifies three distinct affections:

- **Godly sorrow (2 Corinthians 7:10)** – sorrow not merely over the consequences of sin but over the offense against God.[^30]
- **Hatred of sin** – not merely regret but active loathing of the sin itself.
- **Self-abhorrency** – a deep sense of one’s own worthlessness and defilement before a holy God. Ezekiel 36:31 declares, “You will remember your evil ways and your deeds that were not good, and you will loathe yourselves for your iniquities and your abominations.”[^31]

**4. The Volitional Element: Purpose and Endeavor.** “Praying for pardon and strength of grace, with a purpose and endeavor, by supplies of the Spirit, to walk before God unto all well-pleasing in all things.”[^32] Repentance is not merely feeling sorry; it includes a resolute turning from sin to obedience. Psalm 119:6 and 128 speak of the psalmist’s resolve to keep God’s precepts.[^23]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Zechariah 12:10; Acts 11:18; Ezekiel 36:31; 2 Corinthians 7:11; Psalm 119:6, 128.[^33]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Perpetuity of Repentance

The confession declares that repentance is not a one-time event but a lifelong posture: **“As repentance is to be continued through the whole course of our lives, upon the account of the body of death, and the motions thereof, so it is every man’s duty to repent of his particular known sins particularly.”**[^34]

**1. Repentance Is Lifelong.** “As repentance is to be continued through the whole course of our lives, upon the account of the body of death, and the motions thereof.”[^35] The “body of death” (Romans 7:24) remains in believers; therefore, the need for repentance remains. Luke 19:8 records Zacchaeus’s specific repentance: “If I have defrauded anyone of anything, I restore it fourfold.”[^36]

**2. Particular Sins Require Particular Repentance.** “So it is every man’s duty to repent of his particular known sins particularly.”[^37] The confession rejects a vague, general confession that never names specific sins. 1 Timothy 1:13 and 1:15 record Paul’s specific acknowledgment: “Formerly I was a blasphemer, persecutor, and insolent opponent… Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners, of whom I am the foremost.”[^38]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Luke 19:8; 1 Timothy 1:13, 15.[^39]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Extent of Pardoning Mercy

The confession concludes with a declaration of hope and a warning: **“Such is the provision which God hath made through Christ in the covenant of grace for the preservation of believers unto salvation, that although there is no sin so small but it deserves damnation, yet there is no sin so great that it shall bring damnation on them that repent; which makes the constant preaching of repentance necessary.”**[^40]

**1. The Seriousness of All Sin.** “Although there is no sin so small but it deserves damnation.”[^41] Romans 6:23 declares, “The wages of sin is death.” Even the “smallest” sin is an offense against an infinitely holy God and therefore deserves eternal punishment.[^42]

**2. The Greatness of Pardoning Mercy.** “Yet there is no sin so great that it shall bring damnation on them that repent.”[^43] The confession cites Isaiah 1:16-18 and 55:7. Isaiah 1:18 invites, “Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow; though they are red like crimson, they shall become like wool.”[^44]

**3. The Necessity of Constant Preaching of Repentance.** “Which makes the constant preaching of repentance necessary.”[^45] The gospel is not “believe and you will be saved, now live as you please.” The gospel includes the call to repent. Christ’s first sermon began, “Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand” (Matthew 4:17). The apostles preached “repentance and forgiveness of sins” (Luke 24:47). The confession thus rejects antinomianism and hyper-Calvinism.[^46]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Romans 6:23; Isaiah 1:16-18; 55:7.[^47]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Repentance

Several theological pillars support the confession’s doctrine of repentance.[^48]

**Pillar 1: Repentance Is a Grace, Not a Work.** The confession defines repentance as “an evangelical grace” (paragraph 3). It is not a human achievement that earns salvation but a gift of God that accompanies faith.[^49]

**Pillar 2: Repentance Is Inseparable from Faith.** While distinct from faith, repentance never exists apart from faith. The same Spirit who enables the sinner to believe also enables him to repent. The confession’s phrase “by faith in Christ” (paragraph 3) ties repentance to faith.[^50]

**Pillar 3: Repentance Is More Than Sorrow.** True repentance includes intellectual awareness, emotional sorrow and hatred of sin, and volitional turning to obedience. The confession rejects mere remorse (such as Judas felt).[^51]

**Pillar 4: Repentance Is Lifelong.** The “body of death” remains in believers; therefore, repentance must continue throughout the Christian life. This guards against perfectionism.[^52]

**Pillar 5: Repentance Is Specific.** Believers must repent of particular known sins, not merely confess in general terms. This guards against a lazy, generic “forgive me for everything.”[^53]

**Pillar 6: No Sin Is Unforgivable to the Repentant.** The confession offers great hope: no matter how great the sin, if one repents, there is pardon. This does not encourage presumption but offers comfort to the guilt-ridden.[^54]

**Pillar 7: Constant Preaching of Repentance Is Necessary.** The confession rejects any form of Christianity that divorces faith from repentance. The gospel call is always a call to repent and believe.[^55]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Repentance

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 15.[^56]

**Crisis 1: The Antinomian Neglect of Repentance.** Some forms of Christianity preach faith without repentance, treating repentance as a “work” that threatens grace. The confession’s insistence that repentance is an “evangelical grace” (not a human work) and that “constant preaching of repentance is necessary” corrects this.[^57]

**Crisis 2: The Legalistic Reduction of Repentance to Emotional Sorrow.** Many confuse remorse with repentance. The confession’s emphasis on “hatred of sin” and “purpose and endeavor to walk before God unto all well-pleasing” shows that true repentance issues in changed behavior.[^58]

**Crisis 3: The Presumption of Future Repentance.** Some sin willfully, assuming they can repent later. The confession warns that “there is no sin so small but it deserves damnation.” While mercy is available, presumption is dangerous.[^59]

**Crisis 4: The Despair of the Guilty Conscience.** Many believers feel that their sins are too great to be forgiven. The confession declares that “there is no sin so great that it shall bring damnation on them that repent.” This is the comfort of the gospel.[^60]

**Crisis 5: The Neglect of Specific Confession.** Many believers content themselves with vague, general confessions that never address particular sins. The confession’s call to repent “of his particular known sins particularly” is a needed corrective.[^61]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Repentance

**Step 1: Recognize Repentance as a Gift.** Do not boast in your repentance as if it were your own achievement. Thank God for granting you repentance.[^62]

**Step 2: Practice Daily, Specific Confession.** Do not content yourself with vague generalities. Name your sins: “I was impatient with my child. I harbored resentment toward my spouse. I indulged lustful thoughts.” Confess them specifically.[^63]

**Step 3: Distinguish Godly Sorrow from Worldly Sorrow.** Worldly sorrow regrets the consequences of sin; godly sorrow regrets the offense against God. Examine your heart: why are you sorry?[^64]

**Step 4: Turn from Sin to Obedience.** Repentance is not merely feeling sorry; it is turning. Identify the specific actions that need to change, and by the Spirit’s power, change them.[^65]

**Step 5: Receive the Comfort of Pardoning Mercy.** If you have sinned greatly, do not despair. The same covenant of grace that provided repentance provides pardon. Return to the Father; He will receive you.[^66]

**Step 6: Preach Repentance to Yourself and Others.** Do not assume that repentance is only for the unconverted. Believers need to hear the call to repent daily. Make the preaching of repentance a regular part of your family worship and personal devotion.[^67]

**Step 7: Rejoice in the Covenant of Grace.** The provision for repentance is not a sign of God’s unwillingness to save but of His mercy. He has made ample provision for His falling children to be restored. Take heart.[^68]

### Conclusion: The Door That Must Be Entered

Chapter 15 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer’s guide to the grace of repentance. Repentance is not a work that earns salvation but a grace that accompanies faith. It is not a one-time event but a lifelong posture. It is not vague sorrow but specific turning from known sins to known obedience. And it is not a threat to assurance but the ground of assurance—for the same covenant of grace that justifies also provides the ongoing mercy of repentance for falling believers.[^69]

The confession calls believers to recognize repentance as a gift, to practice daily specific confession, to distinguish godly sorrow from worldly sorrow, to turn from sin to obedience, to receive the comfort of pardoning mercy, to preach repentance to themselves and others, and to rejoice in the covenant of grace. The door to life is the door of repentance. Enter it daily.[^70]

**A Prayer from Chapter 15** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O gracious God, who hast granted repentance unto life to Thy elect: We confess that we have often treated repentance as a work rather than a gift. We have been content with vague confessions rather than specific acknowledgment of our sins. We have mourned the consequences rather than the offense against Thy holiness. Forgive us.*

*Grant us, by Thy Spirit, to be made sensible of the manifold evils of our sin. Give us godly sorrow, hatred of sin, and self-abhorrency. Enable us to turn from our particular known sins particularly, and to walk before Thee unto all well-pleasing in all things. When we fall, renew us through repentance unto salvation. And make us to rejoice in the covenant of grace, wherein Thou hast provided pardon even for the greatest sins of the penitent. For Christ’s sake. Amen.*[^71]
