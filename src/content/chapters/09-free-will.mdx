# The Enslaved Will Set Free: Why the Doctrine of Free Will Is the Battlefield for the Gospel

### Introduction: The Doctrine You Cannot Ignore

Chapter 9 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Free Will," stands as one of the most misunderstood, maligned, and yet pastorally essential chapters in the entire confession.[^1] If Chapter 6 diagnoses the universal disease of sin, Chapter 7 announces the covenant structure of redemption, and Chapter 8 unveils the Person of the Mediator, then Chapter 9 explains the *condition of the human will* that the gospel must address.[^2]

The doctrine of free will is not a philosophical luxury for academic theologians. It is the battlefield upon which the gospel is won or lost. As Luther wrote in his magisterial *The Bondage of the Will*, the question of whether the human will is free to choose spiritual good is "the hinge on which our whole controversy turns."[^3]

Sam Waldron notes that the chapter is broken down into five paragraphs, each describing a different state of the human will:[^4]

- **Paragraph 1**: The state of innocency (Adam before the fall)
- **Paragraph 2**: The state of corruption (fallen man after the fall)
- **Paragraph 3**: The state of grace (regenerate man after conversion)
- **Paragraph 4**: The state of operation (the relationship between God's grace and the human will in conversion)
- **Paragraph 5**: The state of glory (the redeemed will in heaven)

The arrangement is deliberate and pastoral. The confession moves from what the will *was*, to what it *became*, to what it *becomes* in grace, to how grace *operates*, to what the will will *be* in glory.[^5] This is the *ordo salutis* from the perspective of the human will—a theological anatomy that exposes the desperate condition of fallen humanity and magnifies the sheer grace of God in salvation.[^6]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Human Will

Chapter 9 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the human will in its four states. The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—anchored every assertion in the Word of God, refusing to speculate beyond what is written.[^7]

#### Paragraph 1 – The State of Innocency: The Created Will

The confession opens with a definition of free will in its original created state: **"God hath endued the will of man with that natural liberty and power of acting upon choice, that it is neither forced, nor by any necessity of nature determined to do good or evil."**[^8]

This is the confession's definition of free will as a natural faculty.[^9] Sam Waldron explains that the phrase "God hath endued" indicates that natural liberty is a gift from God, not an inherent property of the soul.[^10]

**1. "Natural Liberty and Power of Acting upon Choice":** The confession affirms that the human will possesses the ability to choose among options based on deliberation and desire. James 1:14 declares, "Each person is tempted when he is lured and enticed by his own desire."[^11]

**2. "Neither Forced, nor by Any Necessity of Nature Determined to do Good or Evil":** The confession denies two extremes: (a) external compulsion (the will is not forced from outside), and (b) natural necessity (the will is not inherently determined to good or evil apart from moral disposition).[^12] As Waldron states, "This is an affirmation of a kind of free will. It is not *absolute* free will. It is *natural* free will."[^13]

The confession cites Matthew 17:12 (free actions of men), James 1:14 (desire precedes choice), and Deuteronomy 30:19 (the choice between life and death).[^14]

**But the confession immediately qualifies this natural liberty:** **"Man, in his state of innocency, had freedom and power to will and to do that which is good and well-pleasing to God, but yet was unstable, so that he might fall from it."**[^15]

**3. The Freedom and Power of Adam:** Adam possessed *posse peccare, posse non peccare* —the ability to sin and the ability not to sin.[^16] He had both the freedom and the power to obey God's commands. As Ecclesiastes 7:29 declares, "God made man upright, but they have sought out many schemes."[^17]

**4. The Mutability of Adam's Will:** "But yet was unstable, so that he might fall from it."[^18] Adam's original righteousness was real but not confirmed. He had the power to stand but not the immutability to guarantee that he would. His will was *mutable*, and history proved its mutability.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Matthew 17:12; James 1:14; Deuteronomy 30:19; Ecclesiastes 7:29; Genesis 3:6.[^19]

#### Paragraph 2 – The State of Corruption: The Fallen Will

Having described the original condition of the will, the confession now announces the catastrophic effects of the fall: **"Man, by his fall into a state of sin, hath wholly lost all ability of will to any spiritual good accompanying salvation; so as a natural man, being altogether averse from that good, and dead in sin, is not able, by his own strength, to convert himself, or to prepare himself thereunto."**[^20]

This is the confession's statement of **total inability**—the doctrine that fallen man cannot of his own free will choose spiritual good.[^21]

**1. "Wholly Lost All Ability of Will to Any Spiritual Good Accompanying Salvation":** The fall did not remove the natural faculty of the will; it removed the *moral ability* to choose what is spiritually good.[^22] As Waldron explains, "Adam lost original righteousness, and with it his original ability to do spiritual good."[^23] This is the total depravity of the will—not that the will no longer chooses, but that it can only choose evil.

**2. "A Natural Man, Being Altogether Averse from That Good":** The term "natural man" (Greek *psychikos anthropos*, 1 Corinthians 2:14) describes the unregenerate person. His will is not neutral but "altogether averse from that good."[^24] Romans 8:7 declares, "The mind that is set on the flesh is hostile to God, for it does not submit to God's law; indeed, it cannot."[^25]

**3. "Dead in Sin":** Ephesians 2:1 states, "You were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked."[^26] The will is not merely sick or wounded; it is *dead*. As Waldron writes, "Dead people do not have the capacity to raise themselves."[^27]

**4. "Not Able, by His Own Strength, to Convert Himself, or to Prepare Himself Thereunto":** The confession explicitly rejects the doctrine that fallen man can "prepare himself" for conversion—an error associated with Semi-Pelagianism.[^28] The unregenerate cannot so much as take the first step toward salvation.[^29]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** John 15:5; Romans 8:7-8; 1 Corinthians 2:14; Ephesians 2:1; John 6:44; Genesis 6:5; Romans 3:9-12.[^30]

#### Paragraph 3 – The State of Grace: The Regenerate Will

Having described the utter helplessness of fallen man, the confession now announces the miracle of regeneration: **"When God converts a sinner, and translates him into the state of grace, He freeth him from his natural bondage under sin, and by His grace alone enables him freely to will and to do that which is spiritually good; yet so as that by reason of his remaining corruption, he doth not perfectly, nor only, will that which is good, but doth also will that which is evil."**[^31]

This paragraph describes the regenerate will—the state of grace.[^32]

**1. "When God Converts a Sinner... He Freeth Him from His Natural Bondage Under Sin":** Regeneration is a divine act, not a human achievement. God *frees* the sinner—the passive voice indicates that the sinner is acted upon, not acting.[^33] Colossians 1:13 declares, "He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son."[^34]

**2. "By His Grace Alone Enables Him Freely to Will and to Do That Which Is Spiritually Good":** The confession is emphatic: the ability to will good comes "by His grace alone."[^35] This is not a cooperation between God and a partially capable sinner; it is a sovereign enabling. Yet the enabled will is truly free—it "freely wills" that which is good.[^36]

**3. The Persistence of Indwelling Sin:** "Yet so as that by reason of his remaining corruption, he doth not perfectly, nor only, will that which is good, but doth also will that which is evil."[^37] This is the *status mixtus*—the mixed state of the believer. Indwelling sin remains (Romans 7:18-25; Galatians 5:17).[^38] The regenerate will is not perfectly or exclusively good. The believer still wills evil, though not as his dominant or delighting disposition.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Colossians 1:13; John 8:36; Romans 6:18; Philippians 2:13; Romans 7:15-25; Galatians 5:17.[^39]

#### Paragraph 4 – The State of Operation: The Sovereign Enabling of Grace

Paragraph 4 is the most theologically precise in the chapter, explaining *how* God enables the will: **"This will of man is made perfectly and immutably free to do good alone in the state of glory only."**[^40]

This paragraph describes the final state of the will—the state of glory. In heaven, the redeemed will will be "perfectly and immutably free to do good alone."[^41] This is *non posse peccare*—not able to sin. The will that was created able to sin (paragraph 1), became fallen and able only to sin (paragraph 2), and is in this life able to sin and able not to sin (paragraph 3), will finally become not able to sin (paragraph 5).[^42]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Revelation 22:11; Hebrews 12:23; 1 John 3:2; Philippians 3:21.[^43]

#### Paragraph 5 – The State of Glory: The Perfected Will

The confession concludes with the eschatological perfection of the will: **"This will of man is made perfectly and immutably free to do good alone in the state of glory only."**[^44]

This is the final state—the glorified will. The will that was created mutable and fell, that is regenerated but still struggles, will finally be confirmed in righteousness. The redeemed will will have the freedom not to sin—the freedom that Adam lost and that only Christ can restore.[^45]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Revelation 22:11; Hebrews 12:23; 1 John 3:2; Philippians 3:21.[^46]

### Part 2: Historical and Theological Anatomy – The Fourfold State of the Will

The confession's doctrine of free will is not a novel invention. It is the mature fruit of centuries of theological reflection, crystallized in Augustine's anti-Pelagian writings, defended by the Reformers, and codified in the Reformed confessions.[^47]

**The Pelagian Heresy:** Pelagius (4th-5th century) taught that Adam's sin affected only Adam himself; that every human being is born with the same moral capacity as Adam; and that it is possible—at least in principle—to live a sinless life without divine grace.[^48] The confession's paragraph 2 directly refutes this: fallen man has "wholly lost all ability of will to any spiritual good."[^49]

**The Semi-Pelagian Compromise:** The Semi-Pelagians taught that fallen man retains the ability to take the first step toward salvation—to "prepare himself" for grace—after which God cooperates.[^50] The confession rejects this explicitly: the fallen will is "not able, by his own strength, to convert himself, or to prepare himself thereunto."[^51]

**The Augustinian and Reformed Orthodoxy:** Augustine taught that fallen man is in bondage to sin and can only be freed by the sovereign grace of God. The confession follows this tradition: "When God converts a sinner... He freeth him from his natural bondage under sin, and by His grace alone enables him freely to will and to do that which is spiritually good."[^52]

**The Fourfold State:** Thomas Boston famously systematized the four states of man: (1) created able to sin and able not to sin; (2) fallen able only to sin; (3) regenerated able to sin and able not to sin; (4) glorified not able to sin.[^53] The confession's five paragraphs follow this exact pattern.

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Doctrine of the Will

The confession's doctrine of free will is not a speculative anthropology; it is a pastoral necessity. Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 9.[^54]

**Crisis 1: The Pelagian Assumption of the Modern Heart.** The default setting of the modern heart is Pelagian: "I can choose God whenever I want." The confession's paragraph 2 confronts this deadly assumption. The unregenerate do not merely need encouragement; they need resurrection.[^55]

**Crisis 2: The Semi-Pelagian Evangelicalism.** Many evangelicals functionally believe that fallen man retains the ability to "decide for Christ" without any prior enabling grace. The confession's paragraphs 2 and 4 reject this: God must first free the will before it can will spiritual good.[^56]

**Crisis 3: The Hyper-Calvinist Denial of Means.** The confession does not teach passivity. The regenerate will is truly free and must be exercised. Paragraph 3 declares that the redeemed "freely wills and does that which is spiritually good." Thus, we must exhort, warn, and encourage believers to use their freed will in obedience.[^57]

**Crisis 4: The Perfectionist Error.** Paragraph 3 insists that even the regenerate will is not perfectly or exclusively good. Believers still struggle with remaining sin. Perfectionism leads either to despair (when it fails) or to hypocrisy (when it pretends to succeed).[^58]

**Crisis 5: The Loss of Hope for Final Deliverance.** Paragraph 5 promises that the will will be made "perfectly and immutably free to do good alone in the state of glory." The believer's struggle with indwelling sin is not eternal. The day is coming when the will will be fully liberated.[^59]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Will's Fourfold State

The confession's doctrine of the will is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are five practical steps derived from Chapter 9.[^60]

**Step 1: Preach the Inability of Man.** Preach the total inability of fallen man to choose spiritual good. This is not to discourage but to drive sinners out of themselves and to Christ alone.[^61]

**Step 2: Pray for the Sovereign Enabling of the Will.** Do not assume that persuasion alone can convert. Pray that God would "freeth him from his natural bondage under sin."[^62]

**Step 3: Exhort Believers to Exercise Their Freed Will.** The regenerate will is truly free. Exhort believers to obedience, to vigilance, to the use of means.[^63]

**Step 4: Counsel Struggling Believers with Honesty and Hope.** Counsel believers who struggle with indwelling sin to be honest about their remaining corruption, but to look forward to the day when the will will be made "perfectly and immutably free."[^64]

**Step 5: Look Forward to the Glory of the Perfected Will.** The believer's struggle with indwelling sin is not eternal. The day is coming when the will will be fully liberated. Live in hope of that final deliverance.[^65]

### Conclusion: The Bondage of the Will and the Freedom of Grace

Chapter 9 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's anatomy of the will—a diagnostic tool that exposes the human condition and magnifies the grace of God. The doctrine of free will is the hinge on which the gospel turns. If fallen man is free to choose God without grace, then grace is not grace; the cross is not necessary; and the Spirit is not sovereign. But if fallen man is in bondage to sin, and God in His sovereign grace frees the will to choose Christ, then salvation is entirely of the Lord.[^66]

**A Prayer from Chapter 9 (adapted from the Puritan tradition):**

*O Lord, we confess that by nature our wills are in bondage to sin. We cannot will the good; we cannot choose the true; we cannot love the holy. We are dead in our trespasses and sins. But we thank Thee that Thou dost not leave us in our bondage. By Thy sovereign grace, Thou dost free us from our natural bondage under sin. Thou dost enable us freely to will and to do that which is spiritually good. And we look forward to that day when our wills shall be made perfectly and immutably free to do good alone, in the state of glory.*

*Until that day, grant us to will and to work for Thy good pleasure. In Jesus' name. Amen.*[^67]
