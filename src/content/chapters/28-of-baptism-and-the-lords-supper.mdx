# The Visible Doors to Invisible Realities: Why Baptism and the Lord's Supper Are Means of Grace, Not Merits for Justification

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 28 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: A Brief but Crucial Chapter

Chapter 28 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Baptism and the Lord's Supper," is one of the shortest chapters in the entire confession, comprising only two paragraphs.[^1] Yet its brevity does not indicate unimportance. As one expositor notes, "Though this chapter is very short, yet it is important. The subject matter is of immense significance for the church's life, for the worship of God, for the sanctification of the saints, and for the glory of God."[^2]

The chapter serves as a theological introduction to the two ordinances (or "institutions") that Christ gave specifically to the New Covenant church: baptism (treated in Chapter 29) and the Lord's supper (treated in Chapter 30).[^3] Chapter 28 establishes the **foundational principles** that govern both ordinances.[^4]

The chapter is a direct expression of the **Baptist distinction** between sacraments and institutions. The compilers deliberately used the term "ordinances" (or "institutions") rather than "sacraments" to distance themselves from both Roman Catholic and magisterial Reformed teaching.[^5] As one commentary explains, the framers "wanted to avoid any idea that the ordinances convey saving grace automatically or *ex opere operato* (by the work performed)."[^6]

The chapter's two paragraphs cover: (1) the **divine institution and perpetuity** of baptism and the Lord's supper; and (2) the **proper administrators** of these ordinances.[^7]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Baptism and the Lord's Supper

Chapter 28 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature and administration of the two New Covenant ordinances.[^8]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Divine Institution and Perpetuity of the Ordinances

The confession opens: <ConfessionBox>
"Baptism and the Lord's Supper are ordinances of positive and sovereign institution, appointed by the Lord Jesus, the only lawgiver, to be continued in his church to the end of the world."
</ConfessionBox>[^9]

**1. "Baptism and the Lord's Supper are ordinances."** The confession uses the term "ordinances" (Latin *ordino*: "to set in order, appoint").[^10] This term, rather than "sacraments," reflects the Baptist rejection of any notion that these rites automatically convey grace apart from faith.

**2. "Of positive and sovereign institution."** The phrase "positive institution" means that the ordinances have no inherent moral content and are not derived from the law of nature. They are "positive" because they are commanded by God's free will, not because they are necessary by nature.[^11] As one exposition explains, the phrase "positive and sovereign institution" emphasizes that baptism and the Lord's supper are not human inventions or church traditions but "are found in God's Word, commanded by God, and are binding upon the church."[^12]

**3. "Appointed by the Lord Jesus, the only lawgiver."** The confession explicitly grounds the ordinances in Christ's authority. James 4:12 declares, "There is only one lawgiver and judge, he who is able to save and to destroy."[^13] Matthew 28:18-20 records the Great Commission: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit."[^14] 1 Corinthians 11:23 records Paul's words: "For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you, that the Lord Jesus on the night when he was betrayed took bread."[^15]

**4. "To be continued in his church to the end of the world."** The ordinances are not temporary; they are perpetual until Christ returns. The confession cites Matthew 28:19-20, where Christ promises, "Behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."[^16] 1 Corinthians 11:26 declares, "For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes."[^17]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Matthew 28:18-20; 1 Corinthians 11:23-26.[^18]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Proper Administrators of the Ordinances

Paragraph 2 states: <ConfessionBox>
"These holy appointments are to be administered by those who are qualified and called thereunto, according to the commission of Christ."
</ConfessionBox>[^19]

**1. "These holy appointments are to be administered by those who are qualified and called thereunto."** The confession limits the administration of the ordinances to duly qualified and called officers of the church. The confession cites Matthew 28:19 (the Great Commission given to the apostles and their successors in office).[^20]

**2. "According to the commission of Christ."** The commission of Christ (Matthew 28:18-20) is the charter for all ministerial authority in administering the ordinances.[^21]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Matthew 28:19; 1 Corinthians 4:1; Hebrews 5:4.[^22]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Baptist Ordinance Theology

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of baptism and the Lord's supper.

**Pillar 1: Ordinances, Not Sacraments.** The confession deliberately avoids the term "sacrament" to distance itself from any notion that the ordinances convey grace automatically or *ex opere operato* (by the work performed).[^23]

**Pillar 2: Positive and Sovereign Institution.** The ordinances have no inherent moral power. They are effectual only because Christ appointed them and promises to bless them.[^24]

**Pillar 3: Perpetual Obligation.** The ordinances are not temporary ceremonies of the Old Covenant. They are binding on the church until Christ returns.[^25]

**Pillar 4: Qualified Administrators.** Only those who are called according to Christ's commission may administer the ordinances. This excludes lay administration.[^26]

**Pillar 5: The Sufficiency of Scripture.** Everything necessary for the church's ordinance life is contained in the Word of God.

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Ordinance Theology

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 28.

**Crisis 1: The Roman Catholic Doctrine of *Ex Opere Operato*.** Roman Catholicism teaches that the sacraments confer grace automatically, regardless of the faith of the recipient. The confession's use of "ordinances" rather than "sacraments" rejects this error.[^27]

**Crisis 2: The Magisterial Reformed Practice of Infant Baptism.** The confession does not address baptism directly here, but the stage is set for the exclusive immersion of believers in Chapter 29.

**Crisis 3: The Denial of the Ordinances' Perpetuity.** Some Christian traditions (such as Quakers) reject the outward ordinances altogether. The confession affirms that they are "to be continued in his church to the end of the world."[^28]

**Crisis 4: The Neglect of the Lord's Supper.** Many churches relegate the Lord's supper to an occasional, optional observance. The confession affirms its perpetual obligation.[^29]

**Crisis 5: Unqualified Administration.** Some churches permit laypersons to baptize or preside at the Lord's supper. The confession restricts administration to those "qualified and called thereunto according to the commission of Christ."[^30]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Ordinances

**Step 1: Receive the Ordinances as Christ's Appointments.** Do not treat baptism and the Lord's supper as mere human traditions or optional rituals. They are commanded by Christ.[^31]

**Step 2: Do Not Trust in the Ordinances for Salvation.** The ordinances are not means of saving grace in themselves. They are signs and seals of grace to those who receive them by faith.[^32]

**Step 3: Look Beyond the Visible to the Invisible.** In baptism, see the promise of cleansing by the blood of Christ. In the Lord's supper, see the broken body and shed blood of your Savior.[^33]

**Step 4: Partake Worthily.** Examine yourself before coming to the Lord's table. Do not come with unconfessed sin or in an unworthy manner.[^34]

**Step 5: Administer the Ordinances Only by Qualified Men.** Do not allow private baptism or lay presidency at the Lord's table. Follow Christ's commission.[^35]

**Step 6: Continue to the End.** The ordinances are perpetual. Do not neglect them. The Lord's supper proclaims the Lord's death "until he comes."[^36]

**Step 7: Unite the Visible and Invisible Church.** The ordinances are visible signs that point to invisible realities. In receiving them, believers confess their union with Christ and with every other believer.[^37]

### Conclusion: The Visible Doors to Invisible Realities

Chapter 28 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the visible doors that open onto invisible realities. Baptism and the Lord's supper are not human inventions but divine institutions. They are not temporary but perpetual. They are to be administered only by those qualified and called according to Christ's commission. And they are not to be trusted as automatic conveyors of grace but received as signs and seals of grace to those who believe.[^38]

The confession does not here define the meaning of the ordinances. It merely establishes their foundation. The substance of baptism and the Lord's supper await fuller treatment in Chapters 29 and 30. But the foundation is laid: these are Christ's institutions, for Christ's church, administered by Christ's ministers, to Christ's glory, until Christ returns.[^39]

**A Prayer from Chapter 28** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Lord Jesus, the only lawgiver and head of the church: We thank Thee for the ordinances of baptism and the Lord's supper. We thank Thee that Thou hast appointed them, not as burdensome ceremonies, but as visible signs of invisible grace. We thank Thee that in baptism we see the washing of regeneration. We thank Thee that in the breaking of bread we see Thy broken body and shed blood. Grant us to receive these ordinances with faith, to partake worthily, and to continue in them until Thou comest again. Let them not be empty rituals, but means of grace that strengthen our union with Thee and with all the saints. For Thy name's sake. Amen.*[^40]
