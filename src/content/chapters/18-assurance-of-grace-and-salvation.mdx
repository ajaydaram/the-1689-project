# The Certainty That Does Not Deceive: Why Assurance Is the Believer's Birthright, Not a Dangerous Presumption

Chapter 18 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Assurance of Grace and Salvation," stands as one of the most pastorally sensitive and theologically precise chapters in the entire confession.[^1] If Chapter 17 declares the objective fact of the saints' perseverance—that those truly united to Christ will never finally fall away—then Chapter 18 addresses the subjective experience of that perseverance: the believer's personal certainty that he is, in fact, a child of God.[^2] As Sam Waldron notes, "Chapter 18 does not add a new article to the ordo salutis; it describes the reflex act of faith by which the believer knows that he believes."[^3] The chapter's four paragraphs assert that assurance is (1) possible, (2) infallible, (3) attainable, and (4) variable.[^4]

The historical background of this chapter is crucial. Two errors shaped its formulation. The first was the Roman Catholic denial of assurance. The Council of Trent (1547) anathematized anyone who said that "a justified man... can be certain that he is in the state of grace" apart from a special revelation.[^5] Rome taught that assurance could only come by an extraordinary revelation to select saints, but was dangerous for the ordinary Christian.[^6] The second error was that of the antinomian Protestants, who claimed a high assurance of salvation but without corresponding holiness of life.[^7] Against these extremes, the confession asserts that assurance is both possible and beneficial, while also warning against the danger of false assurance and insisting that genuine assurance produces humility and holiness.[^8]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Assurance

Chapter 18 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the believer's certainty of salvation. The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—grounded every assertion in the Word of God, drawing especially on the Johannine epistles, the Pauline letters, and the Epistle to the Hebrews.[^9]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Possibility of Assurance: Distinguishing False Hope from True Certainty

The confession opens by contrasting false assurance with true assurance: **"Although temporary believers and other unregenerate men, may vainly deceive themselves with false hopes and carnal presumptions of being in the favor of God and in a state of salvation, which hope of theirs shall perish; yet such as truly believe in the Lord Jesus, and love him in sincerity, endeavouring to walk in all good conscience before him, may in this life be certainly assured that they are in the state of grace, and may rejoice in the hope of the glory of God, which hope shall never make them ashamed."**[^10]

**1. The Danger of False Assurance.** The confession begins by warning of the existence of false assurance. "Temporary believers" (a reference to Matthew 13:20-21, the rocky soil hearer who receives the word with joy but falls away in time of testing) and "other unregenerate men may vainly deceive themselves with false hopes and carnal presumptions of being in the favor of God."[^11] Job 8:13-14 declares, "Such are the paths of all who forget God; the hope of the godless shall perish." Matthew 7:22-23 records those who cry "Lord, Lord" but are told, "I never knew you."[^12] As one expositor notes, "This is the greatest self-deception and most terrifying thing, namely, to think that you are in right-standing with God, but in truth, you are not."[^13] The confession cites Jeremiah 17:9: "The heart is deceitful above all things."[^14]

**2. The Fact of True Assurance.** The confession then pivots to the positive assertion: true believers "may in this life be certainly assured that they are in the state of grace."[^15] Assurance is not reserved for a spiritual elite nor deferred until heaven. 1 John 5:13 declares, "I write these things to you who believe in the name of the Son of God, that you may know that you have eternal life."[^16] Romans 5:2,5 speaks of rejoicing "in hope of the glory of God" and declares that "hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit."[^17]

**3. The Marks of Those Who May Have Assurance.** The confession describes the subjects of true assurance as those who (a) "truly believe in the Lord Jesus," (b) "love him in sincerity," and (c) "endeavour to walk in all good conscience before him."[^18] 1 John 2:3 declares, "By this we know that we have come to know him, if we keep his commandments." 1 John 3:14 adds, "We know that we have passed out of death into life, because we love the brothers."[^19] Robert Martin emphasizes that assurance is not for those who merely claim faith but for those who possess the fruit of faith: "The confession is not saying that the mere perception or the mere feeling that one is saved is sufficient... It is a certainty rooted in the Word of God and in the observable fruit of grace."[^20]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Job 8:13-14; Matthew 7:22-23; 1 John 2:3; 1 John 3:14,18-19,21,24; 1 John 5:13; Romans 5:2,5.[^21]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Infallibility of Assurance: The Threefold Foundation

Paragraph 2 describes the nature and basis of true assurance: **"This certainty is not a bare conjectural and probable persuasion grounded upon a fallible hope, but an infallible assurance of faith, founded on the blood and righteousness of Christ revealed in the Gospel; and also upon the inward evidence of those graces of the Spirit unto which promises are made, and on the testimony of the Spirit of adoption, witnessing with our spirits that we are the children of God; and, as a fruit thereof, keeping the heart both humble and holy."**[^22]

**1. Negatively Stated: Not Mere Conjecture.** "Not a bare conjectural and probable persuasion grounded upon a fallible hope."[^23] Assurance is not wishful thinking, emotionalism, or subjective feeling. The term "conjectural" refers to an opinion based on incomplete information; "probable" refers to something that is likely but not certain. True assurance transcends mere probability.[^24]

**2. Positively Stated: Infallible Assurance.** "An infallible assurance of faith."[^25] The term "infallible" (from Latin *in-* "not" + *fallere* "to deceive") means "not liable to deceive or fail."[^26] Hebrews 6:11,19 speaks of "the full assurance of hope" and describes hope as "an anchor of the soul, both sure and steadfast."[^27]

**3. The First Root: The Blood and Righteousness of Christ.** Assurance is "founded on the blood and righteousness of Christ revealed in the Gospel."[^28] The objective ground of assurance is the finished work of Christ. Hebrews 6:17-18 speaks of "the unchangeable character of his purpose" and God's oath, "so that by two unchangeable things, in which it is impossible for God to lie, we who have fled for refuge might have strong encouragement to hold fast to the hope set before us."[^29] As Sam Waldron explains, "The blood and righteousness of Christ are the content of the promises. Such promises provide a basis for an infallible assurance for even the chief of sinners."[^30]

**4. The Second Root: The Inward Evidence of Graces.** Assurance is also founded "upon the inward evidence of those graces of the Spirit unto which promises are made."[^31] The confession cites 2 Peter 1:4-5,10-11, where Peter exhorts believers to supplement their faith with virtue, knowledge, self-control, steadfastness, godliness, brotherly affection, and love, concluding: "Therefore, brothers, be all the more diligent to make your calling and election sure."[^32] The presence of these graces provides objective evidence that one is truly in a state of grace. As Waldron notes, "The promises of the gospel, though glorious and free, are also universal and conditional... Thus no one may claim the promises of the gospel as his own without the knowledge that the condition of them has been fulfilled in him."[^33]

**5. The Third Root: The Testimony of the Spirit.** Assurance is founded "on the testimony of the Spirit of adoption, witnessing with our spirits that we are the children of God."[^34] Romans 8:15-16 declares, "For you did not receive the spirit of slavery to fall back into fear, but you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba! Father!' The Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God."[^35] This is the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit, which together with the objective promises and the inward evidence of graces, produces infallible assurance.[^36]

**6. The Fruit: Humility and Holiness.** The confession adds that assurance, as a fruit thereof, "keeps the heart both humble and holy."[^37] 1 John 3:1-3 declares, "See what kind of love the Father has given to us, that we should be called children of God... everyone who thus hopes in him purifies himself as he is pure."[^38] True assurance does not lead to pride or license but to humility and holiness. As the Ligonier devotional notes, "Assurance fosters godliness; it does not inhibit it."[^39]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Hebrews 6:11,19; Hebrews 6:17-18; 2 Peter 1:4-5,10-11; Romans 8:15-16; 1 John 3:1-3.[^40]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Attainability of Assurance: The Duty to Seek It

Paragraph 3 addresses a pastoral difficulty: why do many sincere believers lack assurance? **"This infallible assurance does not so belong to the essence of faith, but that a true believer may wait long, and struggle with many difficulties before he be partaker of it; yet being enabled by the Spirit to know the things which are freely given him of God, he may, without extraordinary revelation, in the right use of means, attain thereunto: and therefore it is the duty of every one to give all diligence to make his calling and election sure, that thereby his heart may be enlarged in peace and joy in the Holy Spirit, in love and thankfulness to God, and in strength and cheerfulness in the duties of obedience, the proper fruits of this assurance;—so far is it from inclining men to looseness."**[^41]

**1. Assurance Is Not of the Essence of Faith.** The confession makes a crucial distinction: full assurance of one's own salvation "does not so belong to the essence of faith."[^42] A true believer may lack full assurance and yet still be a true believer. As Robert Martin explains, the framers "made a very careful distinction when they spoke of the essence or nature of saving faith. They argued that although saving faith cannot coexist with doubt as to its object, that is, concerning the truthfulness of the promises of God and concerning the person and work of Jesus Christ... the full assurance of one's own salvation is not of the essence of saving faith."[^43] Thomas Brooks observed, "It is one thing for me to have grace, it is another thing for me to see my grace. It is one thing for me to believe, and another thing for me to believe that I do believe."[^44]

**2. The Struggle for Assurance.** "A true believer may wait long, and struggle with many difficulties before he be partaker of it."[^45] The confession cites Isaiah 50:10: "Who among you fears the LORD... yet walks in darkness and has no light? Let him trust in the name of the LORD and rely on his God."[^46] Psalm 88 and Psalm 77:1-12 record the struggles of the psalmist.[^47]

**3. Attainable Without Extraordinary Revelation.** "Yet being enabled by the Spirit to know the things which are freely given him of God, he may, without extraordinary revelation, in the right use of means, attain thereunto."[^48] Assurance is not dependent on visions, voices, or special revelations. It is attainable through ordinary means: the Word, prayer, the sacraments, and fellowship.[^49]

**4. The Duty to Pursue Assurance.** "Therefore it is the duty of every one to give all diligence to make his calling and election sure."[^50] 2 Peter 1:10 commands, "Therefore, brothers, be all the more diligent to make your calling and election sure."[^51] Assurance is not optional for the Christian; it is a duty.

**5. The Fruits of Assurance.** The confession lists the benefits of assurance: peace and joy in the Holy Spirit (Romans 5:1-2,5; 14:17), love and thankfulness to God (Psalm 119:32), and strength and cheerfulness in obedience.[^52] These are "the proper fruits of this assurance."[^53]

**6. Assurance Does Not Lead to Licentiousness.** The confession concludes that assurance is "so far from inclining men to looseness."[^54] Romans 6:1-2 asks, "Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means!" Titus 2:11-12,14 declares that grace "trains us to renounce ungodliness."[^55] The objection that assurance leads to carelessness is refuted by Scripture.

#### Paragraph 4 – The Variability of Assurance: Causes of Shaking and Restoration

Paragraph 4 addresses the pastoral reality that assurance can fluctuate: **"True believers may have the assurance of their salvation divers ways shaken, diminished, and intermitted; as by negligence in preserving of it, by falling into some special sin which wounds the conscience and grieves the Spirit; by some sudden or vehement temptation, by God's withdrawing the light of his countenance, and suffering even such as fear him to walk in darkness and to have no light, yet are they never destitute of the seed of God and life of faith, that love of Christ and the brethren, that sincerity of heart and conscience of duty out of which, by the operation of the Spirit, this assurance may in due time be revived, and by the which, in the meantime, they are preserved from utter despair."**[^57]

**1. The Fact of Variability.** "True believers may have the assurance of their salvation divers ways shaken, diminished, and intermitted."[^58] Assurance is not static; it can be weakened or lost temporarily. The confession cites Song of Solomon 5:2-3,6, where the bride has put off her beloved and later finds him gone.[^59]

**2. The Causes of Shaken Assurance.** The confession identifies several causes:

- **Negligence in preserving it** (Song of Solomon 5:2-3,6) – Failure to use the means of grace.[^60]
- **Falling into some special sin** (Psalm 51:8,12,14) – David's sin with Bathsheba wounded his conscience and grieved the Spirit.[^61]
- **Sudden or vehement temptation** (Psalm 116:11; 77:7-8; 31:22) – Asaph despaired, saying, "I said in my alarm, 'I am cut off from your sight.'"[^62]
- **God's withdrawing the light of his countenance** (Psalm 30:7) – God may hide His face for a season, even from those who fear Him.[^63]

**3. The Limits of Variability: Not Destitute of the Seed of God.** "Yet are they never destitute of the seed of God and life of faith."[^64] 1 John 3:9 declares, "No one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him."[^65] Luke 22:32 records Christ's words to Peter: "I have prayed for you that your faith may not fail."[^66] Even when assurance is lost, the seed of God and the life of faith remain.

**4. The Preservation from Utter Despair.** The confession concludes that believers are "preserved from utter despair"[^67] by that "sincerity of heart and conscience of duty" that remains even in the darkness. Lamentations 3:26-31 declares, "It is good that one should wait quietly for the salvation of the LORD... For the Lord will not cast off forever."[^68]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Assurance

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of assurance.[^70]

**Pillar 1: Assurance Is Possible, Not Presumptuous.** Against Rome, the confession affirms that believers may have certainty of salvation in this life—not merely hope, but assurance.[^71]

**Pillar 2: Assurance Is Infallible, Not Merely Probable.** True assurance is not wishful thinking; it is a certainty that cannot deceive. This certainty rests on three infallible grounds: the promises of the gospel, the inward evidence of graces, and the testimony of the Spirit.[^72]

**Pillar 3: Assurance Is Attainable, Not Automatic.** Full assurance does not belong to the essence of faith; a true believer may struggle for it. Yet it is attainable through ordinary means and is the duty of every believer to pursue.[^73]

**Pillar 4: Assurance Is Variable, Not Static.** Assurance may be shaken, diminished, or intermitted due to sin, negligence, temptation, or divine withdrawal. Yet the seed of God remains, and assurance can be revived.[^74]

**Pillar 5: Assurance Produces Holiness, Not License.** True assurance keeps the heart humble and holy. The objection that assurance leads to looseness is false; the fruits of assurance are peace, joy, love, thankfulness, and cheerful obedience.[^75]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted View of Assurance

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 18.[^76]

**Crisis 1: The Roman Catholic Denial of Assurance.** Rome teaches that assurance is dangerous and can only come by a special revelation. The confession's first paragraph firmly declares that true believers "may in this life be certainly assured."[^77] As the Ligonier devotional notes, "Scripture is on the side of the Reformers that every Christian can be assured of salvation through the right use of ordinary means."[^78]

**Crisis 2: The Antinomian False Assurance.** Some claim assurance without corresponding holiness. Paragraph 1 warns of "false hopes and carnal presumptions." Paragraph 2 declares that true assurance keeps the heart "humble and holy." The confession insists that assurance must be accompanied by sincerity of love and good conscience.[^79]

**Crisis 3: The Despair of Doubting Believers.** Many true believers lack full assurance and conclude they are not saved. Paragraph 3 offers comfort: assurance does not belong to the essence of faith; a true believer may "wait long, and struggle with many difficulties" before attaining it. Thomas Brooks's distinction between having grace and seeing grace is crucial pastoral wisdom.[^80]

**Crisis 4: The False Certainty of Emotionalism.** Many believers base their assurance on feelings rather than on the objective grounds of the gospel. Paragraph 2 rejects "bare conjectural and probable persuasion grounded upon a fallible hope" and insists on the infallible assurance founded on the blood and righteousness of Christ.[^81]

**Crisis 5: The Discouragement of Fallen Believers.** Paragraph 4 acknowledges that believers may fall into "some special sin which wounds the conscience and grieves the Spirit." Yet it offers hope: they are "never destitute of the seed of God." The example of David's restoration (Psalm 51) and Peter's recovery (Luke 22:32) demonstrate that assurance can be revived.[^82]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Assurance

**Step 1: Distinguish False Assurance from True.** Examine yourself: is your assurance based on the blood and righteousness of Christ, on inward evidences of grace, and on the Spirit's testimony? Or is it mere presumption? As one expositor warns, "If you are counting on a prayer you prayed or a decision you made, that is not assurance."[^83]

**Step 2: Base Your Assurance on the Promises of the Gospel.** When you doubt, look away from yourself to Christ. Hebrews 6:17-18 declares that God's purpose is unchangeable and it is impossible for God to lie. These are the infallible foundations of assurance.[^84]

**Step 3: Use the Means of Grace.** Assurance is attained "in the right use of means." Read the Word daily, pray without ceasing, partake of the Lord's Supper, gather with the saints. Through these ordinary means, the Spirit strengthens assurance.[^85]

**Step 4: Pursue Holiness as Evidence of Grace.** Peter commands, "Be all the more diligent to make your calling and election sure." The presence of the graces listed in 2 Peter 1:5-7 provides objective evidence that you are in a state of grace.[^86]

**Step 5: Seek the Spirit's Testimony.** Pray that the Spirit of adoption would bear witness with your spirit that you are a child of God. The testimony of the Spirit is not a voice or vision but a growing, settled conviction of sonship.[^87]

**Step 6: Guard Your Assurance Diligently.** The confession warns of "negligence in preserving" assurance. Guard your assurance through watchfulness, prayer, and obedience. Do not neglect the means of grace.[^88]

**Step 7: When Assurance Is Shaken, Look to the Seed of God.** If you have fallen into special sin, do not despair. The seed of God remains. Confess your sin, renew your repentance, and by the Spirit's operation, your assurance may be revived. As Peter was restored after his denial, so you may be restored.[^89]

### Conclusion: The Anchor That Cannot Slip

Chapter 18 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the anchor that cannot slip. Assurance is not a dangerous presumption but a biblical birthright. It is not reserved for a spiritual elite but attainable for every believer through ordinary means. It is not static but variable, yet even when shaken, the seed of God remains and assurance can be revived. And far from leading to license, true assurance keeps the heart both humble and holy.[^90]

The confession calls believers to distinguish false assurance from true, to base their assurance on the promises of the gospel, to use the means of grace, to pursue holiness as evidence of grace, to seek the Spirit's testimony, to guard their assurance diligently, and when assurance is shaken, to look to the seed of God and await its revival. The same gospel that saves also assures. And the same God who perseveres His people also gives them certainty of their perseverance. To Him be the glory, now and forever.[^91]

**A Prayer from Chapter 18** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father of lights, with whom there is no variation or shadow due to change: We confess that our assurance has often been weak, shaken by sin, dimmed by negligence, and clouded by temptation. We have looked to our feelings rather than to Thy promises. We have doubted Thy love when we should have trusted Thy character. Forgive us.*

*We thank Thee that assurance is possible—that we may in this life be certainly assured that we are in the state of grace. We thank Thee that assurance is infallible—founded on the blood and righteousness of Christ, on the inward evidence of Thy graces, and on the testimony of Thy Spirit. We thank Thee that assurance is attainable—without extraordinary revelation, in the right use of means. We thank Thee that though assurance may be shaken, the seed of God remains.*

*Grant us to give all diligence to make our calling and election sure. Enlarge our hearts in peace and joy in the Holy Spirit. Keep us from presumptuous confidence on the one hand and from utter despair on the other. And may the fruit of our assurance be humility and holiness, love and thankfulness, strength and cheerfulness in obedience. For Christ's sake. Amen.*[^92]
