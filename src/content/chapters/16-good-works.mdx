# The Fruit That Proves the Root: Why Good Works Are the Inevitable Harvest of Saving Grace

Chapter 16 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Good Works," occupies a strategic position in the confession's unfolding of the order of salvation.[^1] Having explained the blessings that God works *in* His people—effectual calling (Chapter 10), justification (Chapter 11), adoption (Chapter 12), sanctification (Chapter 13)—and the graces they exercise—saving faith (Chapter 14) and repentance (Chapter 15)—the confession now turns to the *fruit* that inevitably springs from true faith: good works.[^2]

As Sam Waldron notes, "Good works are the fruit and evidence that faith is genuine."[^3] The chapter comprises seven paragraphs, which can be grouped under five headings: (1) the norm of good works (paragraph 1); (2) the importance of good works (paragraph 2); (3) the source of good works (paragraph 3); (4) the limitations of good works (paragraphs 4-5); and (5) the acceptance of good works (paragraphs 6-7).[^4]

The confession walks a careful line between two errors: legalism (which makes good works the ground of salvation) and antinomianism (which denies the necessity of good works altogether).[^5] As Robert Martin observes, "On one side of the spectrum, at one extreme of error, are those who believe that a man's good works are able to save him. On the other side are those who do not believe that such things as good works are possible at all."[^6] The confession steers a biblical course between these extremes.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Good Works

Chapter 16 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, source, purpose, and limitations of good works.[^7]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Norm of Good Works: Scripture as the Only Standard

The confession opens with a clear definition: **"Good works are only such as God hath commanded in his holy word, and not such as without the warrant thereof are devised by men out of blind zeal, or upon any pretense of good intentions."**[^8]

**1. The Positive Statement.** "Good works are only such as God hath commanded in his holy word."[^9] The rule or standard for determining what constitutes a good work is not human opinion, tradition, or intention—it is the Word of God alone. Micah 6:8 declares, "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"[^10] 2 Timothy 3:16-17 affirms that Scripture equips the man of God "for every good work."[^11]

**2. The Negative Statement.** "And not such as without the warrant thereof are devised by men out of blind zeal, or upon any pretense of good intentions."[^12] The confession rejects all forms of humanly devised religion—works that lack biblical warrant, regardless of how sincere or well-intentioned they may be. Isaiah 29:13 warns, "This people draw near with their mouth and honor me with their lips, while their hearts are far from me, and their fear of me is a commandment taught by men."[^13] Matthew 15:9 records Jesus' rebuke: "In vain do they worship me, teaching as doctrines the commandments of men."[^14] As Waldron explains, "Practical or working knowledge of this will can be found only in God's Holy Word."[^15]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Micah 6:8; Hebrews 13:21; Matthew 15:9; Isaiah 29:13.[^16]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Importance of Good Works: Fruit and Evidence of Faith

The confession now lists the vital purposes good works serve: **"These good works, done in obedience to God's commandments, are the fruits and evidences of a true and lively faith; and by them believers manifest their thankfulness, strengthen their assurance, edify their brethren, adorn the profession of the gospel, stop the mouths of the adversaries, and glorify God, whose workmanship they are, created in Christ Jesus thereunto, that having their fruit unto holiness they may have the end, eternal life."**[^17]

**1. Fruits and Evidences of True Faith.** Good works are not the *root* of salvation but the *fruit*; not the cause of justification but its evidence. James 2:18 declares, "Show me your faith apart from your works, and I will show you my faith by my works."[^18] As Waldron states, "Good works are the fruit and evidence that faith is genuine."[^19]

**2. Manifestation of Thankfulness.** Good works flow from gratitude for salvation received. Psalm 116:12-13 asks, "What shall I render to the LORD for all his benefits to me? I will lift up the cup of salvation and call on the name of the LORD."[^20]

**3. Strengthening of Assurance.** Good works provide objective evidence that one's faith is genuine. 2 Peter 1:5-11 exhorts believers to supplement their faith with virtue, knowledge, self-control, steadfastness, godliness, brotherly affection, and love, concluding: "For if you practice these qualities you will never fall. For in this way there will be richly provided for you an entrance into the eternal kingdom of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ."[^21]

**4. Edification of Brethren.** Good works build up the body of Christ. As others witness godly living, they are encouraged and strengthened in their own faith.[^22]

**5. Adorning the Profession of the Gospel.** Good works make the gospel attractive. Matthew 5:16 commands, "Let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven."[^23] 1 Timothy 6:1 urges that the name of God and the teaching be "not reviled."[^24]

**6. Stopping the Mouths of Adversaries.** Good works silence critics of the Christian faith. 1 Peter 2:15 declares, "For this is the will of God, that by doing good you should put to silence the ignorance of foolish people."[^25]

**7. Glorifying God.** The ultimate purpose of all good works is the glory of God. Philippians 1:11 speaks of being "filled with the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ, to the glory and praise of God."[^26]

**8. The Outcome: Eternal Life.** "That having their fruit unto holiness they may have the end, eternal life."[^27] This does not mean good works *merit* eternal life. Rather, they mark the path that leads to eternal life—the path of sanctification. Romans 6:22 declares, "But now that you have been set free from sin and have become slaves of God, the fruit you get leads to sanctification and its end, eternal life."[^28]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Source of Good Works: Wholly from the Spirit of Christ

Paragraph 3 identifies the true source of all good works: **"Their ability to do good works is not at all of themselves, but wholly from the Spirit of Christ; and that they may be enabled thereunto, besides the graces they have already received, there is necessary an actual influence of the same Holy Spirit, to work in them to will and to do of his good pleasure; yet are they not hereupon to grow negligent, as if they were not bound to perform any duty, unless upon a special motion of the Spirit, but they ought to be diligent in stirring up the grace of God that is in them."**[^30]

**1. The Source of Ability.** "Not at all of themselves, but wholly from the Spirit of Christ."[^31] Good works do not originate in human nature or unaided will. John 15:4-5 declares, "Apart from me you can do nothing."[^32] 2 Corinthians 3:5 affirms, "Not that we are sufficient in ourselves to claim anything as coming from us, but our sufficiency is from God."[^33]

**2. The Necessity of the Spirit's Continuous Influence.** "Besides the graces they have already received, there is necessary an actual influence of the same Holy Spirit."[^34] This is the ongoing work of the Spirit, distinct from the initial gifts of regeneration. Philippians 2:13 declares, "For it is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure."[^35]

**3. The Warning Against Quietism.** "Yet are they not hereupon to grow negligent, as if they were not bound to perform any duty, unless upon a special motion of the Spirit."[^36] The confession rejects a passive "let go and let God" mentality that excuses human responsibility. Believers are "diligent in stirring up the grace of God that is in them."[^37] Philippians 2:12 commands, "Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling."[^38]

**4. The Right Order: God's Grace, Human Diligence.** The confession avoids both Pelagian self-sufficiency (we can do good works without God) and fatalistic quietism (we should wait for special motions). The Spirit enables, but believers must act.[^39]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** John 15:4-5; 2 Corinthians 3:5; Philippians 2:13; Philippians 2:12; Hebrews 6:11-12.[^40]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Limitations of Good Works: No Supererogation

Paragraph 4 addresses the error of works of supererogation—the Roman Catholic doctrine that some believers can do more than God requires: **"They who in their obedience attain to the greatest height which is possible in this life, are so far from being able to supererogate, and to do more than God requires, as that they fall short of much which in duty they are bound to do."**[^41]

**1. The Definition of Supererogation.** Supererogation refers to "works over and above what is required"—the idea that saints can perform extra good works that exceed God's commands, which can then be credited to the treasury of merit and applied to others.[^42]

**2. The Confession's Rebuttal.** The confession firmly denies that any human being can do more than God requires. The standard of obedience is absolute perfection, which no fallen human being attains. Galatians 5:17 declares, "The desires of the flesh are against the Spirit, and the desires of the Spirit are against the flesh, for these are opposed to each other, to keep you from doing the things you want to do."[^43]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Job 9:2-3; Galatians 5:17; Luke 17:10.[^44]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Limitations of Good Works: No Merit

Paragraph 5 denies that good works can merit anything from God: **"We cannot by our best works merit pardon of sin or eternal life at the hand of God, by reason of the great disproportion that is between them and the glory to come, and the infinite distance that is between us and God, whom by them we can neither profit nor satisfy for the debt of our former sins; but when we have done all we can, we have done but our duty, and are unprofitable servants; and because as they are good they proceed from his Spirit, and as they are wrought by us they are defiled and mixed with so much weakness and imperfection, that they cannot endure the severity of God's judgment."**[^45]

**1. No Merit for Pardon or Eternal Life.** Good works cannot "pardon sin" or "merit eternal life."[^46] Romans 3:20 declares, "For by works of the law no human being will be justified in his sight."[^47] Ephesians 2:8-9 affirms, "For by grace you have been saved through faith… not a result of works, so that no one may boast."[^48]

**2. The Great Disproportion.** The glory to come far exceeds any human work. Even our best works are out of all proportion to the reward of eternal life.[^49]

**3. The Infinite Distance.** God is self-sufficient and receives no profit from our works. Job 22:2-3 asks, "Can a man be profitable to God?"[^50]

**4. No Satisfaction for Former Sins.** Our good works cannot pay the debt of former sins. The only satisfaction for sin is the blood of Christ.[^51]

**5. Unprofitable Servants.** When we have done all we can, we remain "unprofitable servants" (Luke 17:10). We have only done what we were obliged to do; no surplus merit remains.[^52]

**6. The Defilement of Good Works.** Even believers' best works are "defiled and mixed with so much weakness and imperfection." Isaiah 64:6 laments, "All our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment."[^53]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Romans 3:20; Ephesians 2:8-9; Romans 4:6; Galatians 5:22-23; Isaiah 64:6; Psalm 43:2.[^54]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Acceptance of Believers' Good Works: In Christ

After stating the limitations of good works, the confession now declares how they are nevertheless accepted: **"Yet notwithstanding the persons of believers being accepted through Christ, their good works also are accepted in him; not as though they were in this life wholly unblameable and unreprovable in God's sight, but that he, looking upon them in his Son, is pleased to accept and reward that which is sincere, although accompanied with many weaknesses and imperfections."**[^55]

**1. The Basis of Acceptance: Union with Christ.** Believers themselves are accepted "through Christ." Because the person is accepted in the Beloved (Ephesians 1:6), their works are also accepted *in Him*. As Waldron summarizes, a good work must have "the right matter (God's command), the right root (heart purified by faith), the right manner (done according to God's Word), and the right end (God's glory)."[^56]

**2. Not Because of Inherent Perfection.** Believers' works are not accepted because they are "wholly unblameable and unreprovable." Even the best works are imperfect.[^57]

**3. Accepted Because of Christ.** God accepts sincere works—though imperfect—because He looks upon them "in his Son." This is the doctrine of the *acceptance of good works through Christ*. The confession cites 1 Peter 2:5: "Spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ."[^58]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** Ephesians 1:6; 1 Peter 2:5; Matthew 25:21, 23; Hebrews 6:10.[^59]

#### Paragraph 7 – The Rejection of Unbelievers' Works

The confession concludes by addressing the works of the unregenerate: **"Works done by unregenerate men, although for the matter of them they may be things which God commands, and of good use both to themselves and others; yet because they proceed not from a heart purified by faith, nor are done in a right manner according to the Word, nor to a right end, the glory of God, they are therefore sinful, and cannot please God, nor make a man meet to receive grace from God, and yet their neglect of them is more sinful and displeasing to God."**[^60]

**1. Unregenerate Men Can Do Externally Good Things.** Even unbelievers may do what is commanded in Scripture. Romans 2:14 speaks of Gentiles who "do by nature what the law requires."[^61]

**2. The Four Criteria for a Good Work.** Sam Waldron outlines four criteria for a truly good work: (a) the right matter (commanded by God); (b) the right root (proceeds from a heart purified by faith); (c) the right manner (done according to God's Word); (d) the right end (the glory of God).[^62]

**3. Why Unbelievers' Works Are Sinful.** Works done by the unregenerate "proceed not from a heart purified by faith." Romans 14:23 declares, "Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin."[^63] They are not done "to a right end, the glory of God" but typically for self-glorification.[^64]

**4. They Cannot Please God or Prepare for Grace.** Such works "cannot please God, nor make a man meet to receive grace from God."[^65]

**5. Neglect of Such Works Is Worse.** The confession adds a crucial pastoral note: the neglect of even these imperfect works is "more sinful and displeasing to God."[^66]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Good Works

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of good works.

**Pillar 1: The Norm of Good Works Is Scripture Alone.** Good works are defined by God's Word, not human tradition, zeal, or good intentions.[^68]

**Pillar 2: Good Works Are the Fruit of Faith, Not the Root.** They do not cause justification but evidence it.[^69]

**Pillar 3: The Ability to Do Good Works Is Wholly from the Spirit.** Human nature cannot produce spiritual good. The Spirit must enable both will and deed.[^70]

**Pillar 4: Our Best Works Cannot Merit Anything from God.** They cannot purchase pardon or eternal life, nor are they works of supererogation.[^71]

**Pillar 5: Believers' Works Are Accepted in Christ.** Despite imperfections, sincere works are accepted because of the believer's union with Christ.[^72]

**Pillar 6: Unbelievers' Works Are Sinful.** Though externally good, they proceed from an unsanctified heart and cannot please God.[^73]

**Pillar 7: Good Works Are Necessary in the Christian Life.** Not optional, not meritorious, but the inevitable fruit of genuine faith.[^74]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted View of Good Works

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 16.

**Crisis 1: The Legalistic Error.** Many believe good works earn or contribute to salvation. The confession denies that works can merit pardon or eternal life.[^75]

**Crisis 2: The Antinomian Error.** Others deny that good works are necessary at all. The confession declares that good works are "fruits and evidences of a true and lively faith."[^76]

**Crisis 3: The Quietistic Error.** Some believers become passive, waiting for "special motions" before acting. The confession commands diligence: "stir up the grace of God that is in them."[^77]

**Crisis 4: The Catholic Error of Supererogation.** The Roman Catholic doctrine of works of supererogation falsely teaches that believers can do more than God requires. The confession denies this absolutely.[^78]

**Crisis 5: The Despair of Imperfect Works.** Many believers despair because their works are imperfect. The confession comforts: God accepts sincere works through Christ.[^79]

**Crisis 6: The False Assurance of the Unregenerate.** Many unconverted people mistake external morality for saving faith. The confession warns that works done without faith are sinful.[^80]

**Crisis 7: The False Dichotomy Between Faith and Works.** The confession insists that good works are the inevitable fruit of saving faith—not optional, not meritorious, but necessary.[^81]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Good Works

**Step 1: Define Good Works by Scripture.** When in doubt about whether an action is good, ask: "Is this commanded in God's Word?" Do not rely on tradition, good intentions, or zeal alone.[^82]

**Step 2: Do Good Works as Fruit, Not Root.** Perform good works not to earn salvation but because you are saved. Let gratitude, not merit-seeking, be your motive.[^83]

**Step 3: Rely on the Spirit Daily.** You cannot produce good works in your own strength. Pray for the Spirit's enabling. Do good works in dependence on Him.[^84]

**Step 4: Reject All Works-Righteousness.** Do not imagine that your good works contribute anything to your justification. Trust in Christ alone for acceptance before God.[^85]

**Step 5: Take Comfort in God's Acceptance of Imperfect Works.** When your motives are mixed and your performance imperfect, remember that God accepts your sincere works through Christ. Confess your imperfections and press on.[^86]

**Step 6: Test Your Faith by Your Works.** Examine your life: do you bear the fruit of genuine faith? If your life is devoid of good works, question whether your faith is alive.[^87]

**Step 7: Be Diligent, Not Passive.** Do not wait for "special motions" of the Spirit. Use the means of grace. Stir up the gift of God that is within you. Be active in pursuing holiness.[^88]

### Conclusion: The Fruit That Proves the Root

Chapter 16 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the fruit that proves the root. Good works are not optional for the Christian; they are the inevitable fruit of saving faith. Yet they do not contribute to justification; they are its evidence. They do not merit salvation; they flow from it. They are not perfect; yet they are accepted through Christ.[^89]

The confession calls believers to define good works by Scripture, to perform them as fruit not root, to rely on the Spirit daily, to reject all works-righteousness, to take comfort in God's acceptance of imperfect works, to test their faith by their works, and to be diligent not passive. The same chapter that guards against legalism also guards against antinomianism. The balanced teaching of Scripture is this: we are saved *by grace alone through faith alone*, but the faith that saves is *never alone*—it is always accompanied by good works.[^90]

**A Prayer from Chapter 16** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father, who hast created us in Christ Jesus for good works: We confess that we have often neglected the works Thou hast prepared for us. We have trusted in our works as if they could merit salvation, or we have neglected them as if they were unnecessary. Forgive us. Grant us to do good works as the fruit of true faith—manifesting thankfulness, strengthening assurance, edifying brethren, adorning the gospel, stopping the mouths of adversaries, and glorifying Thy name. Teach us to rely wholly on Thy Spirit for ability, and yet to be diligent in stirring up the grace that is in us. We rest not in our works but in Christ. Accept our imperfect works through His perfect righteousness. For Jesus' sake. Amen.*[^91]
