# The Only Security That Cannot Fail: Why the Perseverance of the Saints Does Not Depend on the Human Will

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 17 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Anchor That Holds

Chapter 17 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Perseverance of the Saints," stands as one of the most comforting chapters in the entire confession.[^1] Having explained the blessings that God works in His people—effectual calling (Chapter 10), justification (Chapter 11), adoption (Chapter 12), sanctification (Chapter 13)—and the graces they exercise—saving faith (Chapter 14), repentance (Chapter 15), and good works (Chapter 16)—the confession now turns to the *preservation* of the saints.[^2] As Sam Waldron notes, this chapter is "the highest comfort seasoned with sober realism."[^3]

The chapter comprises three paragraphs. Paragraph 1 states the *fact* of perseverance—that those truly united to Christ will never totally or finally fall away. Paragraph 2 explains the *grounds* of perseverance—not the believer’s free will, but the immutability of election, the efficacy of Christ’s intercession, union with Christ, the oath of God, the indwelling of the Spirit, and the nature of the covenant of grace. Paragraph 3 addresses the *difficulties* of perseverance—the reality that believers may fall into great sins and remain there for a time, yet they will eventually be renewed to repentance and persevere to the end.[^4]

The confession walks a careful line between two errors: Arminianism (which makes perseverance dependent on the believer’s free will and thus uncertain) and antinomianism (which minimizes the seriousness of sin in the believer’s life).[^5] As the Baptist Catechism states, "They whom God hath accepted in his Beloved, effectually called and sanctified by his Spirit, can neither totally nor finally fall away from the state of grace; but shall certainly persevere therein to the end, and be eternally saved."[^6]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Perseverance

Chapter 17 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on the preservation and perseverance of the saints.[^7]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Fact of Perseverance

The confession opens with a declaration of the certainty of perseverance: **"They whom God hath accepted in his Beloved, effectually called and sanctified by his Spirit, and given the precious faith of his elect unto, can neither totally nor finally fall away from the state of grace; but shall certainly persevere therein to the end, and be eternally saved, seeing the gifts and calling of God are without repentance, whence he still begets and nourisheth in them faith, repentance, love, joy, hope, and all the graces of the Spirit unto immortality; and though many storms and floods arise and beat against them, yet they shall never be able to take them off that foundation and rock which by faith they are fastened upon; notwithstanding, through unbelief and the temptations of Satan, the sensible sight of the light and love of God may for a time be clouded and obscured from them, yet he is still the same, and they shall be kept by the power of God unto salvation, wherefore they are engraven upon the palm of his hands, and their names are written in the book of life from all eternity."**[^8]

**1. The Subjects of Perseverance.** The confession identifies the recipients of this promise: those "whom God hath accepted in his Beloved" (justified and adopted), "effectually called and sanctified by his Spirit" (Chapters 10 and 13), and "given the precious faith of his elect unto" (Chapter 14).[^9] Not everyone who professes faith is the subject of perseverance; only those who possess the *precious faith of the elect*.[^10]

**2. The Nature of Perseverance.** "Can neither totally nor finally fall away from the state of grace."[^11] The two negatives are significant. Believers cannot fall **totally** (they retain a seed of faith even in their worst falls) nor **finally** (they will eventually be restored).[^12] The confession cites John 10:28-29: "I give them eternal life, and they will never perish—into no one will snatch them out of my hand."[^13]

**3. The Ground: The Irrevocable Gifts and Calling.** "Seeing the gifts and calling of God are without repentance" (Romans 11:29).[^14] God does not revoke His gifts like a fickle human being. Philippians 1:6 declares, "He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ."[^15]

**4. The Means: God’s Continuous Nourishment.** "Whence he still begets and nourisheth in them faith, repentance, love, joy, hope, and all the graces of the Spirit unto immortality."[^16] Perseverance is not a passive "let go and let God" but an active work of God through means. He nourishes the graces He has implanted.[^23]

**5. The Security: The Unshakeable Foundation.** "Though many storms and floods arise and beat against them, yet they shall never be able to take them off that foundation and rock which by faith they are fastened upon."[^17] This echoes Matthew 7:24-27, the wise man who built his house on the rock. The storms of temptation, persecution, and trial will come, but the foundation holds.[^18]

**6. The Possible Darkening of Assurance.** "Notwithstanding, through unbelief and the temptations of Satan, the sensible sight of the light and love of God may for a time be clouded and obscured from them."[^19] Believers may lose the *sense* of God’s favor without losing the *state* of grace. David’s cry in Psalm 51: "Restore to me the joy of your salvation" (v. 12) indicates that he had lost the joy, not the salvation itself.[^20]

**7. The Unchanging God and the Final Preservation.** "Yet he is still the same, and they shall be kept by the power of God unto salvation, wherefore they are engraven upon the palm of his hands, and their names are written in the book of life from all eternity."[^21] The ultimate ground of perseverance is not the believer’s grip on God but God’s grip on the believer. Isaiah 49:16 declares, "Behold, I have engraved you on the palms of my hands." Revelation 13:8 speaks of the "book of life of the Lamb who was slain."[^22]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** John 10:28-29; Philippians 1:6; 2 Timothy 2:19; 1 John 2:19; Psalm 89:31-32; 1 Corinthians 11:32; Malachi 3:6.[^23]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Grounds of Perseverance

Paragraph 2 explains why perseverance is certain: **"This perseverance of the saints depends not upon their own free will, but upon the immutability of the decree of election, flowing from the free and unchangeable love of God the Father; upon the efficacy of the merit and intercession of Jesus Christ; the abiding of the Spirit, and of the seed of God within them; and the nature of the covenant of grace: from all which ariseth also the certainty and infallibility thereof."**[^24]

**1. The Negative Ground: Not Free Will.** "Depends not upon their own free will."[^25] This is a direct rejection of the Arminian position that perseverance is uncertain because it depends on the mutable human will. As one expositor notes, "If the final salvation of the saints depended upon something in them, not one of them would finally be saved."[^26]

**2. The Immutability of Election.** "The immutability of the decree of election, flowing from the free and unchangeable love of God the Father."[^27] Romans 8:30 traces the golden chain: "Whom he predestined, them he also called… and whom he called, them he also justified… and whom he justified, them he also glorified."[^28] God’s eternal purpose cannot be thwarted.

**3. The Efficacy of Christ’s Merit and Intercession.** "The efficacy of the merit and intercession of Jesus Christ."[^29] Christ’s death secured not only the believer’s justification but also their preservation. Romans 5:9-10 declares, "Since therefore we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God."[^30] Hebrews 7:25 affirms, "He is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him, since he always lives to make intercession for them."[^31]

**4. The Abiding of the Spirit and the Seed of God.** "The abiding of the Spirit, and of the seed of God within them."[^32] The Holy Spirit is not a temporary guest but a permanent resident (John 14:16). 1 John 3:9 declares, "No one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God’s seed abides in him; and he cannot keep on sinning, because he has been born of God."[^33]

**5. The Nature of the Covenant of Grace.** "The nature of the covenant of grace."[^34] Jeremiah 32:40 describes the covenant: "I will make with them an everlasting covenant, that I will not turn away from doing good to them. And I will put the fear of me in their hearts, that they may not turn from me."[^35] The covenant is unilateral, unconditional, and everlasting.

#### Paragraph 3 – The Difficulties of Perseverance

Paragraph 3 addresses the sobering reality that believers may fall into great sins: **"Yet they may, through the temptations of Satan and of the world, the prevalency of corruption remaining in them, and the neglect of means of their preservation, fall into grievous sins; and for a time continue therein, whereby they incur God’s displeasure, and grieve his Holy Spirit, come to be deprived of some measure of their graces and comforts, have their hearts hardened, and their consciences wounded, hurt and scandalize others, and bring temporal judgments upon themselves; yet they shall renew their repentance, and be preserved through faith in Christ Jesus to the end."**[^37]

**1. The Causes of Falls.** Three causes are identified: (a) "the temptations of Satan and of the world"; (b) "the prevalency of corruption remaining in them"; (c) "the neglect of means of their preservation."[^38] David’s fall with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 11) illustrates all three: Satan’s temptation, the power of remaining corruption, and the neglect of duty (kings were supposed to go to war).[^39]

**2. The Consequences of Falls.** The confession lists seven consequences:

- **God’s displeasure** (Psalm 89:31-33) – not judicial wrath, but fatherly displeasure.
- **Grieving the Holy Spirit** (Ephesians 4:30).
- **Deprived of graces and comforts** (Psalm 51:12 – "Restore to me the joy of your salvation").
- **Hardened hearts** (Hebrews 3:13).
- **Wounded consciences** (Psalm 32:3-4 – "my bones wasted away").
- **Hurt and scandalize others** (2 Samuel 12:14 – "by this deed you have utterly scorned the LORD").
- **Temporal judgments** (1 Corinthians 11:32 – "we are judged by the Lord").[^40]

**3. The Final Outcome: Renewal and Preservation.** "Yet they shall renew their repentance, and be preserved through faith in Christ Jesus to the end."[^41] The confession does not promise immediate restoration, but it promises certain restoration. Peter’s denial and restoration (Luke 22:31-32, 61-62) is the classic example: Christ prayed for him, and after his fall, he "turned again" and strengthened his brothers.[^42]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Perseverance

Several theological pillars support the confession’s doctrine of perseverance.[^44]

**Pillar 1: Perseverance Is a Fact, Not a Possibility.** The confession declares that true believers "cannot totally nor finally fall away." This is not a statement about what *might* happen but about what *cannot* happen.[^45]

**Pillar 2: Perseverance Depends on God, Not Man.** The negative ground ("depends not upon their own free will") is as important as the positive grounds. Arminianism makes perseverance uncertain by placing it in the hands of mutable humans. The confession places it in the hands of an immutable God.[^46]

**Pillar 3: The Decree of Election Is Immutable.** God’s eternal purpose to save His people cannot be thwarted. The golden chain of Romans 8:30 links predestination, calling, justification, and glorification into an unbreakable sequence.[^47]

**Pillar 4: Christ’s Intercession Is Efficacious.** Christ’s priestly work did not end at the cross; He "always lives to make intercession" (Hebrews 7:25). His prayers for Peter (Luke 22:31-32) secured Peter’s restoration.[^48]

**Pillar 5: The Spirit’s Indwelling Is Permanent.** The Spirit is not a temporary guest but a permanent resident. The "seed of God" remains in believers (1 John 3:9).[^49]

**Pillar 6: The Covenant of Grace Is Everlasting.** Jeremiah 32:40 describes a covenant in which God promises, "I will not turn away from doing good to them" and "I will put the fear of me in their hearts, that they may not turn from me."[^50]

**Pillar 7: True Believers May Fall Grievously but Not Finally.** The confession acknowledges the reality of backsliding without denying the certainty of final preservation. This sober realism keeps the doctrine from becoming a license to sin.[^51]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Perseverance

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 17.[^52]

**Crisis 1: The Arminian Uncertainty.** If final salvation depends on the believer’s free will, no one can have true assurance. The confession’s grounding of perseverance in God’s immutable decree and Christ’s efficacious intercession provides the only foundation for genuine assurance.[^53]

**Crisis 2: The Antinomian License.** Some who profess perseverance live in sin, assuming that their eternal security permits careless living. The confession’s paragraph 3 refutes this error by detailing the serious consequences of sin in the believer’s life: God’s displeasure, grieving the Spirit, loss of comfort, wounded conscience, temporal judgments.[^54]

**Crisis 3: The Despair of Fallen Believers.** Many who fall into grievous sin conclude that they were never truly saved or that they have lost their salvation. The confession offers hope: "Yet they shall renew their repentance, and be preserved through faith in Christ Jesus to the end." Peter’s restoration after his denial is the model.[^55]

**Crisis 4: The Confusion Between State and Assurance.** Many believers equate the loss of assurance with the loss of salvation. The confession teaches that the "sensible sight of the light and love of God may for a time be clouded" without the loss of the state of grace. David lost the joy of salvation (Psalm 51:12) but not salvation itself.[^56]

**Crisis 5: The Neglect of the Means of Preservation.** The confession identifies "neglect of means of their preservation" as a cause of falling. This exhorts believers to use the means of grace: Word, prayer, sacraments, and fellowship.[^57]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Perseverance

**Step 1: Rest in the Immutable Grounds of Perseverance.** Your final salvation does not depend on the strength of your grip on God but on the strength of His grip on you. Rest in the immutability of election, the efficacy of Christ’s intercession, and the permanence of the Spirit’s indwelling.[^58]

**Step 2: Use the Means of Preservation.** The confession warns against "neglect of means." Therefore, diligently use the Word, prayer, sacraments, and fellowship. These are the channels through which God nourishes the graces of the Spirit.[^59]

**Step 3: When You Fall, Do Not Despair.** If you have fallen into grievous sin, the confession offers hope. You are not beyond restoration. Renew your repentance. Return to Christ. He who prayed for Peter prays for you.[^60]

**Step 4: Do Not Presume.** The same confession that assures of final preservation warns of temporal judgments, wounded consciences, and grieving the Spirit. Do not use the doctrine of perseverance as a license to sin.[^61]

**Step 5: Distinguish Between State and Assurance.** When you lose the sense of God’s favor, do not conclude that you have lost your salvation. The sun is still there even when clouds obscure it. Seek the restoration of joy through confession and repentance.[^62]

**Step 6: Comfort Struggling Believers.** When others fall, do not cast them away. The covenant of grace provides for restoration. Point them to Christ’s intercession and the Spirit’s preserving power.[^63]

**Step 7: Rejoice in the Security of the Covenant.** Jeremiah 32:40 promises that God will put His fear in your hearts "that you may not turn from me." The same grace that saved you preserves you. Rejoice in the security of the everlasting covenant.[^64]

### Conclusion: The Only Security That Cannot Fail

Chapter 17 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer’s anchor in the storms of life. The doctrine of the perseverance of the saints is not a license to sin but a guarantee that God will complete what He began. It does not rest on the shifting sands of the human will but on the immutable decree of election, the efficacious intercession of Christ, the abiding presence of the Spirit, and the everlasting nature of the covenant of grace.[^65]

The same chapter that offers the highest comfort—that those who are truly in Christ can never finally fall away—also offers a sober realism: believers may fall grievously, grieve the Spirit, wound their consciences, and incur temporal judgments. But the final word is not judgment; it is restoration: "Yet they shall renew their repentance, and be preserved through faith in Christ Jesus to the end."[^66]

This is the only security that cannot fail. Not because we are strong, but because He is faithful. To Him be the glory, now and forever.[^67]

**A Prayer from Chapter 17** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father, whose gifts and calling are without repentance: We thank Thee that our final salvation does not depend on our fickle wills but on Thine immutable decree, on the intercession of Thy Son, on the abiding presence of Thy Spirit, and on the everlasting nature of Thy covenant. We confess that we have often neglected the means of preservation, fallen into sin, grieved Thy Spirit, and wounded our consciences. Restore unto us the joy of Thy salvation. When we fall, renew our repentance. When we are tempted, keep us by Thy power. And bring us at last to the glory promised in the covenant of grace. For Christ’s sake. Amen.*[^68]
