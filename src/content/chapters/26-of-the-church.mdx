# The Household of God: Why the Doctrine of the Church Is the Believer's Spiritual Home

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 26 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Longest and Most Distinctive Chapter

Chapter 26 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Church," is the longest and most distinctive chapter in the entire confession.[^1] If earlier chapters have established the work of redemption—from eternal election to effectual calling, justification, adoption, sanctification, and the believer's liberty—then Chapter 26 answers the question: **What is the community into which the redeemed are gathered?**[^2]

The chapter comprises **fifteen paragraphs**, making it substantially longer than the parallel chapters in the Presbyterian Westminster Confession (WCF 25) and the Congregationalist Savoy Declaration (Chapter 26).[^3] As one expositor notes, "Our Baptist forefathers said, no, this ecclesiology and even this practical ecclesiology, that's clear enough in scripture. We believe that we bringing this back up and putting it into our confession."[^4] The chapter can be outlined in five major sections:[^5]

- **Paragraphs 1–3**: The identity of the church (universal/invisible, local/visible, and the perpetuity of the church)
- **Paragraph 4**: The head of the church (Christ alone, rejecting the papacy)
- **Paragraphs 5–7**: The gathering of the church (calling, visible saints, and the authority given to each congregation)
- **Paragraphs 8–13**: The government and officers of the church (elders, deacons, and congregational authority)
- **Paragraphs 14–15**: The cooperation between churches (counsel and fellowship without control)

The chapter is a direct expression of **Baptist ecclesiology**—emphasizing regenerate church membership, the independence and spiritual-giftedness of each local congregation, a plurality of elders, and the sufficiency of Scripture for ordering the church's life.[^6]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Church

Chapter 26 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, government, and officers of the church.[^7]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Universal and Invisible Church

The confession opens: <ConfessionBox>
"The catholic or universal church, which (with respect to the internal work of the Spirit and truth of grace) may be called invisible, consists of the whole number of the elect, that have been, are, or shall be gathered into one, under Christ, the head thereof; and is the spouse, the body, the fulness of him that filleth all in all."
</ConfessionBox>[^8]

**1. "The catholic or universal church."** The term "catholic" (lowercase) means "universal," not "Roman Catholic."[^9] The confession is confessing the **one universal church** of all true believers in all ages and places.

**2. "May be called invisible."** The universal church is invisible "with respect to the internal work of the Spirit and truth of grace."[^10] We cannot see the work of the Spirit in a person's heart. We cannot see the "truth of grace" — the genuine saving faith that uniting a soul to Christ. The invisible church consists of the elect. As one commentary states, "The catholic (meaning universal) church, which is called invisible, consists of the whole number of the elect (e.g., 1 Cor. 1:2; Heb. 12:23)."[^11]

**3. "The whole number of the elect, that have been, are, or shall be gathered into one."** This is the church triumphant and militant together. It includes all believers from Adam to the last person who will be saved.[^12]

**4. "Under Christ, the head thereof."** Christ is the exclusive head of the universal church. The confession cites Colossians 1:18: "He is the head of the body, the church."[^13]

**5. "The spouse, the body, the fulness of him that filleth all in all."** The confession draws on the rich biblical metaphors for the church: the bride of Christ (Ephesians 5:23-27), the body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12:27), and the fulness (or completion) of Christ (Ephesians 1:23).[^14]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Hebrews 12:23; Colossians 1:18; Ephesians 1:10,22-23, 5:23,27,32.[^15]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Local and Visible Church

Paragraph 2 states: <ConfessionBox>
"All persons throughout the world, professing the faith of the gospel, and obedience unto God by Christ according unto it, not destroying their own profession by any errors everting the foundation, or unholiness of conversation, are and may be called visible saints; and of such ought all particular congregations to be constituted."
</ConfessionBox>[^16]

**1. "All persons... professing the faith of the gospel."** The visible church consists of those who make a credible profession of faith. The confession cites 1 Corinthians 1:2: "To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus, called to be saints."[^17]

**2. "Not destroying their own profession by any errors everting the foundation."** This is the condition for being considered a visible saint: the profession must be credible. The confession adds that those who destroy their profession by foundational errors or unholy living are not to be regarded as visible saints.[^18]

**3. "Of such ought all particular congregations to be constituted."** Every local church should be composed of those who profess faith and live accordingly. This is the Baptist doctrine of **regenerate church membership**.[^19]

**4. Visible saints, not invisible ones.** The confession carefully distinguishes between the visible church (those who outwardly profess) and the invisible church (those who are truly elect). The visible church is a "mixed body."[^20]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** 1 Corinthians 1:2; Acts 11:26; Romans 1:7; Ephesians 1:20-22.[^21]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Mixture and Perpetuity of the Church

Paragraph 3 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The purest churches under heaven are subject to mixture and error; and some have so degenerated as to become no churches of Christ, but synagogues of Satan; nevertheless Christ always has had, and ever shall have a kingdom in this world, to the end thereof, of such as believe in him, and make profession of his name."
</ConfessionBox>[^22]

**1. "The purest churches under heaven are subject to mixture and error."** Even the best churches are not perfect. The confession cites the letters to the seven churches in Revelation 2-3, where even the church at Ephesus — commended for her works — is warned that she has left her first love.[^23] The church on earth is **militant**, not yet triumphant.

**2. "Some have so degenerated as to become no churches of Christ, but synagogues of Satan."** The confession acknowledges that some bodies that call themselves churches may have so corrupted the gospel that they cease to be true churches.[^24]

**3. "Christ always has had, and ever shall have a kingdom in this world."** The confession affirms the **perpetuity of the church**. Despite mixture and error, Christ will always have a true church until the end of the world. Matthew 16:18 declares, "On this rock I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it."[^25]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** 1 Corinthians 5; Revelation 2-3; Revelation 18:2; 2 Thessalonians 2:11-12; Matthew 16:18; Psalm 72:17, 102:28; Revelation 12:17.[^26]

#### Paragraph 4 – Christ as the Exclusive Head of the Church

Paragraph 4 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The Lord Jesus Christ is the Head of the church, in whom, by the appointment of the Father, all power for the calling, institution, order or government of the church, is invested in a supreme and sovereign manner; neither can the Pope of Rome in any sense be head thereof, but is that antichrist, that man of sin, and son of perdition, that exalts himself in the church against Christ, and all that is called God; whom the Lord shall destroy with the brightness of his coming."
</ConfessionBox>[^27]

**1. "The Lord Jesus Christ is the Head of the church."** The confession declares the exclusive headship of Christ over the church. Colossians 1:18 affirms, "He is the head of the body, the church."[^28]

**2. "All power... is invested in a supreme and sovereign manner."** Christ possesses all authority for the church's calling, institution, order, and government. Matthew 28:18-20 records Christ's declaration: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me."[^29]

**3. "Neither can the Pope of Rome in any sense be head thereof."** The confession explicitly rejects the papacy as a rival to Christ's headship.

**4. "Is that antichrist, that man of sin, and son of perdition."** This is the confession's strongest statement. It identifies the papacy with the Antichrist described in 2 Thessalonians 2:2-9.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Colossians 1:18; Matthew 28:18-20; Ephesians 4:11-12; 2 Thessalonians 2:2-9.[^30]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Calling and Gathering of the Church

Paragraph 5 states: <ConfessionBox>
"In the execution of this power wherewith he is so intrusted, the Lord Jesus calls out of the world unto himself, through the ministry of his word, by his Spirit, those that are given unto him by his Father, that they may walk before him in all the ways of obedience, which he prescribes to them in his word. Those thus called, he commands to walk together in particular societies, or churches, for their mutual edification, and the due performance of that public worship, which he requires of them in the world."
</ConfessionBox>[^31]

**1. "The Lord Jesus calls out of the world."** Effectual calling (Chapter 10) is the foundation of the church. The confession cites John 10:16: "I have other sheep that are not of this fold; I must bring them also."[^32]

**2. "Through the ministry of his word, by his Spirit."** The ordinary means of effectual calling is the Word preached. Romans 10:17 declares, "Faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ."[^33]

**3. "Those that are given unto him by his Father."** The confession reiterates the doctrine of election (Chapter 3). John 12:32 records Christ's promise: "I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself."[^34]

**4. "He commands to walk together in particular societies, or churches."** The gathered life of the church is not optional. Matthew 18:15-20 gives instructions for church discipline and the gathered church.[^35]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** John 10:16; John 12:32; Matthew 28:20; Matthew 18:15-20.[^36]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Members of the Church

Paragraph 6 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The members of these churches are saints by calling, visibly manifesting and evidencing (in and by their profession and walking) their obedience unto that call of Christ; and do willingly consent to walk together, according to the appointment of Christ; giving up themselves to the Lord, and one to another, by the will of God, in professed subjection to the ordinances of the Gospel."
</ConfessionBox>[^37]

**1. "Saints by calling."** Members are not saints by nature but by God's calling. Romans 1:7 addresses believers as "called to be saints."[^38]

**2. "Visibly manifesting and evidencing their obedience."** The confession emphasizes the importance of a credible profession. Members must be those who demonstrate their faith in their walk.[^39]

**3. "Willingly consent to walk together."** Church membership is not coerced; it is voluntary. The confession cites Acts 2:41-42, where those who received the word were baptized and devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and fellowship.[^40]

**4. "Giving up themselves to the Lord, and one to another."** This is the language of covenant membership. Believers commit themselves to the Lord and to one another.[^28]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** Romans 1:7; 1 Corinthians 1:2; Acts 2:41-42, 5:13-14; 2 Corinthians 9:13.[^41]

#### Paragraph 7 – The Authority of Each Local Church

Paragraph 7 states: <ConfessionBox>
"To each of these churches therefore gathered, according to his mind declared in his word, he has given all that power and authority, which is in any way needful for their carrying on that order in worship and discipline, which he has instituted for them to observe; and that there ought to be no dependency of one particular church upon another; but each church is to enjoy all the liberties and privileges which Christ has granted to it, independent of any other church or ecclesiastical jurisdiction, yet so as to take care not to be a stumbling block to others, nor to disturb the peace of the churches of Christ."
</ConfessionBox>[^42]

**1. "He has given all that power and authority."** Christ has endowed each local church with full authority for worship and discipline. Paragraph seven "highlights the independence and spiritual-giftedness of every local congregation — 'To each of these churches.'"[^43]

**2. "No dependency of one particular church upon another."** The confession affirms **congregational autonomy**. No church is subject to another church or to any external ecclesiastical jurisdiction.[^44]

**3. "Each church is to enjoy all the liberties and privileges."** The church is not under the authority of bishops, presbyteries, or synods. Its only head is Christ.

**4. "Yet so as to take care not to be a stumbling block to others."** The confession adds a crucial qualifier: autonomy does not mean isolation. Churches should exercise their liberties in a way that does not cause others to stumble.[^45]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 7:** Ephesians 1:22-23; Colossians 2:19; 1 Corinthians 4:17; Acts 14:23; 1 Corinthians 14:33,40; 1 Corinthians 16:1; Matthew 18:15-20; 1 Corinthians 5:4-5.[^46]

#### Paragraph 8 – The Officers of the Church: Elders and Deacons

Paragraph 8 states: <ConfessionBox>
"A particular church gathered and completely organized according to the mind of Christ, consists of officers and members; and the officers appointed by Christ to be chosen and set apart by the church (so called and gathered) for the peculiar administration of ordinances, and execution of power or duty, which he entrusts them with, or calls them to, to be continued to the end of the world, are bishops or elders, and deacons."
</ConfessionBox>[^47]

**1. "Consists of officers and members."** A fully organized church has both members and officers. As one expositor notes, "Do we affirm a matter in a form with regards to the universal church? Yes, we do. That's paragraph 1. Do we affirm a matter in a form with regards to the particular church? Yes, we do. ... In paragraphs 2 and 5, we confess the matter or the materials of a local congregation, and that matter or the material is visible saints. In paragraph 8... we affirm some things about the form of a particular church or the local congregation."[^48]

**2. "The officers appointed by Christ to be chosen and set apart by the church."** The officers are appointed by Christ, but chosen by the church. This is the congregational principle: the church elects its officers. Acts 6:3-5 describes the choosing of the first deacons.[^49]

**3. "Bishops or elders, and deacons."** The confession recognizes two offices in the New Testament church: elder (also called bishop/overseer) and deacon.[^50]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 8:** Acts 1:21-26; Acts 14:23; 1 Timothy 3:1-13; 4:14; 5:17; Acts 6:3-6; Philippians 1:1.[^51]

#### Paragraph 9 – The Election of Officers

Paragraph 9 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The way appointed by Christ for the calling of any person, fitted and gifted by the Holy Spirit, unto the office of bishop or elder in a church, is, that he be chosen thereunto by the common suffrage of the church itself; and solemnly set apart by fasting and prayer, with imposition of hands of the eldership of the same church, if there be any before constituted therein; and of a deacon, that he be chosen by the like suffrage, and set apart by prayer, and the like laying on of hands."
</ConfessionBox>[^52]

**1. "Chosen thereunto by the common suffrage of the church itself."** Elders are not appointed by a bishop or imposed by a presbytery; they are chosen by the congregation.

**2. "Solemnly set apart by fasting and prayer, with imposition of hands."** The confession describes an ordination service. Acts 13:3 records the laying on of hands for Paul and Barnabas.[^53]

**3. "Of the eldership of the same church, if there be any before constituted therein."** The laying on of hands is to be performed by the existing eldership — not by an external bishop.

**4. "Of a deacon, that he be chosen by the like suffrage."** Deacons are elected by the same congregational vote.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 9:** Acts 6:3,5-6; Acts 1:21-26; 1 Timothy 4:14; Acts 13:3; 1 Timothy 3:10; Acts 14:23.[^54]

#### Paragraph 10 – The Work of Pastors

Paragraph 10 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The work of pastors being constantly to attend the service of Christ, in his churches, in the ministry of the word and prayer, with watching for their souls, as they that must give an account to God; is to be performed by them, with whom the rest of the members are also to communicate, and to submit themselves in the Lord; but, as the power and authority which Christ has committed to them, consists not in a lordly domination, but in a holy ministration of the Spirit and truth, so their duty is, to labour in the word and doctrine; to feed the flock of God; to administer the ordinances by virtue of the power and authority received from Christ, and to execute discipline according to the word; and the members of the church are willingly to be subject to them, as the stewards of Christ, and as those who watch for their souls."
</ConfessionBox>[^55]

**1. "The work of pastors being constantly to attend the service of Christ... in the ministry of the word and prayer."** The primary duties of the pastor are preaching and prayer. Acts 6:4 records the apostles' commitment: "We will devote ourselves to prayer and to the ministry of the word."[^56]

**2. "Watching for their souls, as they that must give an account to God."** Hebrews 13:17 exhorts, "Obey your leaders and submit to them, for they are keeping watch over your souls, as those who will have to give an account."[^57]

**3. "The power and authority... consists not in a lordly domination, but in a holy ministration of the Spirit and truth."** The confession rejects a hierarchical, authoritarian view of pastoral ministry. Pastors are servants, not lords.[^58]

**4. "The members of the church are willingly to be subject to them."** The congregation owes its pastors willing submission, not out of compulsion but out of respect for their office.[^27]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 10:** Acts 6:4; Hebrews 13:17; 1 Timothy 3:2; 5:17; 1 Thessalonians 5:12-13; 1 Peter 5:2-3; 2 Timothy 4:2.[^59]

#### Paragraphs 12–13 – Membership and Discipline

Paragraph 12 states: <ConfessionBox>
"All believers ought to join themselves as members of particular churches, when and where they have opportunity so to do; and that for their own profit, and the good of the churches, they should wait upon the ordinances of Christ, and be subject to the government and admonition of the church, in all things, according to the rule of Christ."
</ConfessionBox>[^62]

Paragraph 13 states: <ConfessionBox>
"No church members, upon any offence taken by them, having performed their duty required of them towards the person they are offended at, ought to disturb any church order, or absent themselves from the assemblies of the church, or administration of any ordinances, upon the account of such offence at any of their fellow members, but to wait upon Christ in the further proceeding of the church."
</ConfessionBox>[^63]

**1. "All believers ought to join themselves as members of particular churches."** Membership in a local church is not optional; it is a biblical duty.[^64]

**2. "Subject to the government and admonition of the church."** Members are to be under the discipline of the church.

**3. The process of resolving offenses.** Paragraph 13 addresses how to handle disputes within the church, following Matthew 18.

#### Paragraphs 14–15 – Cooperation Between Churches

Paragraph 14 states: <ConfessionBox>
"In cases of differences among churches, or of persecution, or upon any other weighty consideration, it is lawful for them to meet together in synods or councils, to consider of and debate matters concerning them, to give their advice therein, and to be mutual comforts to each other, but not to exercise any jurisdiction over one another, or over any church in the management of the affairs of worship or discipline which belong to each particular church to manage by itself according to the word of God."
</ConfessionBox>[^66]

Paragraph 15 states: <ConfessionBox>
"Such synods or councils, being assemblies of men, may err, and have erred; therefore they are not to be made the rule of faith or practice, but to be used as a help in both."
</ConfessionBox>[^67]

**1. "It is lawful for them to meet together in synods or councils."** Churches may cooperate for mutual benefit in cases of difference, persecution, or weighty matters.

**2. "To give their advice therein... but not to exercise any jurisdiction."** Synods and councils have **advisory** authority only — not **jurisdictional** authority.[^68]

**3. "They are not to be made the rule of faith or practice, but to be used as a help in both."** Even the decisions of councils are fallible and subject to the Word of God.

**4. A distinctively Baptist ecclesiology.** This contrasts with Presbyterianism, which gives binding authority to presbyteries and synods.[^69]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Reformed Baptist Ecclesiology

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of the church.[^71]

**Pillar 1: The Universal/Invisible Church vs. the Local/Visible Church.** The confession distinguishes between the church as all the elect and the church as local congregations of professing believers.[^72]

**Pillar 2: The Perpetuity of the Church.** Christ always has had, and ever shall have, a kingdom in this world.[^73]

**Pillar 3: Christ as the Exclusive Head.** No pope, bishop, or human authority can usurp Christ's headship.[^74]

**Pillar 4: Regenerate Church Membership.** Only visible saints — those who profess faith and walk in obedience — ought to be members of local churches.[^75]

**Pillar 5: Congregational Autonomy.** Each local church has full power and authority for worship and discipline, with no dependency on outside jurisdiction.[^76]

**Pillar 6: Two Offices: Elders and Deacons.** The New Testament church is to be governed by a plurality of elders (also called bishops/overseers/pastors) and served by deacons.[^77]

**Pillar 7: Congregational Election of Officers.** Elders and deacons are to be chosen by the common suffrage of the church.[^78]

**Pillar 8: The Primacy of Preaching and Prayer.** The pastor's primary duties are the ministry of the word and prayer.[^79]

**Pillar 9: The Authority of Synods Is Advisory, Not Jurisdictional.** Churches may meet in synods for counsel, but they exercise no binding authority.[^80]

**Pillar 10: The Sufficiency of Scripture.** The church must be organized according to the mind of Christ declared in His Word.

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Ecclesiology

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 26.

**Crisis 1: The Loss of the Distinction Between Visible and Invisible Church.** Many confuse the visible church (the local congregation) with the invisible church (the elect). The confession helps believers understand that not everyone in the church is truly saved.[^81]

**Crisis 2: The Neglect of Church Membership.** Many professing Christians see no need to join a local church. Paragraph 12 declares that it is the duty of every believer to join themselves as members.[^82]

**Crisis 3: The Erosion of Regenerate Church Membership.** Many churches admit unregenerate persons to membership. The confession insists that particular congregations ought to be constituted of visible saints.[^83]

**Crisis 4: The Clericalization of Ministry.** Some treat pastors as a separate class with special authority. Paragraph 10 rejects "lordly domination" and insists on pastoral authority as "a holy ministration of the Spirit and truth."[^84]

**Crisis 5: The Hierarchical Model of Church Government.** Some churches adopt Presbyterian or Episcopal models, subjecting the local church to external authority. Paragraph 7 declares that there ought to be "no dependency of one particular church upon another."[^85]

**Crisis 6: The Isolation of Independent Churches.** Some churches so emphasize autonomy that they refuse all cooperation with other churches. Paragraphs 14-15 affirm that it is lawful for churches to meet in synods for mutual counsel.[^86]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Doctrine of the Church

**Step 1: Rejoice in Your Membership in the Universal Church.** If you are a believer, you are part of the elect, the whole number gathered under Christ. Your name is written in heaven.[^87]

**Step 2: Join a Local Church.** Church membership is not optional. Find a gospel-preaching church where the Word is faithfully proclaimed and the ordinances rightly administered.[^88]

**Step 3: Examine Your Profession.** Are you truly a visible saint? Do you profess the faith and walk in obedience? If your life contradicts your profession, you have no right to be considered a member.[^89]

**Step 4: Submit to the Government of the Church.** Christ has given authority to the elders of your church. Submit to their instruction and admonition as those who watch for your soul.[^90]

**Step 5: Pray for Your Pastors.** The work of pastors is constant and demanding. Pray that they would be faithful in the ministry of the word and prayer.[^91]

**Step 6: Participate in Church Discipline.** Do not abandon the church when offenses arise. Follow the process of Matthew 18. Do not absent yourself from the assemblies.[^92]

**Step 7: Cooperate with Other Churches, But Not Under Their Authority.** Join with other churches for mutual counsel and encouragement, but recognize that no external body has jurisdiction over your local church.[^93]

### Conclusion: The Household of God

Chapter 26 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the household of God. The church is not a human organization but a divine institution. It is the body of Christ, the bride of Christ, the fullness of Him who fills all in all. Christ is its exclusive Head. The church is composed of visible saints who profess faith and walk in obedience. Each local church is autonomous, possessing full authority for worship and discipline. Its officers are elders (who preach and pray) and deacons (who serve the temporal needs of the congregation). And churches may cooperate in synods for counsel, but never for control.[^94]

The confession calls believers to rejoice in their membership in the universal church, to join a local church, to examine their profession, to submit to the government of the church, to pray for their pastors, to participate in church discipline, and to cooperate with other churches without ceding authority. The same Christ who is the Head of the universal church is present wherever two or three are gathered in His name. And the same Spirit who unites believers to Christ also unites them to one another in the household of God.[^95]

**A Prayer from Chapter 26** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Christ, the Head of the church, in whom all power is invested: We thank Thee for the church, the body of which Thou art the Head, the bride for whom Thou didst give Thyself. We thank Thee for the universal church, the whole number of the elect gathered under Thee. We thank Thee for particular churches, where visible saints walk together in obedience to Thy Word. Grant us to love Thy church. Give us pastors who will be faithful in the ministry of the word and prayer. Give us deacons who will serve with humility. And keep us from all hierarchy that would usurp Thy headship, and from all isolation that would deny the fellowship of the saints. Build Thy church, and let the gates of hell not prevail against it. For Thy name's sake. Amen.*[^96]
