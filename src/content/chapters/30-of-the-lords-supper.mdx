# He Is Here: Why the Lord's Supper Is the Believer's Spiritual Feast, Not a Repeated Sacrifice

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 30 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Nearest Taste of Heaven

Chapter 30 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Lord's Supper," is one of the longest and most theologically nuanced chapters in the entire confession.[^1] If Chapter 28 established the foundational principles for the two New Covenant ordinances, and Chapter 29 defined the nature, subjects, and mode of baptism, then Chapter 30 completes the exposition of the second ordinance: the Lord's supper.[^2]

The chapter comprises **eight paragraphs**, which can be grouped under five headings:

- **Paragraph 1**: The **institution** of the supper by Christ
- **Paragraph 2**: The **nature** of the supper as a memorial, not a sacrifice
- **Paragraphs 3–4**: The **administration** of the supper (who may administer, the prohibition of withholding the cup, the rejection of adoration of the elements)
- **Paragraphs 5–6**: The **elements** of the supper (bread and wine; no change in substance)
- **Paragraphs 7–8**: The **reception** of the supper (who may receive, the consequences of unworthy participation)[^3]

The chapter stands directly against **Roman Catholic** teaching on four points: (1) that the Lord's supper is a propitiatory sacrifice (the Mass); (2) that the bread and wine are changed into the physical body and blood of Christ (transubstantiation); (3) that the elements are to be adored; and (4) that the cup should be withheld from the laity.[^4] The confession also guards against **Zwinglian** memorialism by affirming a real spiritual presence of Christ in the supper, though not a physical or local presence.[^5]

As one expositor notes, "Chapter 30 is a very full, clear, careful handling of this important matter."[^6] Sam Waldron notes that the chapter is characterized by "repeated movement from positive statement to negative polemic," reflecting its primary polemical context: the refutation of Roman Catholic errors.[^7]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Lord's Supper

Chapter 30 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, purpose, and administration of the Lord's supper.[^8]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Institution: An Everlasting Memorial and Covenant

The confession opens with the institution of the supper: <ConfessionBox>
"The Lord Jesus, in the night wherein he was betrayed, instituted the supper of the Lord to be observed in his churches, unto the end of the world, for the perpetual remembrance and showing forth the sacrifice of himself in his death, the sealing of all benefits thereof unto true believers, their spiritual nourishment and growth in him, their further engagement in and to all duties which they owe unto him, and to be a bond and pledge of their communion with him, and with each other."
</ConfessionBox>[^9]

**1. "The Lord Jesus, in the night wherein he was betrayed, instituted the supper."** The confession anchors the supper in the historical event of Christ's betrayal. Matthew 26:26-28 records: "Now as they were eating, Jesus took bread, and after blessing it broke it and gave it to the disciples, and said, 'Take, eat; this is my body.'"[^10]

**2. "To be observed in his churches, unto the end of the world."** The supper is not a temporary ordinance. 1 Corinthians 11:26 declares, "For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes."[^11]

**3. "For the perpetual remembrance and showing forth the sacrifice of himself in his death."** The supper is a memorial of Christ's once-for-all sacrifice. Luke 22:19 records Jesus' words: "Do this in remembrance of me."[^12]

**4. "The sealing of all benefits thereof unto true believers."** The supper is a seal of the covenant of grace, confirming the benefits of Christ's death to believers. The confession cites 1 Corinthians 10:16-17: "The cup of blessing that we bless, is it not a participation in the blood of Christ?"[^13]

**5. "Their spiritual nourishment and growth in him."** The supper is a means of grace for the believer's spiritual growth. John 6:35 declares, "I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger."[^14]

**6. "Their further engagement in and to all duties which they owe unto him."** The supper strengthens the believer's commitment to obedience.[^15]

**7. "To be a bond and pledge of their communion with him, and with each other."** The supper is both a vertical and horizontal act of communion. 1 Corinthians 10:17 declares, "Because there is one bread, we who are many are one body, for we all partake of the one bread."[^16]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Matthew 26:26-28; 1 Corinthians 11:23-26; Luke 22:19-20; 1 Corinthians 10:16-17; John 6:35.[^17]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Nature: A Memorial, Not a Sacrifice

Paragraph 2 states: <ConfessionBox>
"In this ordinance Christ is not offered up to his Father, nor any real sacrifice made at all for remission of sins of the quick or dead, but only a memorial of that one offering up of himself by himself upon the cross, once for all, and a spiritual oblation of all possible praise unto God for the same; so that the popish sacrifice of the mass, as they call it, is most abominable, injurious to Christ's own only sacrifice, the alone propitiation for all the sins of the elect."
</ConfessionBox>[^18]

**1. "Christ is not offered up to his Father, nor any real sacrifice made."** The confession directly denies the Roman Catholic doctrine of the Mass as a propitiatory sacrifice.[^19]

**2. "Only a memorial of that one offering up of himself... once for all."** The supper is a memorial of a past sacrifice, not a repetition of it. Hebrews 7:27 declares, "He has no need, like those high priests, to offer sacrifices daily, first for his own sins and then for those of the people, since he did this once for all when he offered up himself."[^20]

**3. "A spiritual oblation of all possible praise unto God for the same."** The supper is a sacrifice of praise, not a propitiatory sacrifice. Hebrews 13:15 commands, "Through him then let us continually offer up a sacrifice of praise to God, that is, the fruit of lips that acknowledge his name."[^21]

**4. "The popish sacrifice of the mass... is most abominable."** The confession uses unusually strong language. This reflects the Reformed consensus that the Mass "is injurious to Christ's own only sacrifice, the alone propitiation for all the sins of the elect."[^22]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Hebrews 9:25-26, 28; 10:11-14, 18; Hebrews 13:10-11, 15-16.[^23]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Administration: The Role of the Minister

Paragraph 3 addresses the proper administration of the supper: <ConfessionBox>
"In this ordinance Christ hath appointed his ministers to pray, and bless the elements of bread and wine, and thereby to set them apart from a common to a holy use, and to take and break the bread, to take the cup, and themselves communicating, to give both to the communicants."
</ConfessionBox>[^24]

**1. "His ministers to pray, and bless the elements... and thereby to set them apart."** The confession affirms that the minister (elder/pastor) is the proper administrator of the supper. The act of blessing sets the bread and wine apart for holy use.[^25]

**2. "To take and break the bread, to take the cup, and themselves communicating, to give both to the communicants."** The minister is to partake himself. This guards against a hierarchical separation where the priest administers but does not receive.[^26]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Matthew 26:26-28; Mark 14:22-24; Luke 22:19-20; 1 Corinthians 11:23-26.[^27]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Rejection of Roman Corruptions

Paragraph 4 continues the polemic: <ConfessionBox>
"The denying of the cup to the people, worshipping the elements, the lifting them up, or carrying them about for adoration, and reserving them for any pretended religious use, are all contrary to the nature of this ordinance, and to the institution of Christ."
</ConfessionBox>[^28]

**1. "The denying of the cup to the people."** The confession rejects the Roman Catholic practice of withholding the cup from the laity (communion in one kind).[^29] Matthew 26:27 records that Christ "took a cup, and when he had given thanks he gave it to them, saying, 'Drink of it, all of you.'"[^30]

**2. "Worshipping the elements, the lifting them up, or carrying them about for adoration."** The confession rejects the adoration of the consecrated host.[^31]

**3. "Reserving them for any pretended religious use."** The confession rejects the practice of reserving the elements in a tabernacle or monstrance.[^32]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Matthew 26:27-28; Mark 14:23; 1 Corinthians 11:25-29.[^33]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Elements: Bread and Wine

Paragraph 5 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The outward elements in this ordinance, being rightly set apart to the use ordained by Christ, have such relation to him crucified, as that truly, although in terms used by the Scripture, they are called by the names of the things they represent, to wit, the body and blood of Christ; albeit, in substance and nature, they still remain truly and only bread and wine, as they were before."
</ConfessionBox>[^34]

**1. "They are called by the names of the things they represent... the body and blood of Christ."** The confession affirms the sacramental language of Scripture. When Christ said, "This is my body," He meant that the bread sacramentally represents His body.[^35]

**2. "Albeit, in substance and nature, they still remain truly and only bread and wine, as they were before."** The confession explicitly rejects transubstantiation. 1 Corinthians 11:26-28 speaks of "eating the bread and drinking the cup" after consecration, calling them bread and cup.[^36]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Matthew 26:26-29; 1 Corinthians 11:26-28.[^37]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Real Presence: Spiritual, Not Physical

Paragraph 6 affirms the real spiritual presence of Christ in the supper: <ConfessionBox>
"That doctrine which maintains a change of the substance of bread and wine into the substance of Christ's body and blood (commonly called transubstantiation) by consecration of a priest, or by any other way, is repugnant not to Scripture alone, but even to common sense and reason; overthroweth the nature of the ordinance; and hath been, and is, the cause of manifold superstitions, yea, of gross idolatries."
</ConfessionBox>[^38]

**1. Transubstantiation is repugnant to Scripture, common sense, and reason.** The confession rejects the idea that the bread and wine change into the physical body and blood of Christ.[^39]

**2. The nature of the ordinance is overthrown.** If the elements are no longer bread and wine, the ordinance loses its sacramental character.[^40]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** Acts 3:21; 1 Corinthians 11:24-25; Luke 24:6, 39.[^41]

#### Paragraph 7 – The Worthy Receiver

Paragraph 7 states: <ConfessionBox>
"Worthy receivers, outwardly partaking of the visible elements in this ordinance, do then also inwardly by faith, really and indeed, yet not carnally and corporally, but spiritually, receive and feed upon Christ crucified, and all the benefits of his death; the body and blood of Christ being then not corporally or carnally in, with, or under the bread and wine; yet as really, but spiritually, present to the faith of believers in that ordinance, as the elements themselves are to their outward senses."
</ConfessionBox>[^42]

**1. "Inwardly by faith, really and indeed... spiritually, receive and feed upon Christ crucified."** The confession affirms that believers truly receive Christ in the supper — not physically, but spiritually, by faith.[^43]

**2. "The body and blood of Christ being then not corporally or carnally in, with, or under the bread and wine."** The confession rejects Lutheran consubstantiation.[^44]

**3. "As really, but spiritually, present to the faith of believers."** Christ is as truly present to the believer's faith as the bread and wine are to the believer's senses.[^45]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 7:** 1 Corinthians 10:16-17; 1 Corinthians 11:23-26; John 6:35, 53, 56.[^46]

#### Paragraph 8 – The Unworthy Receiver

The confession concludes: <ConfessionBox>
"All ignorant and ungodly persons, as they are unfit to enjoy communion with Christ, so are they unworthy of the Lord's table, and cannot, without great sin against Christ, while they remain such, partake of these holy mysteries, or be admitted thereunto; yea, whosoever shall receive unworthily, are guilty of the body and blood of the Lord, eating and drinking judgment to themselves."
</ConfessionBox>[^47]

**1. "All ignorant and ungodly persons... are unworthy of the Lord's table."** The confession teaches that unbelievers ought not to partake of the supper. 2 Corinthians 6:14 commands, "Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers."[^48]

**2. "Whosoever shall receive unworthily, are guilty of the body and blood of the Lord."** 1 Corinthians 11:27-29 warns, "Whoever, therefore, eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord."[^49]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 8:** 2 Corinthians 6:14; 1 Corinthians 11:27-29; Matthew 7:6.[^50]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Reformed Baptist Doctrine of the Lord's Supper

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of the Lord's supper.[^51]

**Pillar 1: A Memorial, Not a Sacrifice.** The supper is not a propitiatory sacrifice (contra Rome), but a memorial of Christ's once-for-all sacrifice.[^52]

**Pillar 2: Spiritual Presence.** Christ is truly present to the believer's faith, not physically in the elements (contra Luther), but spiritually.[^53]

**Pillar 3: No Transubstantiation.** The bread and wine remain bread and wine after consecration.[^54]

**Pillar 4: Communion in Both Kinds.** The cup must be given to all communicants.[^55]

**Pillar 5: No Adoration of the Elements.** The bread and wine are not to be worshipped.[^56]

**Pillar 6: Worthy Reception Required.** Unbelievers and ungodly persons may not partake.[^57]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Corrupted Lord's Supper

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 30.

**Crisis 1: The Roman Catholic Mass.** The confession's rejection of the Mass as "most abominable" and "injurious to Christ's only sacrifice" remains relevant wherever the doctrine of transubstantiation is taught.[^58]

**Crisis 2: Memorialism.** Some Protestants treat the supper as a bare memorial, denying any real spiritual presence. The confession affirms that believers "spiritually receive and feed upon Christ crucified."[^59]

**Crisis 3: The Neglect of the Lord's Supper.** Many churches relegate the supper to infrequent observance. The confession calls for its observance "unto the end of the world."[^60]

**Crisis 4: Open Communion Without Discernment.** Some churches admit the unregenerate to the table. The confession insists that "ignorant and ungodly persons... are unworthy of the Lord's table."[^61]

**Crisis 5: The Loss of the Supper's Significance.** Many believers approach the table casually. The confession calls for self-examination and worthy reception.[^62]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Lord's Supper

**Step 1: Partake Frequently.** The supper is to be observed "unto the end of the world." Do not neglect the table.[^63]

**Step 2: Examine Yourself.** Before coming to the table, examine your life for unconfessed sin. Come not presumptuously but with humility and repentance.[^64]

**Step 3: Look Beyond the Elements.** See the bread and wine as signs pointing to the body and blood of Christ. Feed on Christ by faith.[^65]

**Step 4: Rejoice in the Real Presence.** Christ is truly present to your faith when you partake. He is not physically in the bread, but He is spiritually with you.[^66]

**Step 5: Discern the Body.** Recognize that you are partaking in communion not only with Christ but with all believers. Let the supper strengthen your love for the church.[^67]

**Step 6: Reject False Teaching.** Do not be misled by the doctrine of transubstantiation or the practice of withholding the cup. Follow Christ's institution.[^68]

**Step 7: Come Worthily.** Do not come to the table while living in unrepentant sin. But do not stay away out of a false humility either. Come with faith, repentance, and love.[^69]

### Conclusion: He Is Here

Chapter 30 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the table where Christ meets His people. The Lord's supper is a memorial, not a sacrifice; a feast, not a re-offering. The elements remain bread and wine, yet Christ is truly, spiritually present to the faith of the believer. The cup is for all. The table is for the worthy. And the supper is to be observed until the Lord returns.[^70]

This is the nearest taste of heaven on earth. Here, at the table, we proclaim His death. Here, we feed on Him by faith. Here, we are united to Him and to one another. He is not absent. He is here.[^71]

**A Prayer from Chapter 30** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Lord Jesus, who in the night in which Thou wast betrayed didst institute this supper: We thank Thee for this ordinance, the perpetual memorial of Thy death. We thank Thee that Thou art not offered again, but that we partake of the benefits of Thy one sacrifice. We thank Thee that though the bread and wine remain bread and wine, Thou art truly, spiritually present to our faith. Grant us to partake worthily, examining ourselves, discerning Thy body, and feeding on Thee by faith. Let this supper be to us spiritual nourishment and growth in Thee, a bond of our communion with Thee and with each other. Until Thou comest again, let us proclaim Thy death. For Thy name's sake. Amen.*[^72]
