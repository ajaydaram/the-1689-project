# The Article on Which the Church Stands or Falls: Why Justification by Faith Alone Is the Heart of the Gospel

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 11 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Doctrine That Determs Eternity

Chapter 11 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled “Of Justification,” stands as the material principle of the Reformation—the doctrine upon which the church stands or falls (*articulus stantis et cadentis ecclesiae*).[^1] If Chapter 3 declares God’s eternal decree of election, Chapter 6 diagnoses the universal bondage of sin, Chapter 8 presents the person and work of Christ the Mediator, and Chapter 10 describes effectual calling, then Chapter 11 explains the *legal basis* upon which a sinner is declared righteous before the holy God.[^2]

As Sam Waldron notes, “Justification is the article by which the church stands or falls because it alone answers the sinner’s deepest terror and opens the door to unshakeable assurance.”[^3] The chapter comprises six paragraphs, each addressing a distinct aspect of this doctrine: the nature and ground of justification (paragraph 1), the instrument of faith (paragraph 2), the satisfaction of Christ (paragraph 3), the eternal decree and its application (paragraph 4), the preservation of the justified and the place of confession (paragraph 5), and the unity of the way of salvation across both Testaments (paragraph 6).[^4]

The confession’s language is deliberately forensic (legal) rather than transformative. Justification is not a process of becoming righteous but a declaration of being righteous on the basis of another’s righteousness. This stands against the Roman Catholic doctrine of infusion, defined at the Council of Trent (1547), which anathematized anyone who says that “the justice of Christ is imputed to them” or that “the formal cause of justification is the righteousness of Christ.”[^5] The 1689 Confession stands with Luther, Calvin, and the Reformed tradition in affirming that we are justified *by faith alone* because we are justified *by Christ alone*.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Justification

Chapter 11 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on justification, drawing especially on the Apostle Paul’s letters to the Romans and Galatians, as well as the Old Testament witness of Abraham and David.[^6]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Nature and Ground of Justification

The confession opens with a declaration that is both negative and positive: **“Those whom God effectually calls, he also freely justifies; not by infusing righteousness into them, but by pardoning their sins, and by accounting and accepting them as righteous; not for anything wrought in them, or done by them, but for Christ’s sake alone; not by imputing faith itself, the act of believing, or any other evangelical obedience to them, as their righteousness; but by imputing Christ’s active obedience unto the whole law, and passive obedience in his death for their whole and sole righteousness, they receiving and resting on him and his righteousness by faith; which faith they have not of themselves, it is the gift of God.”**[^7]

**1. The Negative Ground: Not by Infusion.** “Not by infusing righteousness into them.”[^8] This clause directly targets the Roman Catholic doctrine of justification, which teaches that grace is “poured into” the soul (*gratia infusa*), making the sinner inwardly righteous, and that this inherent righteousness is the formal cause of justification.[^9] The confession rejects this because (a) it confuses justification with sanctification, (b) it cannot provide assurance (since no one knows if they have enough inherent righteousness), and (c) it denies the sufficiency of Christ’s alien righteousness.[^10]

**2. The Positive Ground: Pardon and Acceptance.** “By pardoning their sins, and by accounting and accepting them as righteous.”[^11] Justification has two components: forgiveness (remission of the penalty for sin) and acceptance (reckoning as righteous). Both are grounded entirely outside the sinner. The confession cites Romans 3:24: “Justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.”[^12]

**3. The Sole Basis: Christ’s Active and Passive Obedience.** The confession makes a crucial distinction between Christ’s **active obedience** (his perfect keeping of the law throughout his life) and his **passive obedience** (his suffering and death on the cross).[^13] Both are imputed to the believer. Without the active obedience, believers would have only their sins forgiven but no positive righteousness to stand before God’s judgment. As the Westminster Larger Catechism states, “Christ’s active obedience is the righteousness of the law fulfilled by him for us.”[^14] The confession cites Romans 5:17-19 and 2 Corinthians 5:21.

**4. Faith Is Not the Righteousness Itself.** “Not by imputing faith itself, the act of believing, or any other evangelical obedience to them, as their righteousness.”[^15] Faith is the *instrument* that receives Christ’s righteousness, not the *basis* of justification. The Roman Catholic charge that Protestants make faith into a meritorious work is thus refuted. Faith is an empty hand. The confession cites Philippians 3:8-9: “Not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ.”[^16]

**5. Faith Is a Gift.** “Which faith they have not of themselves, it is the gift of God.”[^17] Even the faith that receives Christ is not a human work; it is the gift of God (Ephesians 2:8-9). This guards against any form of semi-Pelagianism that would make the human decision the decisive factor.

#### Paragraph 2 – Faith: The Alone Instrument, Yet Never Alone

<ConfessionBox>
"Faith thus receiving and resting on Christ and his righteousness, is the alone instrument of justification; yet it is not alone in the person justified, but is ever accompanied with all other saving graces, and is no dead faith, but works by love."
</ConfessionBox>[^19]

**1. The Alone Instrument.** Faith is the *instrumental* cause of justification—the means by which the sinner receives Christ’s righteousness. The confession cites Romans 3:28: “For we hold that one is justified by faith apart from works of the law.”[^20] This is the *sola fide* of the Reformation.

**2. Yet Never Alone.** The confession immediately adds that justifying faith is “not alone in the person justified, but is ever accompanied with all other saving graces.”[^21] This guards against antinomianism. The faith that saves is a living faith, not a dead profession. As James 2:17 declares, “Faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead.”[^22] The confession cites Galatians 5:6: “For in Christ Jesus neither circumcision nor uncircumcision counts for anything, but only faith working through love.”[^23]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Romans 3:28; Galatians 5:6; James 2:17, 22, 26.[^24]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Perfect Satisfaction of Christ

<ConfessionBox>
"Christ, by his obedience and death, did fully discharge the debt of all those who are justified; and did, by the sacrifice of himself in the blood of his cross, undergoing in their stead the penalty due to them, make a proper, real, and full satisfaction to God’s justice in their behalf. Yet, inasmuch as he was given by the Father for them, and his obedience and satisfaction accepted in their stead, and both freely, not for anything in them, their justification is only of free grace; that both the exact justice and rich grace of God might be glorified in the justification of sinners."
</ConfessionBox>[^25]

**1. Full Discharge of Debt.** “Christ did fully discharge the debt of all those who are justified.”[^26] The confession uses legal language: a debt is paid in full. Colossians 2:14 declares that Christ “canceled the record of debt that stood against us.”[^27]

**2. Proper, Real, and Full Satisfaction.** The confession rejects any notion that Christ’s death was merely a moral example or a token payment. It was “proper, real, and full satisfaction to God’s justice.”[^28] Anselm of Canterbury’s *Cur Deus Homo* (“Why God Became Man”) is echoed here: sin against an infinite God requires an infinite satisfaction, which only the God-man could provide.[^29]

**3. Free Grace.** “Their justification is only of free grace.”[^30] Even though Christ satisfied divine justice, the application of that satisfaction to the sinner is still of grace, not because the sinner earned it. The confession cites Romans 3:24 (“justified freely by his grace”) and Ephesians 1:7 (“redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins”).[^31]

**4. The Glory of Both Justice and Grace.** The confession concludes that justification displays both God’s “exact justice” (because the penalty was fully paid) and “rich grace” (because it was paid for the ungodly). Romans 3:26 declares that God is both “just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus.”[^32]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Hebrews 10:14; 1 Peter 1:18-19; Isaiah 53:5-6; Romans 8:32; 2 Corinthians 5:21; Romans 3:26; Ephesians 1:6-7.[^33]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Decree, Accomplishment, and Application

<ConfessionBox>
"God did from all eternity decree to justify all the elect; and Christ did in the fullness of time die for their sins, and rise again for their justification; nevertheless, they are not justified personally, until the Holy Spirit in time does actually apply Christ to them."
</ConfessionBox>[^34]

**1. The Eternal Decree.** “God did from all eternity decree to justify all the elect.”[^35] Justification is not an afterthought but part of God’s eternal plan. The confession cites Galatians 3:8 (“the Scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the Gentiles by faith, preached the gospel beforehand to Abraham”).[^36]

**2. The Historical Accomplishment.** “Christ did in the fullness of time die for their sins, and rise again for their justification.”[^37] Romans 4:25 declares that Christ “was delivered up for our trespasses and raised for our justification.”[^38]

**3. The Personal Application.** “Nevertheless, they are not justified personally, until the Holy Spirit in time does actually apply Christ to them.”[^39] This clause distinguishes between objective redemption (accomplished by Christ) and subjective application (applied by the Spirit). The confession cites Colossians 1:21-22: “And you, who once were alienated and hostile in mind, doing evil deeds, he has now reconciled in his body of flesh by his death.”[^40]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Galatians 3:8; 1 Peter 1:2; 1 Timothy 2:6; Romans 4:25; Colossians 1:21-22; Titus 3:4-7.[^41]

#### Paragraph 5 – Preservation and Fatherly Discipline

<ConfessionBox>
"Although they can never fall from the state of justification, yet they may, by their sins, fall under God’s fatherly displeasure; and in that condition, they usually do not have the light of his countenance restored to them, until they humble themselves, confess their sins, beg pardon, and renew their faith and repentance."
</ConfessionBox>[^42]

**1. Eternal Security.** “They can never fall from the state of justification.”[^43] The confession affirms the perseverance of the saints. John 10:28 declares, “No one will snatch them out of my hand.”[^44]

**2. Fatherly Displeasure.** Yet believers may “fall under God’s fatherly displeasure” through sin.[^45] This is not judicial wrath (which was exhausted at the cross) but parental discipline. Psalm 89:31-33 states, “If they violate my statutes… I will punish their transgression with the rod, but I will not remove my steadfast love from him.”[^46]

**3. The Path to Restoration.** “They usually do not have the light of his countenance restored to them, until they humble themselves, confess their sins, beg pardon, and renew their faith and repentance.”[^47] The confession cites Psalm 32:5 (“I acknowledged my sin to you… and you forgave the iniquity of my sin”), Psalm 51, and Matthew 26:75 (Peter’s weeping after his denial).[^48]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Matthew 6:12; 1 John 1:7,9; John 10:28; Psalm 89:31-33; Psalm 32:5; Psalm 51; Matthew 26:75.[^49]

#### Paragraph 6 – One Gospel Across the Testaments

<ConfessionBox>
"The justification of believers under the Old Testament was, in all these respects, one and the same with the justification of believers under the New Testament."
</ConfessionBox>[^50]

**1. The Unity of the Covenant of Grace.** Abraham was justified by faith, not by works (Genesis 15:6; Romans 4:1-5). David speaks of the blessedness of the man to whom God “counts righteousness apart from works” (Romans 4:6-8).[^51]

**2. The Same Christ, Same Faith.** The confession cites Galatians 3:9: “So then, those who are of faith are blessed along with Abraham, the man of faith.”[^52] The object of Old Testament faith was Christ, though seen through types and shadows. Hebrews 13:8 declares, “Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever.”[^53]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** Galatians 3:9; Romans 4:22-24.[^54]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Justification

The confession’s doctrine of justification rests on six interconnected theological pillars.[^55]

**Pillar 1: Forensic Declaration, Not Infusion.** Justification is a judicial act of God, not a transformative process. This is the non-negotiable distinction from Rome.[^56]

**Pillar 2: Double Imputation.** Our sins are imputed to Christ; Christ’s righteousness is imputed to us. This is the “happy exchange” (Luther).[^57]

**Pillar 3: The Alien Righteousness (*iustitia aliena*).** The righteousness that justifies is not inherent in the believer but outside him (*extra nos*)—in Christ.[^58]

**Pillar 4: *Sola Fide* as the Alone Instrument.** Faith justifies not as a work but as the hand that receives Christ.[^59]

**Pillar 5: The Union of Justification and Sanctification.** The faith that justifies is never alone; it produces good works, though works are not the ground of justification.[^60]

**Pillar 6: The Unity of the Gospel.** Old and New Testament believers are justified in the same way—by grace through faith.[^61]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Justification

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 11.[^62]

**Crisis 1: The Roman Catholic Shadow.** Many evangelicals functionally believe that their standing with God fluctuates with their performance. The confession’s alien righteousness is the only remedy.[^63]

**Crisis 2: Antinomianism.** Some argue that since justification is by faith alone, holiness does not matter. Paragraph 2 refutes this: justifying faith works by love.[^64]

**Crisis 3: The Loss of Assurance.** Without imputed righteousness, assurance is impossible. The confession grounds assurance in Christ’s finished work, not in subjective feelings.[^65]

**Crisis 4: The Confusion of Justification and Sanctification.** Many believers cannot distinguish between being declared righteous and being made righteous. Paragraph 1 makes the distinction clear.[^66]

**Crisis 5: The Neglect of Confession in the Justified.** Paragraph 5 shows that while believers cannot lose justification, they can lose the sense of God’s favor until they confess and repent.[^67]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Justification

**Step 1: Preach the Alien Righteousness to Yourself Daily.** Remind yourself each morning that your standing with God does not depend on your performance but on Christ’s.[^68]

**Step 2: Distinguish Justification from Sanctification.** Do not confuse your position with your progress. One is complete; the other is ongoing.[^69]

**Step 3: Practice Daily Confession.** When you sin, do not doubt your justification. Confess your sin to restore fellowship, not to be re-justified.[^70]

**Step 4: Reject All Works-Righteousness.** Do not add any human merit—not your faith, not your obedience, not your repentance—as the ground of your acceptance.[^71]

**Step 5: Rejoice in the Same Gospel as Abraham.** Read the Old Testament as a book of grace, not law. Abraham saw Christ’s day and was glad.[^72]

### Conclusion: The Article on Which the Church Stands or Falls

Chapter 11 is the heartbeat of the 1689 Baptist Confession. Without justification by faith alone, there is no gospel. But because Christ’s active and passive obedience is imputed to believers, received by faith alone (which is the gift of God), the sinner can stand before the holy God—not in his own righteousness, but in the righteousness of another. This is the alien righteousness. This is the only hope. This is the article by which the church stands or falls.[^73]

**A Prayer from Chapter 11** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Holy God, the righteous Judge: I come before You empty-handed. I do not bring my good works, my theological knowledge, my ministry success, or my repentant tears. I bring nothing but the righteousness of Your Son—his active obedience to the whole law and his passive obedience in death—imputed to me by faith alone. I receive Him. I rest on Him. And because of Him, I am justified, now and forever. For Christ’s sake. Amen.*[^74]
