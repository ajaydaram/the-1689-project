# Buried in Death and Resurrection: Why Believer's Baptism Is the Unique Sign of the New Covenant

## Buried in Death and Resurrection: Why Believer's Baptism Is the Unique Sign of the New Covenant

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 29 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

Chapter 29 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled “Of Baptism,” is the first of two chapters dedicated to the two New Covenant ordinances, serving as the logical sequel to the foundational principles established in Chapter 28. Though the chapter is short, comprising only four paragraphs, its theological weight is immense. As one commentator notes, “In the ordinance of baptism, we see a complete departure from the Westminster Confession and Savoy Declaration.”[^2] Here, the framers of the Confession—Particular Baptists meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—articulated their distinct understanding of baptism as a sign of union with Christ and a public identification with the triune God.[^3]

The chapter unfolds in a logical progression: the spiritual significance of baptism (paragraph 1); the proper subjects of baptism (paragraph 2); the outward element and Trinitarian formula (paragraph 3); and the necessary mode of immersion (paragraph 4).

This chapter stands against two errors: **paedobaptism** (the baptism of infants) which the Confession rejects because it lacks a credible profession of repentance and faith, and **the error of those who deny that baptism is a sign and seal of the covenant of grace** because they misunderstand the New Covenant’s nature.

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Baptism

Chapter 29 is a masterful digest of Scripture’s teaching on the nature, subjects, element, and mode of baptism.

#### Paragraph 1 – The Significance of Baptism: A Sign of Union with Christ

The confession opens by defining what baptism signifies: <ConfessionBox>
"Baptism is an ordinance of the New Testament, ordained by Jesus Christ, to be unto the party baptized, a sign of his fellowship with him, in his death and resurrection; of his being engrafted into him; of remission of sins; and of giving up into God, through Jesus Christ, to live and walk in newness of life."
</ConfessionBox>[^1]

**1. “An ordinance of the New Testament.”** The confession is careful to locate baptism within the New Covenant. Unlike circumcision, which was the sign of the Old Covenant (Genesis 17), baptism is not a continuation of an Old Testament rite under a new administration. The term **ordinance** (rather than “sacrament”) distances the Confession from any notion that baptism automatically conveys grace apart from faith.

**2. “Ordained by Jesus Christ.”** The confession grounds baptism in the authority of Christ alone. Matthew 28:19 records the Great Commission: “Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.”

**3. A sign of fellowship in Christ’s death and resurrection.** Baptism is a visible sign of union with Christ in His death and resurrection. The confession cites Romans 6:3-5 (“Do you not know that all of us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death?”) and Colossians 2:12 (“buried with him in baptism, in which you were also raised with him through faith in the powerful working of God”).

**4. A sign of being engrafted into Him.** Baptism signifies incorporation into Christ. Galatians 3:27 declares, “For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ.”

**5. A sign of remission of sins.** Baptism signifies the washing away of sins. Mark 1:4 speaks of “a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins,” and Acts 22:16 commands, “Rise and be baptized and wash away your sins, calling on his name.”

**6. A sign of giving up to God to walk in newness of life.** Baptism is not merely a past event but a present reality. It signifies the believer’s commitment to live a new life. Romans 6:4 declares, “We were buried therefore with him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, we too might walk in newness of life.”

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Romans 6:3-5; Colossians 2:12; Galatians 3:27; Mark 1:4; Acts 22:16; Romans 6:4.

#### Paragraph 2 – The Proper Subjects of Baptism: Believers Only

Paragraph 2 states: <ConfessionBox>
"Those who do actually profess repentance towards God, faith in, and obedience to, our Lord Jesus Christ, are the only proper subjects of this ordinance."
</ConfessionBox>[^4]

**1. “Those who do actually profess repentance towards God.”** The confession insists that only those who give evidence of genuine repentance are proper candidates for baptism. Mark 16:16 declares, “Whoever believes and is baptized will be saved.” The confession cites Acts 8:36-37: “See, here is water! What prevents me from being baptized?” And Philip said, “If you believe with all your heart, you may.” The Ethiopian eunuch’s baptism was preceded by his profession of faith.

**2. “Faith in, and obedience to, our Lord Jesus Christ.”** The confession lists three elements of the credible profession: repentance, faith, and obedience. This guards against a mere intellectual assent that does not issue in transformed living. Acts 2:41 records that “those who received his word were baptized.” Acts 8:12 speaks of those who “believed Philip as he preached good news about the kingdom of God and the name of Jesus Christ, they were baptized, both men and women.”

**3. “The only proper subjects.”** The confession’s language is exclusive. It explicitly rejects infant baptism as well as the baptism of anyone who cannot make a credible profession of repentance and faith. This stands in direct contrast to the Westminster Confession, which taught that the infants of believing parents are to be baptized. As one sermon notes, baptism is “for believers only — those whose hearts have been renewed by the Spirit and whose affections are set on Christ.”[^5][^6]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Mark 16:16; Acts 8:36-37; Acts 2:41; Acts 8:12; Acts 18:8.

#### Paragraph 3 – The Outward Element and the Trinitarian Formula

Paragraph 3 states: <ConfessionBox>
"The outward element to be used in this ordinance is water, wherein the party is to be baptized, in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."
</ConfessionBox>[^7]

**1. “The outward element… is water.”** The confession affirms the use of water as the material element of baptism. John the Baptist baptized with water (Matthew 3:11). Acts 8:38 records that Philip and the eunuch “went down into the water.”

**2. “Wherein the party is to be baptized, in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit.”** The confession affirms the Trinitarian formula given by Christ in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). This formula distinguishes Christian baptism from the baptism of John, which was not administered in the name of the triune God.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Matthew 28:19-20; Acts 8:38.

#### Paragraph 4 – The Necessary Mode: Immersion

The confession concludes with a declaration of the mode of baptism: <ConfessionBox>
"Immersion, or dipping of the person in water, is necessary to the due administration of this ordinance."
</ConfessionBox>[^8]

**1. “Immersion, or dipping of the person in water, is necessary.”** The confession teaches that immersion is not merely a permissible option but the required mode. The very word “baptism” (Greek *baptizo*) means “to dip, to plunge, to immerse.” As one sermon explains, “The mode of water baptism by immersion is distinctly ‘baptist.’ By better understanding the symbolism of baptism … we can grasp more and more fully the powerful imagery of baptism as it relates to our union with Christ as mediator.”[^9]

**2. The scriptural basis for immersion.** The confession cites Matthew 3:16: “And when Jesus was baptized, immediately he went up from the water.” Jesus came **up from** the water, implying He had been **in** the water. John 3:23 records that “John also was baptizing at Aenon near Salim, because water was plentiful there.” The need for “plentiful water” indicates immersion, not sprinkling or pouring.

**3. The symbolism of immersion.** Immersion alone pictures the reality of being buried with Christ and raised with Him to new life (Romans 6:4). Sprinkling cannot capture the imagery of a burial and a resurrection.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** Matthew 3:16; John 3:23.

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Baptist Doctrine of Baptism

Several theological pillars support the confession’s doctrine of baptism.

**Pillar 1: Baptism Is a Sign, Not the Substance.** Baptism is not a means of saving grace in itself. It is a visible sign pointing to the invisible realities of union with Christ, remission of sins, and newness of life. As Joseph Rios states, baptism is “a sign of union with Christ, a seal of the covenant of grace, and a public identification with the triune God.”

**Pillar 2: Believers Only.** The only proper subjects of baptism are those who make a credible profession of repentance, faith, and obedience. As one exposition notes, baptism is “for believers only — those whose hearts have been renewed by the Spirit and whose affections are set on Christ.”

**Pillar 3: Immersion Is the Necessary Mode.** The Greek word *baptizo* means “to dip, to immerse.” Only immersion adequately symbolizes burial with Christ and resurrection to new life.

**Pillar 4: The Trinitarian Formula.** Baptism is to be administered in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, as Christ commanded in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19).

**Pillar 5: Baptism Is Not Regenerative.** Unlike Roman Catholic teaching, the confession rejects the notion that baptism conveys saving grace *ex opere operato* (by the work performed). Baptism is a sign, not the substance; a seal, not the source. It must be accompanied by faith to be effectual. As Blue Letter Bible notes, “Creeds, catechisms, and confessions do not replace Scripture, nor the authority of Scripture.”

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted Doctrine of Baptism

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 29.

**Crisis 1: Infant Baptism.** The practice of baptizing infants lacks New Testament warrant. The confession’s insistence that only “those who do actually profess repentance” are the proper subjects explicitly rejects paedobaptism. As one sermon notes, the confession “removes the concept that baptism is a ‘sign and seal’ of the Covenant of Grace. This strikes at the heart of the theology that has become the foundation of infant baptism in reformed theology for hundreds of years.”

**Crisis 2: Baptismal Regeneration.** Some traditions teach that baptism itself washes away sin and regenerates the soul. The confession teaches that baptism is a **sign** of remission of sins, not the instrument that accomplishes it. The Ethiopian eunuch’s baptism followed his profession of faith (Acts 8:37); it did not create it.

**Crisis 3: Sprinkling or Pouring.** Many churches practice sprinkling or pouring as modes of baptism. The confession’s insistence on “immersion, or dipping of the person in water” rejects these alternatives. As one sermon notes, “the word for baptism itself … as well as the examples in scripture” demonstrate that immersion is the biblical mode.

**Crisis 4: The Neglect of Baptism Altogether.** Some churches treat baptism as an optional extra. The confession grounds baptism in the Great Commission: “Go … make disciples … baptizing them.” Obedience to Christ requires the administration of baptism. As one sermon states, “Baptism is an ordinance of the New Testament, ordained by Jesus Christ, to be continued in his church to the end of the world.”

**Crisis 5: Unbaptized Believers.** Many professing believers have never been baptized. The confession, following Scripture, commands that “those who do actually profess repentance … are the only proper subjects of this ordinance.” Every believer who has not yet been baptized should be baptized as an act of obedience to Christ.

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Baptism

**Step 1: Be Baptized if You Have Not Been.** If you have professed faith in Christ but have not been baptized by immersion, obey the Lord’s command. Baptism does not save you, but it is the ordinance that Christ appointed to signify your union with Him. 

**Step 2: See Baptism as a Picture of Your Union with Christ.** When you are baptized, remember that you are being buried with Christ and raised with Him to newness of life (Romans 6:4). Let baptism be a means of reminding yourself of the gospel’s reality in your life. As one sermon states, baptism is “a sign of fellowship with him, in his death and resurrection; of his being engrafted into him; of remission of sins; and of giving up into God … to live and walk in newness of life.”

**Step 3: Baptize Only Believers.** If you are a pastor or church leader, do not baptize infants or those who cannot make a credible profession of faith. Follow the New Testament pattern: preach the gospel, receive professions of repentance and faith, and then baptize those who believe.

**Step 4: Baptize by Immersion.** Administer baptism only by immersion. Going down into the water and coming up out of it pictures burial and resurrection — the very heart of the gospel. 

**Step 5: Use the Trinitarian Formula.** Baptize in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, as Christ commanded (Matthew 28:19). This distinguishes Christian baptism from the baptism of John.

**Step 6: Preach Baptism as a Public Testimony.** Encourage believers to see baptism not as a private devotion but as a public identification with Christ and His church. As one sermon notes, baptism is “a public identification with the triune God.”

### Conclusion: The Picture of the Gospel in Water

Chapter 29 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer’s guide to the ordinance that pictures the gospel in water. Baptism is not a human invention but a divine institution, ordained by Jesus Christ, the only lawgiver of the church. It is not a work that earns salvation but a sign that points to it — a visible picture of the believer’s union with Christ in His death, burial, and resurrection. It is not for infants or for the unregenerate, but only for those who repent and believe. And it is to be administered not by sprinkling or pouring, but by immersion, which alone pictures the reality of being buried with Christ and raised with Him to newness of life. 

This is not the tradition of men; it is the command of Christ. And it is to be continued in His church, and for His glory, until the end of the world. May we obey Him, for His name’s sake.

**A Prayer from Chapter 29** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Lord Jesus, the Head of the church, who by Thy blood hast purchased for us every blessing of the New Covenant: We thank Thee for the ordinance of baptism, that visible sign of our union with Thee. We thank Thee that in baptism we see a picture of Thy death and resurrection, the remission of sins, and the call to walk in newness of life. Grant that every believer who has not been baptized may obediently receive this ordinance. Grant that Thy church may baptize only those who profess repentance and faith, and that she may baptize them by immersion, in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Let not baptism be a mere ritual, but a means of grace that strengthens our union with Thee and our testimony before the world. Until Thou comest again, let the waters of baptism proclaim the gospel. For Thy name’s sake. Amen.*
