# The Heart of the Gospel: Why Jesus Christ Is the Only Mediator Between God and Man\n\n## The Heart of the Gospel: Why Jesus Christ Is the Only Mediator Between God and Man

### (1689 Baptist Confession, Chapter 8 – Complete Exposition with Full Footnotes)

### Introduction: The Center of the Confession

Chapter 8 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Christ the Mediator," stands at the very center of the confession's soteriology.[^1] If Chapter 3 declares God's eternal decree, Chapter 4 His creative act, Chapter 5 His providential governance, Chapter 6 the tragedy of the fall, and Chapter 7 the covenant structure of redemption, then Chapter 8 unveils the *Person* who alone accomplishes redemption: the Lord Jesus Christ, the one Mediator between God and man.[^2] As the Westminster Shorter Catechism asks, "Who is the Redeemer of God's elect?" The answer: "The only Redeemer of God's elect is the Lord Jesus Christ, who, being the eternal Son of God, became man, and so was, and continueth to be, God and man, in two distinct natures, and one person, forever."[^3] This is the substance of Chapter 8.

The chapter comprises ten paragraphs, following a logical progression that Samuel Waldron outlines as follows: the ordination of Christ to the mediatorial office (paragraph 1), the incarnation (paragraph 2), the qualifications for the office (paragraph 3), the execution of the office through active and passive obedience (paragraph 4), the central operations of resurrection, ascension, intercession, and return (paragraph 5), the efficacy of Christ's work for Old Testament saints (paragraph 6), the communication of properties (paragraph 7), the effectual application of redemption (paragraph 8), the exclusivity of Christ's mediatorship (paragraph 9), and the necessity of the threefold office (paragraph 10).[^4]

The confession's compilers—Particular Baptist elders meeting in London in 1677 and again in 1689—grounded every assertion in Scripture, drawing on the creedal heritage of the ancient church (especially the Nicene and Chalcedonian definitions) while remaining faithful to the biblical text.[^5] As Tom Nettles observes, the "carefully crafted language" of the Second London Confession "protected the unity of Christ's person" against ancient and modern heresies.[^6]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Christ's Mediatorial Work

Chapter 8 is a masterpiece of biblical synthesis, drawing on the whole counsel of God to present a comprehensive Christology.[^7]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Ordination of the Mediator: The Eternal Covenant Between Father and Son

The confession opens with a declaration that reaches back into eternity: <ConfessionBox>\n**"It pleased God, in His eternal purpose, to choose and ordain the Lord Jesus, His only begotten Son, according to the covenant made between them both, to be the mediator between God and man; the prophet, priest, and king; the head and savior of His church; the heir of all things; and judge of all the world; unto whom He did from all eternity give a people to be His seed, and to be by Him in time redeemed, called, justified, sanctified, and glorified."**[^8]\n</ConfessionBox>

**1. The Author of the Ordination: "It pleased God."**[^9] The confession begins with the sovereign good pleasure of God the Father. The Father was not reluctant to send His Son; He was pleased to do so. Isaiah 53:10 declares, "Yet it was the will of the LORD to crush him; he has put him to grief."[^10] John 3:16 affirms that God "so loved the world that He gave His only Son."[^11] The initiative in salvation is entirely from the Father. As Waldron notes, "The Father is the author of the ordination of the Son to His mediatorial office."[^12]

**2. The Object of the Ordination: "The Lord Jesus, His only begotten Son."**[^13] Christ Himself is elect—the "Prime Elect" of God.[^14] The language of "only begotten" (Greek *monogenēs*) emphasizes His unique, one-of-a-kind relationship to the Father, not merely a historical sonship but an eternal sonship. John 1:14 declares, "We have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father."[^15]

**3. The Framework of the Ordination: "According to the covenant made between them both."**[^16] This is the covenant of redemption, the eternal pact between the Father and the Son.[^17] In this covenant, the Father agreed to give the Son a people to be His seed (John 17:6), and the Son agreed to redeem them by His incarnation, obedience, and death (Hebrews 10:5-10).[^18] As the Founders Ministries article explains, "The covenant of redemption is the foundation of all redemptive history."[^19]

**4. The Office: "Mediator between God and man; the prophet, priest, and king."**[^20] A mediator is one who interposes between parties at enmity for the purpose of reconciliation. A mediator must be able to represent both sides; therefore, Christ must be both truly God and truly man (1 Timothy 2:5).[^21] The confession then specifies the threefold mediatorial office, which corresponds to the threefold misery of fallen humanity: ignorance (prophet), alienation (priest), and bondage (king).[^22] As prophet, Christ reveals the will of God (Deuteronomy 18:18; John 1:18). As priest, He offers Himself as a sacrifice and intercedes for His people (Hebrews 4:14-16; 7:25). As king, He rules over His church and subdues all His enemies (Psalm 2:6; 110:1-2).[^23]

**5. The Specific Roles: "Head and savior of His church; the heir of all things; and judge of all the world."**[^24] As head of the church, He is the source of life and authority to His body (Ephesians 1:22-23). As heir of all things, He possesses all the blessings of the new creation (Hebrews 1:2). As judge of all the world, He will execute final judgment on the last day (Acts 17:31).[^25]

**6. The Eternal Purpose: "Unto whom He did from all eternity give a people to be His seed, and to be by Him in time redeemed, called, justified, sanctified, and glorified."**[^26] This is the *ordo salutis* in summary. John 17:6 records Christ's prayer: "I have manifested your name to the people whom you gave me out of the world."[^27] Romans 8:30 declares, "And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified."[^28] As John Gill explains, "Those whom the Father gave to Christ are actually redeemed, called, justified, sanctified, and glorified by Him."[^29]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Incarnation of the Mediator: The Hypostatic Union

Having established the eternal ordination of Christ, the confession now describes the incarnation—how the eternal Son took to Himself a true human nature. Paragraph 2 states: <ConfessionBox>\n**"The Son of God, the second person in the Holy Trinity, being very and eternal God, the brightness of the Father's glory, and of one substance and equal with him, who made the world, and upholdeth and governeth all things he hath made, did, when the fullness of time was come, take unto him man's nature, with all the essential properties and common infirmities thereof, yet without sin; being conceived by the Holy Spirit in the womb of the Virgin Mary, the Holy Spirit coming down upon her, and the power of the Most High overshadowing her, and so was made of a woman of the tribe of Judah, of the seed of Abraham and David, the fulness of the Godhead dwelling bodily in him."**[^31]\n</ConfessionBox>

Sam Waldron notes that this is "one of the longest and certainly one of the most important and central chapters of the Confession."[^32] The paragraph addresses four key aspects of Christ's person: His specific personality, His unqualified deity, His true humanity, and His sinless integrity.[^33]

**First, His Specific Personality.** "The Son of God, the second person in the Holy Trinity."[^34] The confession begins by identifying *who* became incarnate. Christ is not a created being who became God's Son; He is the eternal Son. The confession cites John 1:1-3 and John 1:14: "The Word became flesh."[^35] The Nicene doctrine of eternal generation is affirmed: the Son is "eternally begotten of the Father" (John 3:16; 1 John 4:9).[^36]

**Second, His Unqualified Deity.** "Very and eternal God, the brightness of the Father's glory, and of one substance and equal with him, who made the world, and upholdeth and governeth all things he hath made."[^37] This language echoes Hebrews 1:3 ("He is the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature") and Colossians 1:16-17 ("By him all things were created... and in him all things hold together").[^38] The confession distinguishes between person and essence: as to person, Christ is second; as to essence, He is not second but "very and eternal God."[^39] This guards against Arianism, which taught that the Son was a creature.[^40]

**Third, His True Humanity.** "Did, when the fullness of time was come, take unto him man's nature, with all the essential properties and common infirmities thereof."[^41] Christ assumed a full human nature—not merely a body but a soul, not merely an appearance but a complete humanity (Hebrews 2:14-18).[^42] He took "all the essential properties" of humanity: a rational soul (Matthew 26:38) and a physical body (Luke 24:39). He also took "the common infirmities" of humanity: weariness (John 4:6), hunger (Matthew 4:2), thirst (John 19:28), sorrow (John 11:35), and the capacity to suffer physical death.[^43]

**Fourth, His Sinless Integrity.** "Yet without sin."[^44] The confession is emphatic: Christ's humanity was exactly like ours in every respect except sin. This is the doctrine of the sinless integrity of the Mediator. Waldron lists twelve biblical texts that substantiate this: Isaiah 53:9 ("He had done no violence, nor was there any deceit in his mouth"); John 8:46 ("Which one of you convicts Me of sin?"); 2 Corinthians 5:21 ("He made Him who knew no sin to be sin on our behalf"); Hebrews 4:15 ("One who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin").[^45] Even in His human nature, Christ never committed a sin, never harbored a sinful desire, never spoke a sinful word.[^46]

**The Mode of the Incarnation.** "Being conceived by the Holy Spirit in the womb of the Virgin Mary, the Holy Spirit coming down upon her, and the power of the Most High overshadowing her."[^47] This is the virginal conception: Christ was born of a virgin (Matthew 1:18-23; Luke 1:26-35), without a human father, conceived by the Holy Spirit.[^48] The result is the hypostatic union: "the fulness of the Godhead dwelling bodily in him" (Colossians 2:9).[^49] The two natures—divine and human—are united in one person forever, without confusion, change, division, or separation (the Chalcedonian definition).[^50]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Qualifications for the Office: Anointing and Commissioning

Paragraph 3 states: <ConfessionBox>\n**"The Lord Jesus, in his human nature thus united to the divine, in the person of the Son, was sanctified and anointed with the Holy Spirit above measure, having in him all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge; in whom it pleased the Father that all fullness should dwell; to the end that being holy, harmless, undefiled, and full of grace and truth, he might be thoroughly furnished to execute the office of a mediator and surety. Which office he took not upon himself, but was thereunto called by his Father, who also put all power and judgment into his hand, and gave him commandment to execute the same."**[^52]\n</ConfessionBox>

This paragraph identifies two essential qualifications for the mediatorial office: the anointing of the Holy Spirit and the commissioning of God the Father.[^53]

**First, the Anointing of the Holy Spirit.** The confession echoes the language of Psalm 45:7: "You have loved righteousness and hated wickedness; therefore God, your God, has anointed you with the oil of gladness beyond your companions."[^54] In His human nature, Christ was "sanctified and anointed with the Holy Spirit above measure." The phrase "above measure" distinguishes Christ from all other anointed servants: the prophets, priests, and kings of the Old Testament received the Spirit in measure (Numbers 11:25; 2 Kings 2:9-10), but Christ received the Spirit without measure.[^55] John 3:34 declares, "For he whom God has sent utters the words of God, for he gives the Spirit without measure."[^56] In Him dwell "all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3), and in Him "the Father was pleased that all the fullness should dwell" (Colossians 1:19).[^57]

The purpose of this anointing is that Christ, "being holy, harmless, undefiled, and full of grace and truth, might be thoroughly furnished to execute the office of a mediator and surety."[^58] The term "surety" (Hebrews 7:22) describes Christ as the guarantor of the covenant—the one who pledges Himself for the fulfillment of its promises.[^59] As John Owen writes, "Christ is the surety of the covenant, undertaking for both parties: for God, to make satisfaction; for man, to make obedience."[^60]

**Second, the Commissioning of God the Father.** The confession emphasizes that Christ did not take this office upon Himself but was called by His Father. This is the consistent witness of Scripture: "No one takes this honor for himself, but only when called by God, just as Aaron was" (Hebrews 5:4). Christ was called by the Father (Hebrews 5:5), who "put all power and judgment into his hand" (John 5:22) and "gave him commandment to execute the same" (John 10:18).[^61]

The distinction between anointing and commissioning is important: the Holy Spirit fitted Christ for the work, and the Father gave Him the authority and command to perform it.[^62]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Execution of the Office: Humiliation and Obedience

Having established Christ's person and qualifications, the confession now describes His work. Paragraph 4 states: <ConfessionBox>\n**"This office the Lord Jesus did most willingly undertake; which that he might discharge, he was made under the law, and did perfectly fulfill it; and underwent the punishment due to us, which we should have borne and suffered, being made sin and a curse for us; enduring most grievous sorrows in his soul, and most painful sufferings in his body; was crucified, and died, and was buried, and remained under the power of death, yet saw no corruption."**[^64]\n</ConfessionBox>

**The Voluntary Undertaking.** The confession emphasizes that Christ "most willingly undertook" His mediatorial work.[^65] This echoes John 10:18: "No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord." Christ was not a reluctant victim but a willing Savior.[^66] As the writer of Hebrews declares, "When Christ came into the world, he said, 'Sacrifices and offerings you have not desired, but a body have you prepared for me... I have come to do your will, O God'" (Hebrews 10:5-7).[^67]

**Active Obedience: "Made under the law, and did perfectly fulfill it."** Christ was "made under the law" (Galatians 4:4).[^68] As the second Adam, He came under the covenant of works—the very covenant Adam broke—and "did perfectly fulfill it." This is Christ's *active obedience*: His lifelong submission to the law of God, His perfect keeping of every commandment from His birth to His death.[^69] The active obedience of Christ is the righteousness that is imputed to believers for their justification (Romans 5:19).[^70]

**Passive Obedience: "Underwent the punishment due to us, which we should have borne and suffered, being made sin and a curse for us."** The confession then describes Christ's *passive obedience*: He "underwent the punishment due to us."[^71] The language of "being made sin" echoes 2 Corinthians 5:21 ("He made Him who knew no sin to be sin on our behalf").[^72] The language of "a curse" echoes Galatians 3:13 ("Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us").[^73]

**The Dimensions of Suffering.** The confession specifies the dimensions of Christ's sufferings: "enduring most grievous sorrows in his soul, and most painful sufferings in his body."[^74] The "grievous sorrows in his soul" include the agony of Gethsemane ("My soul is very sorrowful, even to death," Matthew 26:38) and the cry of dereliction on the cross ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Matthew 27:46).[^75] The "painful sufferings in his body" include the scourging (Isaiah 53:5; Matthew 27:26), the crown of thorns (Matthew 27:29), the nails (John 20:25), and the slow agony of crucifixion.[^76]

**Death and Burial.** Finally, the confession states the reality of His death and burial: "was crucified, and died, and was buried, and remained under the power of death, yet saw no corruption."[^77] Christ truly died (Mark 15:37-39), was truly buried (Matthew 27:59-60), and remained in the tomb until the third day. Yet His body "saw no corruption" (Acts 2:31): He was not abandoned to decay because He was the Holy One of God (Psalm 16:10).[^78]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Central Operation: Resurrection, Ascension, Intercession, and Return

Paragraph 5 states: <ConfessionBox>\n**"On the third day he arose from the dead, with the same body in which he suffered; with which he also ascended into heaven, and there sitteth at the right hand of his Father, making intercession; and shall return to judge men and angels at the end of the world."**[^80]\n</ConfessionBox>

**First, the Resurrection.** "On the third day he arose from the dead, with the same body in which he suffered."[^81] Christ's resurrection was a bodily resurrection—the same body that was laid in the tomb was raised to new life. This is the assurance of our own resurrection (1 Corinthians 15:20-23).[^82] As Paul declares, "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17).[^83]

**Second, the Ascension and Session.** "With which he also ascended into heaven, and there sitteth at the right hand of his Father."[^84] Christ's ascension was the exaltation of His human nature into the presence of God (Acts 1:9-11). His session at the "right hand of the Father" signifies His enthronement as King and His authority over all creation (Hebrews 1:3; Ephesians 1:20-21).[^85]

**Third, the Intercession.** "Making intercession."[^86] Christ's present work in heaven is intercession—pleading His finished work on behalf of His people (Romans 8:34; Hebrews 7:25). Unlike the intercession of earthly priests, which was limited by the blood of animals, Christ's intercession is effectual because it rests on His finished work. As John Owen wrote, "His intercession is a continuation of His oblation."[^87]

**Fourth, the Future Return.** "And shall return to judge men and angels at the end of the world."[^88] The final act of Christ's mediatorial office is the judgment of all rational creatures—both men and angels (Acts 17:31; Matthew 25:31-46). This is the great and terrible day of the Lord.[^89]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Ancient Communication: Virtue Before Incarnation

Paragraph 6 addresses an important theological question: how were Old Testament saints saved before the incarnation? <ConfessionBox>\n**"Although the price of redemption was not actually paid by Christ till after his incarnation, yet the virtue, efficacy, and benefit thereof were communicated to the elect in all ages successively from the beginning of the world, in and by those promises, types, and sacrifices wherein he was revealed, and signified to be the seed of the woman which should bruise the serpent's head, and the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world, being the same yesterday, and to-day, and for ever."**[^91]\n</ConfessionBox>

**The Problem:** How could people be saved before Christ actually died? The confession answers: the "price of redemption" (the actual payment) was not made until the incarnation, but the "virtue, efficacy, and benefit" (the saving power) were applied to believers from the beginning of the world.[^92]

**The Means:** Old Testament believers were saved through "promises, types, and sacrifices" that pointed forward to Christ. The *protoevangelium* of Genesis 3:15 ("the seed of the woman who shall bruise the serpent's head") was the first gospel promise. The sacrificial system (Leviticus 1-7) typified the Lamb of God. The Passover lamb (Exodus 12) prefigured Christ, "the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29).[^93]

**The Basis:** The confession cites Revelation 13:8, where Christ is called "the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world." God's eternal decree and Christ's covenantal undertaking were effective for believers before the historical event of the cross.[^94]

**The Uniformity:** "Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever" (Hebrews 13:8).[^95] Abraham saw Christ's day and was glad (John 8:56). David sang of His suffering and glory (Psalm 22). The entire Old Testament looks forward to Him. As Paul declares, "the Scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the Gentiles by faith, preached the gospel beforehand to Abraham" (Galatians 3:8).[^96]

#### Paragraph 7 – The Mysterious Communions: The Communication of Properties

Paragraph 7 states: <ConfessionBox>\n**"Christ, in the work of mediation, acteth according to both natures, by each nature doing that which is proper to itself; yet, by reason of the unity of the person, that which is proper to one nature is sometimes in Scripture attributed to the person denominated by the other nature."**[^98]\n</ConfessionBox>

This is the doctrine of the *communicatio idiomatum*—the communication of properties.[^99] Because Christ is one person with two natures, actions that belong to one nature are attributed to the whole person. For example, Scripture says that "the Lord of glory was crucified" (1 Corinthians 2:8). Crucifixion is an action of the human nature, but it is attributed to the divine person.[^100] Conversely, Scripture says that the Son of God "descended from heaven" (John 3:13), though descent is an action of the divine nature.[^101]

This is not a confusion of the natures but a unity of the person. The confession is faithful to the definition of Chalcedon: "one and the same Christ, Son, Lord, Only-begotten, recognized in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation."[^102]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 7:** Hebrews 9:14; 1 Peter 3:18; Acts 20:28; John 3:13; Matthew 28:18-20.[^103]

#### Paragraph 8 – The Effectual Application: Union with Christ and the Graces of the Spirit

Paragraph 8 states: <ConfessionBox>\n**"To all those for whom Christ hath purchased redemption, he doth certainly and effectually apply and communicate the same; making intercession for them, and revealing unto them, in and by the Word, the mysteries of salvation; effectually persuading them by his Spirit to believe and obey, and governing their hearts by his Word and Spirit; overcoming all their enemies by his almighty power and wisdom, in such manner and ways as are most consonant to his wonderful and unsearchable dispensation."**[^104]\n</ConfessionBox>

This paragraph describes the application of redemption. It is not enough that Christ died for His people; He must also make His death effectual to them.[^105]

**First, Intercession.** Christ "makes intercession for them." Unlike the intercession of earthly priests, which was limited by the blood of animals, Christ's intercession is effectual because it rests on His finished work (Romans 8:34; Hebrews 7:25).[^106]

**Second, Revelation.** Christ "reveals unto them, in and by the Word, the mysteries of salvation." The Spirit uses the Word to unveil the riches of Christ to the hearts of believers (Ephesians 1:9; 1 Corinthians 2:10-12).[^107]

**Third, Persuasion.** Christ "effectually persuading them by his Spirit to believe and obey." This is the effectual call: the Spirit's work of overcoming the resistance of the fallen will, making dead sinners willing and able to believe (John 6:44-45; Acts 13:48).[^108]

**Fourth, Governance.** Christ "governing their hearts by his Word and Spirit; overcoming all their enemies." The grace of Christ not only saves but also preserves, governs, and protects His people until the end (John 10:28-29; Romans 8:30).[^109]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 8:** John 6:37; Romans 8:30-34; John 14:16; Hebrews 7:25; Ephesians 1:9; 1 Corinthians 2:10-12.[^110]

#### Paragraph 9 – The Inalienable Possession: Christ Alone Is Mediator

Paragraph 9 declares: <ConfessionBox>\n**"This office of mediator between God and man is proper only to Christ, who is the prophet, priest, and king of the church of God; and may not be either in whole, or any part thereof, transferred from him to any other."**[^111]\n</ConfessionBox>

The confession is emphatic: Christ alone is the Mediator. No other being—not the Virgin Mary, not the saints, not angels—may share in this office.[^112] There is no co-mediator, no co-redemptrix, no deputy. The threefold office of prophet, priest, and king "may not be either in whole, or any part thereof, transferred from him to any other."[^113]

This is not a denial of the priesthood of all believers (1 Peter 2:9); it is an affirmation of the uniqueness of Christ's mediatorial work. Believers are called priests, but they are not mediators. They can pray for one another, but they cannot atone. They can share the gospel, but they cannot reveal new truth.[^114]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 9:** 1 Timothy 2:5; Acts 4:12; Hebrews 7:24; Revelation 1:5-6.[^115]

#### Paragraph 10 – The Final Functions: The Necessity of the Three Offices

The final paragraph concludes: <ConfessionBox>\n**"The number and order of the offices are necessary; for ignorance would arise from the want of a prophet, alienation from the want of a priest, and bondage from the want of a king."**[^116]\n</ConfessionBox>

This paragraph succinctly states the necessity of Christ's threefold office.[^117] Without a prophet, we would have no revelation from God—we would be lost in ignorance (John 1:18). Without a priest, we would have no atonement for sin—we would remain alienated from God (Hebrews 5:1-4). Without a king, we would have no deliverance from our spiritual enemies—we would remain in bondage (Psalm 110:1-2).[^118]

Christ is all we need. His three offices correspond exactly to the threefold misery of fallen humanity: ignorance, alienation, and bondage. As Waldron writes, "The number and order of the offices is necessary, since they answer the threefold need of fallen men."[^119]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Confessional Christology

The confession's doctrine of Christ rests on several interconnected theological pillars, each essential to maintaining biblical orthodoxy.[^121]

**Pillar 1: The Covenant of Redemption.** The work of Christ is grounded in the eternal pact between the Father and the Son (paragraph 1). This ensures that the gospel is not an afterthought but the eternal purpose of the Triune God.[^122]

**Pillar 2: The Hypostatic Union.** Christ is one person in two natures, divine and human, without confusion, change, division, or separation (paragraph 2). This doctrine, defined at Chalcedon (AD 451), is the non-negotiable foundation of all Christology.[^123]

**Pillar 3: The Active and Passive Obedience.** The confession teaches that Christ's obedience has two dimensions: active (His perfect keeping of the law) and passive (His suffering of the penalty for sin). Both are necessary for the complete salvation of sinners (paragraph 4).[^124]

**Pillar 4: The Effectual Application of Redemption.** The confession rejects any notion that Christ's work merely makes salvation *possible*. Instead, Christ "certainly and effectually applies and communicates" redemption to those for whom He purchased it (paragraph 8). This is the Reformed doctrine of definite atonement.[^125]

**Pillar 5: The Uniqueness and Exclusivity of Christ.** The confession declares that Christ alone is the Mediator (paragraph 9). This is not religious exclusivism born of arrogance but fidelity to the clear teaching of Scripture: "There is one God, and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (1 Timothy 2:5).[^126]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Diminished or Distorted Christology

The confession's doctrine of Christ is not a speculative theology; it is a pastoral necessity. Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 8.[^127]

**Crisis 1: The Denial of Christ's Deity.** In liberal Protestantism and some forms of modern evangelicalism, the deity of Christ is increasingly questioned or denied. The confession's affirmation that Christ is "very and eternal God, the brightness of the Father's glory, and of one substance and equal with him" (paragraph 2) is a direct counter to Arianism, Socinianism, and all forms of unitarianism.[^128] If Christ is not fully God, He cannot be a perfect mediator, for He could not represent the divine side of the covenant (Hebrews 1:3).[^129]

**Crisis 2: The Denial of Christ's True Humanity.** The ancient heresy of Docetism—which taught that Christ only appeared to be human—is alive in the modern tendency to spiritualize Jesus or to deny His genuine human experiences. The confession's affirmation that Christ took "man's nature, with all the essential properties and common infirmities thereof" (paragraph 2) guards against this error. Christ truly wept (John 11:35), truly hungered (Matthew 4:2), truly suffered (Luke 22:44), and truly died (Mark 15:37). His humanity is our assurance that He can sympathize with our weaknesses (Hebrews 4:15).[^130]

**Crisis 3: The Denial of Christ's Sinlessness.** Some modern theologians have attempted to argue that Jesus, like all humans, must have been a sinner. The confession's affirmation that Christ was "without sin" (paragraph 2) is non-negotiable. If Christ sinned, He could not be a perfect sacrifice; He would need a savior Himself (Hebrews 7:26-27).[^131]

**Crisis 4: The Subtraction of the Atonement.** Many modern presentations of the gospel reduce the work of Christ to moral example or cosmic victory, omitting the substitutionary atonement. The confession's focus on Christ "being made sin and a curse for us" (paragraph 4) is a clear affirmation of penal substitution—the doctrine that Christ bore the penalty for sin in our place (2 Corinthians 5:21; Galatians 3:13).[^132]

**Crisis 5: The Addition of Other Mediators.** In Roman Catholicism and some forms of Eastern Christianity, Mary and the saints are invoked as co-mediators. The confession's declaration that Christ's mediatorial office "may not be either in whole, or any part thereof, transferred from him to any other" (paragraph 9) is a direct rejection of this error.[^133] Christ alone is the Mediator (1 Timothy 2:5).[^134]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Christ the Mediator

The confession's doctrine of Christ is not a set of propositions to be memorized but a reality to be lived. Here are seven practical steps derived from Chapter 8.[^135]

**Step 1: Rest in the Finished Work of Christ.** Because Christ "did perfectly fulfill" the law and "underwent the punishment due to us" (paragraph 4), you have nothing to add to His work. Your salvation is complete in Him. Stop trying to earn what has already been purchased. Rest in the active and passive obedience of your Savior (Romans 5:19; 2 Corinthians 5:21).[^136]

**Step 2: Come to Christ as Prophet, Priest, and King.** Do not approach Christ in one office only. Come to Him as Prophet to teach you, as Priest to forgive you, and as King to rule over you (paragraph 10). A half-hearted Christology yields a half-hearted Christianity. As John Calvin wrote, "Christ is not fully known unless we recognize Him as the one who brings us the whole of salvation."[^137]

**Step 3: Trust in the Effectual Application of Redemption.** If you are in Christ, your salvation is not fragile; it is certain. Christ "certainly and effectually applies" redemption to those for whom He purchased it (paragraph 8). He intercedes for you, reveals the mysteries of salvation to you, persuades you to believe, and governs your heart. Your security is not in your grip on Him but in His grip on you (John 10:28-29).[^138]

**Step 4: Reject All False Mediators.** Do not look to saints, angels, or Mary for access to God. Christ alone is the Mediator (paragraph 9). Pray to the Father through the Son in the Spirit. Your access to the throne of grace is not through any human intermediary but through the blood of Jesus (Hebrews 4:14-16).[^139]

**Step 5: Preach the Full Christology.** Preachers and teachers must not reduce Jesus to a moral example or a therapist. Preach the full biblical Christ: the eternal Son who became flesh, the sinless Savior who died for sinners, the risen Lord who intercedes at the right hand of the Father, and the coming King who will judge the world (paragraphs 2, 4, 5). Anything less is not the gospel (1 Corinthians 2:2).[^140]

**Step 6: Cultivate Love for the Person of Christ.** The confession's purpose is not merely doctrinal but doxological. Waldron notes: "He is this person that the Father wants us to love with the same tenderness, affection, and exaltation which He does." Meditate on the glories of Christ (Philippians 3:8). Sing hymns that exalt His person and work. Let your heart be captivated by the One who is "the brightness of the Father's glory" (Hebrews 1:3).[^141]

**Step 7: Live in Hope of His Return.** Christ "shall return to judge men and angels at the end of the world" (paragraph 5). This is not a threat but a promise for those who love Him. The same Jesus who died for you will return for you (John 14:3). Until that day, live in joyful anticipation, serving Him as your King, trusting Him as your Priest, and learning from Him as your Prophet (1 Thessalonians 4:16-18).[^142]

### Conclusion: The Center of the Confession

Chapter 8 is the heart of the 1689 Baptist Confession. Without a right understanding of Christ's person and work, every other doctrine—sin, covenant, justification, sanctification, the church, the last things—lacks its foundation.[^143] But because the eternal Son of God became man, lived a sinless life, died a substitutionary death, rose victorious over the grave, and now intercedes at the right hand of the Father, the gospel is secure.[^144]

The same Christ who was ordained from eternity as the Mediator of the covenant of grace (paragraph 1) is the One who effectually applies redemption to His people (paragraph 8). He is the prophet who reveals, the priest who sacrifices, the king who rules. He is the heir of all things and the judge of all the world. He is the same yesterday, today, and forever (paragraph 6).[^145]

May we, with the apostle Paul, count everything as loss for the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus our Lord (Philippians 3:8). May we preach nothing but Christ and Him crucified (1 Corinthians 2:2). And may we rest in the finished work of the only Mediator between God and man—the Lord Jesus Christ (1 Timothy 2:5).[^146]

**A Prayer from Chapter 8** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O eternal Son of God, second person of the Holy Trinity, brightness of the Father's glory, and very and eternal God: We praise Thee that Thou didst not cling to Thy glory but didst humble Thyself, taking upon Thee the form of a servant, being made in the likeness of men (Philippians 2:6-7). We thank Thee that Thou wast conceived by the Holy Spirit, born of the Virgin Mary, and made under the law, that Thou mightest redeem those who were under the law (Galatians 4:4-5).*

*We adore Thee for Thy sinless life, by which Thou hast fulfilled all righteousness (Matthew 3:15), and for Thy substitutionary death, by which Thou hast borne the curse we deserved (Galatians 3:13). We rejoice in Thy resurrection, ascension, and session at the right hand of the Father (Romans 8:34). We rest in Thy intercession, knowing that Thou livest always to make intercession for us (Hebrews 7:25).*

*Grant us to love Thee with the same tenderness and affection with which the Father loves Thee. Teach us to come to Thee as Prophet, Priest, and King. And until Thou returnest to judge the world, keep us steadfast in faith, hope, and love. For Thou alone art the Mediator between God and man (1 Timothy 2:5), the Head and Savior of Thy church (Ephesians 1:22-23), the Heir of all things (Hebrews 1:2), and the Judge of the living and the dead (Acts 10:42). Amen.*[^147]
