# The Grace That Makes Us Children: Why Adoption Is the Cinderella Doctrine of the Christian Faith

Chapter 12 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of Adoption," stands as the most concise yet one of the most tender chapters in the entire confession.[^1] If Chapter 11 declares the believer's legal standing before God's court, Chapter 12 announces that the justified sinner is not merely pardoned but *welcomed*—brought into the very family of God as a beloved child.[^2]

The chapter is unique in the confession, consisting of only a single, beautifully crafted paragraph.[^3] As one pastor notes, "It's the only chapter in our confession that has just one paragraph."[^4] Yet within this brevity lies a depth of pastoral comfort that has led some to call adoption the "Cinderella doctrine of the Christian faith"—a truth that has often been overlooked but is of surpassing beauty and importance.[^5]

The confession follows the biblical order: justification (Chapter 11) is the legal foundation; adoption (Chapter 12) is the relational consequence.[^6] As Sam Waldron notes, "All those that are justified, God vouchsafed, in and for the sake of his only Son Jesus Christ, to make partakers of the grace of adoption."[^7]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of Adoption

The chapter is structured as one extended sentence in the original English,[^8] and Waldron outlines it as follows: "I. The foundation of adoption A. Its recipients: 'all those that are justified' B. Its source: 'God vouchsafed' C. Its ground: 'in and for the sake of His only Son Jesus Christ.' II. The blessings of adoption A. Incorporation into God's family B. Reception of a filial disposition C. Experience of paternal treatment by God D. Reception of the promised inheritance."[^9]

#### The Text of Chapter 12

<ConfessionBox>
"All those that are justified, God conferred, in and for the sake of his only Son Jesus Christ, to make partakers of the grace of adoption,[^1] by which they are taken into the number, and enjoy the liberties and privileges of the children of God,[^2] have his name put on them,[^3] receive the spirit of adoption,[^4] have access to the throne of grace with boldness, are enabled to cry Abba, Father,[^5] are pitied,[^6] protected,[^7] provided for,[^8] and chastened by him as by a Father,[^9] yet never cast off,[^10] but sealed to the day of redemption,[^11] and inherit the promises as heirs of everlasting salvation."
</ConfessionBox>[^12][^10]

**1. The Recipients of Adoption: "All those that are justified."** The confession grounds adoption in justification. One cannot be a child of God apart from being first declared righteous in Christ. As Pastor Robert Martin explains, "The justified person is reckoned as legally right with God. And in experiential terms, being right with God means that the justified believer may enjoy rest of conscience, that his sins are forgiven, and that he will not be condemned for them at the great day of God's judgment."[^11] From this legal standing flows the intimate relationship of sonship.[^12]

**2. The Divine Source: "God conferred."** The older English term "vouchsafed" means "to grant or furnish in a gracious or condescending manner."[^13] Adoption is not earned; it is graciously bestowed. The Baptist Catechism defines adoption as "an act of God's free grace whereby we are received into the number and have a right to all the privileges of the sons of God."[^14]

**3. The Sole Ground: "In and for the sake of his only Son Jesus Christ."** The confession is emphatic: adoption is not based on anything in the sinner but solely on Christ. God the Father adopts us for the sake of His only begotten Son, the natural Son, so that we might become adopted sons.[^15] Ephesians 1:5 declares that God "predestined us for adoption to himself as sons through Jesus Christ."[^16]

**4. The Linguistic Background: υἱοθεσία (huiothesia).** The New Testament term for adoption literally means "the placing of a son."[^17] As Martin notes, "It refers to the bringing of one who is not a natural child into the legal status of a son or daughter."[^18] In Roman culture, adoption was a legal act that permanently transferred a person from one family to another with all accompanying rights and privileges.[^19] Paul uses this powerful cultural image to convey the believer's new status in Christ.

**5. The Trinitarian Shape of Adoption.** The grace of adoption is Trinitarian in nature.[^20] "God the Father predestines, He elects, He has already determined who He will adopt."[^21] The Son secures adoption through His redemptive work: "When the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of woman, born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law, so that we might receive adoption as sons" (Galatians 4:4-5).[^22] The Spirit applies adoption: "You have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba! Father!'" (Romans 8:15).[^23]

#### The Privileges of Adoption: A Twelvefold Blessing

The confession enumerates the specific blessings that flow from adoption. These form a rich tapestry of the believer's new relationship with God.[^24]

**1. Taken into the Number.** "By which they are taken into the number."[^25] The adopted child is no longer an outsider, an alien, a stranger. John 1:12 declares, "But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God."[^26]

**2. Liberties and Privileges of Children of God.** The confession cites Romans 8:17: "and if children, then heirs—heirs of God and fellow heirs with Christ."[^27] The adopted child possesses all the rights of a natural-born child.

**3. His Name Put on Them.** 2 Corinthians 6:18 proclaims, "I will be a father to you, and you shall be sons and daughters to me, says the Lord Almighty."[^28] Revelation 3:12 promises that Christ will write on the overcomer "the name of my God."[^29]

**4. The Spirit of Adoption.** Romans 8:15 declares, "For you did not receive the spirit of slavery to fall back into fear, but you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba! Father!'"[^30] The Spirit does not merely assure us of our adoption; He creates within us the filial disposition that cries out to God as Father.[^31]

**5. Access to the Throne of Grace with Boldness.** Ephesians 2:18 affirms, "For through him we both have access in one Spirit to the Father."[^32] Hebrews 4:16 exhorts believers to "draw near with confidence to the throne of grace."[^33]

**6. Enabled to Cry, Abba, Father.** Galatians 4:6 states, "And because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, 'Abba! Father!'"[^34] The Aramaic term "Abba" is an intimate, familial address—the cry of a young child to his father.[^35]

**7. Pitied by God.** Psalm 103:13 declares, "As a father shows compassion to his children, so the LORD shows compassion to those who fear him."[^36]

**8. Protected by God.** Proverbs 14:26 affirms, "In the fear of the LORD one has strong confidence, and his children will have a refuge."[^37] 1 Peter 5:7 invites believers to cast "all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you."[^38]

**9. Provided for by God.** Hebrews 12:6-7 teaches that God's provision includes not only material care but also loving discipline: "the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and chastises every son whom he receives."[^39]

**10. Chastened as by a Father.** The confession specifically mentions chastening as a privilege of adoption. Hebrews 12:6-7 declares that the absence of discipline is evidence of illegitimacy, not favor.[^40]

**11. Never Cast Off.** Isaiah 54:8-9 and Lamentations 3:31 assure believers that God's covenant love is irrevocable.[^41]

**12. Sealed to the Day of Redemption.** Ephesians 4:30 warns, "Do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, by whom you were sealed for the day of redemption."[^42]

**13. Inherit the Promises as Heirs.** Hebrews 1:14 and 6:12 speak of the promised inheritance that awaits all the children of God.[^43]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of Adoption

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of adoption.

**Pillar 1: Adoption Is an Act of Free Grace.** The Baptist Catechism defines adoption as "an act of God's free grace."[^45] It is not earned by works or merited by character. God adopts sinners freely, for Christ's sake alone.

**Pillar 2: Adoption Is Distinct from Justification.** Justification is a forensic declaration of righteousness; adoption is the bestowal of a new relationship. Yet the two are inseparable: all who are justified are adopted, and all who are adopted are justified.[^46]

**Pillar 3: Adoption Is Trinitarian.** The Father predestines us for adoption (Ephesians 1:5), the Son secures adoption through His redemptive work (Galatians 4:4-5), and the Spirit applies adoption by giving us the Spirit of adoption (Romans 8:15).[^47]

**Pillar 4: The Privileges of Adoption Are Comprehensive.** The confession lists twelve distinct blessings that flow from adoption, ranging from intimate access to the Father to the assurance of eternal inheritance.[^48]

**Pillar 5: Adoption Is Eternal and Irrevocable.** The adopted child is "never cast off" and is "sealed to the day of redemption."[^49] Once God adopts, He never abandons.

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Neglected Doctrine

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 12.

**Crisis 1: The Orphan Heart.** J. I. Packer observed that many believers live as spiritual orphans because they have not grasped the doctrine of adoption.[^50] The confession's teaching that believers are "taken into the number" of God's children is the remedy.[^51]

**Crisis 2: The Fear of God's Wrath.** Many believers struggle with the fear that God is angry with them. The doctrine of adoption assures them that God is not a harsh judge but a loving Father.[^52]

**Crisis 3: The Loss of Intimacy in Prayer.** If God is a judge, prayer is formal and distant. If God is a Father, prayer is intimate and bold. The confession's affirmation that believers have "access to the throne of grace with boldness" is a direct challenge to prayerless Christianity.[^53]

**Crisis 4: The Misunderstanding of Discipline.** Many believers interpret God's chastening as rejection or abandonment. The confession teaches that discipline is a mark of sonship, not illegitimacy.[^54]

**Crisis 5: The Absence of Assurance.** Adoption is the ground of assurance. If God has adopted me as His child, He will never cast me off. The confession's declaration that believers are "sealed to the day of redemption" is the anchor of the soul.[^55]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of Adoption

**Step 1: Preach the Gospel to Yourself Daily.** Remind yourself each morning that you are not merely a forgiven criminal but a beloved child. The same God who justified you has adopted you.[^56]

**Step 2: Pray with Boldness as a Child to Your Father.** Do not approach God as a trembling slave approaching a harsh master. Approach Him as a child running to a loving Father, crying, "Abba, Father."[^57]

**Step 3: Embrace Discipline as a Mark of Sonship.** When trials come, do not interpret them as rejection. Interpret them as the loving discipline of a Father who is training His children for glory.[^58]

**Step 4: Live as an Heir of the Promise.** Your inheritance is not uncertain; it is secured by Christ. Live with hope, knowing that all the promises of God are yes in Christ.[^59]

**Step 5: Extend the Love of Adoption to Others.** As you have been received into God's family, receive others into the family of the church. Adoption is not only a doctrine to be believed but a grace to be embodied.[^60]

### Conclusion: The Cinderella Doctrine

Chapter 12 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is the "Cinderella doctrine" of the Christian faith—often overlooked but of surpassing beauty.[^61] The believer is not merely pardoned; he is welcomed. He is not merely acquitted; he is adopted. The same God who sits as Judge upon the throne is the Father who runs to embrace His prodigal children. And because He has adopted us in Christ, we shall never be cast off, but sealed to the day of redemption, inheriting the promises as heirs of everlasting salvation.[^62]

**A Prayer from Chapter 12** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O Father of mercies and God of all comfort: We thank Thee that Thou hast not only forgiven our sins but received us into Thy family. We were orphans, but Thou hast adopted us. We were slaves, but Thou hast made us sons. We were strangers, but Thou hast given us Thy name. Grant us to live as Thy children—to cry, "Abba, Father!" with boldness, to embrace Thy discipline as a mark of love, and to rest in the assurance that we are sealed to the day of redemption. For Christ's sake. Amen.*[^63]
