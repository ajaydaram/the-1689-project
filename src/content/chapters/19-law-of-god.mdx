# The Law That Was Never Cancelled: Why the Moral Law Remains the Christian's Perfect Rule of Righteousness

Chapter 19 of the 1689 Baptist Confession of Faith, entitled "Of the Law of God," addresses one of the most controversial and misunderstood topics in the Christian faith.[^1] If Chapter 11 declares the believer's justification apart from the law, and Chapter 16 describes the good works that flow from saving faith, then Chapter 19 explains the *continuing role of the law* in the life of the believer and in the broader purposes of God.[^2] As Sam Waldron notes, "There are few subjects about which so much confusion reigns in our day as the Law of God. The wise and biblical treatment of this subject, given in 1689, is desperately needed in our day."[^3]

The chapter comprises seven paragraphs, which can be grouped under four main headings: (1) the original dispensation of the law to Adam (paragraph 1); (2) the Mosaic codification of the law (paragraphs 2-4); (3) the inherent obligation of the moral law (paragraph 5); and (4) the special functions of the law for Christians (paragraphs 6-7).[^4]

The chapter is a direct response to two errors: antinomianism (the view that the law has no place in the Christian life) and legalism (the view that obedience to the law contributes to justification).[^5] Between these extremes, the confession charts a biblical course, affirming that the moral law is forever binding, yet distinguishing carefully between the law as a covenant of works (which condemns) and the law as a rule of life (which guides).[^6]

### Part 1: Exegetical Foundations – The Biblical Shape of the Law of God

Chapter 19 is a masterful digest of Scripture's teaching on the nature, purpose, and continuing relevance of God's law.[^7]

#### Paragraph 1 – The Original Dispensation of the Law to Adam

The confession opens by tracing the law to its original source: **"God gave to Adam a law of universal obedience written in his heart, and a particular precept of not eating the fruit of the tree of knowledge of good and evil; by which he bound him and all his posterity to personal, entire, exact, and perpetual obedience; promised life upon the fulfilling, and threatened death upon the breach of it, and endued him with power and ability to keep it."**[^8]

**1. The Two Components of the Original Law.** The confession identifies two elements in the original law given to Adam.[^9] First, there was the "law of universal obedience written in his heart"—the moral law inscribed in his very nature as a creature made in God's image.[^10] Romans 2:14-15 speaks of this law: "For when Gentiles who do not have the Law do instinctively the things of the Law... they show the work of the Law written in their hearts."[^11] As Sam Waldron explains, "This is the concept of the natural law. In chapter 19 this becomes an important and foundational concept... The law written in the heart and therefore passed down to all Adam's posterity confirms that there is a moral law which forever binds all men in all ages."[^12]

Second, there was a "particular precept of not eating the fruit of the tree of knowledge of good and evil."[^13] This was a positive law—a command added to the natural law, which had no inherent moral content but served as a test of obedience.[^14]

**2. The Extent of Obligation.** "By which he bound him and all his posterity to personal, entire, exact, and perpetual obedience."[^15] The confession employs four adverbs to describe the standard of obedience required by the law: *personal* (each individual is responsible), *entire* (the whole law must be kept), *exact* (perfect conformity is demanded), and *perpetual* (obedience must be constant, never ceasing).[^16] Galatians 3:10 declares, "Cursed is everyone who does not continue to do everything written in the Book of the Law."[^17] Romans 10:5 states, "The law of righteousness requires that the person who obeys it must live by it."[^18]

**3. The Promises and Penalties.** "Promised life upon the fulfilling, and threatened death upon the breach of it."[^19] This was the structure of the covenant of works. The confession cites Genesis 2:17: "In the day that you eat of it you shall surely die."[^20] Leviticus 18:5 declares, "You shall therefore keep my statutes and my rules; if a person does them, he shall live by them."[^21]

**4. The Original Ability.** "And endued him with power and ability to keep it."[^22] Adam was not created in a state of moral neutrality. He possessed original righteousness—the ability to obey the law perfectly. As the confession notes in Chapter 4, Adam had "the law of God written in their hearts, and power to fulfill it."[^23]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 1:** Genesis 1:27; Ecclesiastes 7:29; Romans 10:5; Galatians 3:10,12; Genesis 2:17.[^24]

#### Paragraph 2 – The Mosaic Codification: The Moral Law Written on Stone

Paragraph 2 affirms the continuity of the moral law after the fall and its delivery at Sinai: **"The same law that was first written in the heart of man continued to be a perfect rule of righteousness after the fall, and was delivered by God upon Mount Sinai, in ten commandments, and written in two tables, the four first containing our duty towards God, and the other six, our duty to man."**[^25]

**1. The Substantial Identity of the Two Laws.** The confession teaches the "substantial equivalence" of the law written on Adam's heart and the Ten Commandments.[^26] Sam Waldron explains that the key biblical passage supporting this is Romans 2:12-15. Paul there asserts that Gentiles who do not have the written law "show the work of the Law written in their hearts."[^27] If raw pagans are in possession of the law of God, it can only be because "that law was written in the heart of Adam at creation and has been passed down to them by their procreation from him, and has not even been effaced by the Fall."[^28]

**2. The Continued Perfection After the Fall.** "Continued to be a perfect rule of righteousness after the fall."[^29] The fall did not change the law. As A. A. Hodge notes, "This law after the fall, while it ceased to offer salvation on the grounds of obedience, nevertheless continued to be the revealed expression of God's will, binding all human consciences as the rule of life."[^30] The confession provides biblical evidence from the period between the fall and Sinai, citing the observance of the Sabbath before Sinai (Genesis 2:3; Exodus 16), the condemnation of murder after Cain's act (Genesis 4), the sanctity of marriage (Genesis 12:17), the prohibition of theft (Genesis 31:30; 44:8), and the prohibition of false witness (Genesis 27:12).[^31]

**3. The Comprehensive Summary.** The confession does not teach that the Ten Commandments are an exhaustive treatise on the moral law, but rather "a comprehensive summary."[^32] The Westminster Larger Catechism (Q. 98) affirms that the moral law is "summarily comprehended" in the Ten Commandments.[^33]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 2:** Romans 2:14-15; Deuteronomy 10:4.[^34]

#### Paragraph 3 – The Ceremonial Law: Typological and Abrogated

Paragraph 3 describes the ceremonial law and its abrogation: **"Besides this law, commonly called moral, God was pleased to give to the people of Israel ceremonial laws, containing several typical ordinances, partly of worship, prefiguring Christ, his graces, actions, sufferings, and benefits; and partly holding forth divers instructions of moral duties, all which ceremonial laws being appointed only to the time of reformation, are, by Jesus Christ the true Messiah and only law-giver, who was furnished with power from the Father for that end abrogated and taken away."**[^35]

**1. The Nature of the Ceremonial Law.** The ceremonial laws were "typical ordinances"—types and shadows pointing forward to Christ.[^36] Hebrews 10:1 declares, "For since the law has but a shadow of the good things to come instead of the true form of these realities, it can never, by the same sacrifices that are continually offered every year, make perfect those who draw near."[^37] Colossians 2:17 states, "These are a shadow of the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ."[^38]

**2. The Twofold Purpose.** The ceremonial laws served two purposes: (a) prefiguring Christ—His graces, actions, sufferings, and benefits; and (b) holding forth "instructions of moral duties."[^39] 1 Corinthians 5:7 declares, "Cleanse out the old leaven that you may be a new lump, as you really are unleavened. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed."[^40]

**3. The Abrogation.** All ceremonial laws were "appointed only to the time of reformation" (the coming of Christ) and are now "abrogated and taken away."[^41] Colossians 2:14,16-17 declares that Christ "canceled the record of debt that stood against us with its legal demands. This he set aside, nailing it to the cross... Therefore let no one pass judgment on you in questions of food and drink, or with regard to a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath."[^42] Ephesians 2:14-16 speaks of Christ abolishing the law of commandments expressed in ordinances.[^43]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 3:** Hebrews 10:1; Colossians 2:17; 1 Corinthians 5:7; Colossians 2:14,16-17; Ephesians 2:14,16.[^44]

#### Paragraph 4 – The Judicial Law: Expired but Morally Instructive

Paragraph 4 addresses the judicial laws of Israel: **"To them also he gave sundry judicial laws, which expired together with the state of that people, not obliging any now by virtue of that institution; their general equity only being of moral use."**[^45]

**1. The Expiration of the Judicial Law.** "Expired together with the state of that people."[^46] The civil laws given to Israel were tied to the theocratic nation. When Israel ceased to exist as a theocratic state, those laws ceased to be binding as a legal code.[^47]

**2. The General Equity.** "Their general equity only being of moral use."[^48] The principle of "general equity" means that although the specific legal code is no longer binding, the moral principles embodied in those laws—principles of justice, fairness, and equity—remain instructive.[^49] 1 Corinthians 9:8-10 cites the law of Moses (not muzzling the ox while it treads out the grain) as having moral significance for Christian practice.[^50] As Sam Waldron explains, "The confession makes two balancing points regarding the judicial law: it speaks of its ancient expiration and its moral application."[^51]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 4:** 1 Corinthians 9:8-10.[^52]

#### Paragraph 5 – The Inherent Obligation of the Moral Law

Paragraph 5 affirms the universal and permanent obligation of the moral law: **"The moral law does for ever bind all, as well justified persons as others, to the obedience thereof, and that not only in regard of the matter contained in it, but also in respect of the authority of God the Creator, who gave it; neither does Christ in the Gospel any way dissolve, but much strengthen this obligation."**[^53]

**1. The Universal Obligation.** "The moral law does for ever bind all, as well justified persons as others."[^54] Romans 13:8-10 declares, "Owe no one anything, except to love each other, for the one who loves another has fulfilled the law."[^55] James 2:8,10-12 speaks of "the royal law" and warns that whoever keeps the whole law but fails in one point is guilty of all.[^56] The confession explicitly includes justified persons among those bound by the moral law.

**2. The Basis of Obligation.** The moral law binds "not only in regard of the matter contained in it, but also in respect of the authority of God the Creator, who gave it."[^57] The law is not arbitrary. Its commands are grounded in God's own holy character and in His authority as Creator. James 2:10-11 declares, "For he who said, 'Do not commit adultery,' also said, 'Do not murder.'"[^58]

**3. Christ Strengthens, Not Dissolves.** "Neither does Christ in the Gospel any way dissolve, but much strengthen this obligation."[^59] Matthew 5:17-19 records Christ's words: "Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them."[^60] Romans 3:31 asks, "Do we then overthrow the law by this faith? By no means! On the contrary, we uphold the law."[^61]

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 5:** Romans 13:8-10; James 2:8,10-12; Matthew 5:17-19; Romans 3:31.[^62]

#### Paragraph 6 – The Uses of the Law for Believers (The First Threefold Use)

Paragraph 6 explains the positive uses of the law for Christians, distinguishing the law as a covenant of works from the law as a rule of life: **"Although true believers are not under the law as a covenant of works, to be thereby justified or condemned, yet it is of great use to them as well as to others, in that as a rule of life, informing them of the will of God and their duty, it directs and binds them to walk accordingly; discovering also the sinful pollutions of their natures, hearts, and lives, so as examining themselves thereby, they may come to further conviction of, humiliation for, and hatred against, sin; together with a clearer sight of the need they have of Christ and the perfection of his obedience; it is likewise of use to the regenerate to restrain their corruptions, in that it forbids sin; and the threatenings of it serve to show what even their sins deserve, and what afflictions in this life they may expect for them, although freed from the curse and unallayed rigour thereof. The promises of it likewise show them God's approbation of obedience, and what blessings they may expect upon the performance thereof, though not as due to them by the law as a covenant of works; so as man's doing good and refraining from evil, because the law encourages to the one and deters from the other, is no evidence of his being under the law and not under grace."**[^63]

**1. The Negative Ground.** "True believers are not under the law as a covenant of works, to be thereby justified or condemned."[^64] Romans 6:14 declares, "You are not under law but under grace."[^65] Romans 8:1 affirms, "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."[^66] This is the crucial distinction: the law cannot justify or condemn the believer because Christ has fulfilled it on their behalf.

**2. The Threefold Use of the Law.** The Reformed tradition has identified three uses of the law: the *civil use* (restraining sin in society), the *pedagogical use* (driving sinners to Christ), and the *didactic or normative use* (the rule of life for believers).[^67] Paragraph 6 focuses on the second and third uses, with special attention to believers.

**First Use (for Believers): Rule of Life.** "As a rule of life, informing them of the will of God and their duty, it directs and binds them to walk accordingly."[^68] This is the normative or third use of the law. The law informs believers what pleases God. As Robert Martin explains, "The gospel is dependent upon the law of God. The atonement is predicated on the moral law. It is the law that Adam broke. It is the law that we break as sinners and even as believers."[^69]

**Second Use (for Believers): Mirror to Discover Sin.** "Discovering also the sinful pollutions of their natures, hearts, and lives, so as examining themselves thereby, they may come to further conviction of, humiliation for, and hatred against, sin."[^70] This is the pedagogical or second use, applied to believers. The law continues to reveal sin even in the regenerate, leading to humility and deeper dependence on Christ. Romans 7:7 declares, "If it had not been for the law, I would not have known sin."[^71]

**Third Use (for Believers): Incentive to Holiness.** "It is likewise of use to the regenerate to restrain their corruptions, in that it forbids sin; and the threatenings of it serve to show what even their sins deserve, and what afflictions in this life they may expect for them, although freed from the curse... The promises of it likewise show them God's approbation of obedience, and what blessings they may expect upon the performance thereof."[^72] The law, though no longer a covenant of works, remains a means of sanctification, encouraging obedience and discouraging sin.

**3. The Compatibility of Law and Gospel.** The confession concludes that doing good because the law encourages it "is no evidence of his being under the law and not under grace."[^73] The objection that obedience to the law implies a legalistic relationship is false. Believers obey the law out of gratitude, not to earn salvation.

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 6:** Romans 6:14; Romans 8:1; Romans 7:7; Psalm 19:7-11.[^74]

#### Paragraph 7 – The Harmony of Law and Gospel

The confession concludes with a statement on the harmony between law and gospel: **"Neither are the forementioned uses of the law contrary to the grace of the gospel, but do sweetly comply with it; the Spirit of Christ subduing and enabling the will of man to do that freely and cheerfully which the will of God revealed in the law requires to be done."**[^75]

**1. Sweet Compliance, Not Contradiction.** "Neither are the forementioned uses of the law contrary to the grace of the gospel, but do sweetly comply with it."[^76] The law and the gospel are not enemies. They work together in the economy of redemption. Robert Martin explains: "What the law prescribes, the gospel gives the strength to accomplish."[^77]

**2. The Spirit Enables Obedience.** "The Spirit of Christ subduing and enabling the will of man to do that freely and cheerfully which the will of God revealed in the law requires to be done."[^78] Ezekiel 36:27 promises, "I will put my Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes and be careful to obey my rules."[^79] Philippians 2:13 declares, "It is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure."[^80] The same grace that saves also enables obedience—not reluctantly, but "freely and cheerfully."

**Key Scripture Proofs for Paragraph 7:** Galatians 3:21; Ezekiel 36:27; Philippians 2:13.[^81]

### Part 2: Theological Anatomy – The Pillars of a Robust Doctrine of the Law

Several theological pillars support the confession's doctrine of the law.[^82]

**Pillar 1: The Natural Law Written on the Heart.** The confession affirms that the moral law was written on Adam's heart at creation and transmitted to all his posterity. This is the foundation for the universality of moral obligation.[^83]

**Pillar 2: The Substantial Identity of the Moral Law and the Ten Commandments.** The confession teaches that the law written on Adam's heart and the Ten Commandments delivered at Sinai are substantially the same. This guards against any suggestion that the moral law was introduced only at Sinai.[^84]

**Pillar 3: The Threefold Division of the Mosaic Law.** The confession distinguishes between the moral law (permanently binding), the ceremonial law (abrogated), and the judicial law (expired but morally instructive). This distinction is essential for understanding which Old Testament laws apply to Christians.[^85]

**Pillar 4: The Universal and Permanent Obligation of the Moral Law.** The moral law binds all people in all ages, including justified believers. Christ did not dissolve the law but strengthened it.[^86]

**Pillar 5: Believers Are Not Under the Law as a Covenant of Works.** The confession carefully distinguishes between the law as a covenant of works (which condemns) and the law as a rule of life (which guides). Believers are freed from the curse and condemnation of the law, not from its instruction.[^87]

**Pillar 6: The Threefold Use of the Law for Believers.** The law serves as a rule of life (informing believers of God's will), a mirror (revealing remaining sin), and an incentive (encouraging obedience and discouraging sin).[^88]

**Pillar 7: The Harmony of Law and Gospel.** The law and the gospel are not opposed. The Spirit enables believers to do freely and cheerfully what the law requires.[^89]

### Part 3: Pastoral Diagnosis – The Crises of a Distorted View of the Law

Several contemporary crises are directly addressed by Chapter 19.[^90]

**Crisis 1: Antinomianism.** Some Christians believe that because they are saved by grace, the law has no place in their lives. The confession's affirmation that the moral law "doth for ever bind all" and that "Christ... much strengthen this obligation" is a direct refutation of antinomianism.[^91]

**Crisis 2: Legalism.** Others believe that obedience to the law contributes to justification. The confession's careful distinction between the law as a covenant of works (which condemns) and the law as a rule of life (which guides) guards against legalism.[^92]

**Crisis 3: Dispensational Discontinuity.** Some dispensational systems teach that the moral law was not in force before Sinai or is not binding on the church. The confession's teaching that the same law written on Adam's heart continues after the fall and is summarized in the Ten Commandments refutes this error.[^93]

**Crisis 4: The Neglect of the Law's Uses.** Many believers do not know how to use the law positively. The confession's description of the law as a rule of life, a mirror, and an incentive to holiness provides practical guidance.[^94]

**Crisis 5: The False Dichotomy Between Law and Gospel.** Many Christians pit law against gospel as if they were enemies. The confession's affirmation that they "sweetly comply" with each other restores a biblical balance.[^95]

### Part 4: Practical Return – Living in Light of the Law

**Step 1: Distinguish the Law as a Covenant of Works from the Law as a Rule of Life.** Understand that the law cannot justify you. Your acceptance before God rests on Christ's perfect obedience, not your own. Yet the law remains your guide for holy living.[^96]

**Step 2: Use the Law as a Mirror.** Regularly examine your life in light of the Ten Commandments. Allow the law to convict you of remaining sin, lead you to humility, and drive you back to Christ.[^97]

**Step 3: Use the Law as a Rule of Life.** Let the Ten Commandments inform your daily decisions. Ask: "Does this action honor God? Does it love my neighbor? Does it reflect the character of Christ?"[^98]

**Step 4: Recognize the Harmony of Law and Gospel.** Do not pit law against gospel. The same Spirit who saved you enables you to obey. Obedience is not legalism; it is the fruit of grace.[^99]

**Step 5: Rejoice in the Abrogation of the Ceremonial Law.** Do not bind yourself or others to Old Testament ceremonies. Christ has fulfilled them. Your worship is not in types and shadows but in the reality of Christ Himself.[^100]

**Step 6: Learn from the General Equity of the Judicial Law.** While the civil laws of Israel are not binding as a code, the principles of justice they embody remain instructive for Christian ethics and civil government.[^101]

**Step 7: Preach the Law and the Gospel Faithfully.** Preach the law to drive sinners to Christ. Preach the gospel to save them. Preach the law again as a rule of life for believers. The law and the gospel are not enemies; they are partners in the work of redemption.[^102]

### Conclusion: The Law That Was Never Cancelled

Chapter 19 of the 1689 Baptist Confession is not a museum piece of seventeenth-century theology. It is the believer's guide to the law that was never cancelled. The same moral law that was written on Adam's heart continues to bind all people in all ages, including justified believers. Christ did not dissolve the law; He fulfilled it and strengthened its obligation. Believers are not under the law as a covenant of works, but the law remains a rule of life, a mirror to discover sin, and an incentive to holiness. The law and the gospel are not enemies; they sweetly comply with one another, for the Spirit of Christ enables believers to do freely and cheerfully what the law requires.[^103]

**A Prayer from Chapter 19** (adapted from the Puritan tradition):

*O holy God, whose law is perfect, converting the soul: We confess that we have often despised Thy law, treating it as an enemy rather than a friend. We have stumbled into antinomianism, imagining that grace frees us from obedience. We have also stumbled into legalism, imagining that our obedience could earn Thy favor. Forgive us.*

*We thank Thee for the moral law, written on Adam's heart and summarized in the Ten Commandments. We thank Thee that it continues to be a perfect rule of righteousness after the fall. We thank Thee that believers are not under the law as a covenant of works, yet it remains a rule of life, a mirror to discover sin, and an incentive to holiness. We thank Thee that the law and the gospel are not contrary but sweetly comply, for Thy Spirit enables us to do freely and cheerfully what Thy law requires.*

*Grant us to love Thy law, to meditate on it day and night, and to walk in its paths—not to be justified, but to please Thee, who hast justified us freely by Thy grace. In Christ's name. Amen.*[^104]
